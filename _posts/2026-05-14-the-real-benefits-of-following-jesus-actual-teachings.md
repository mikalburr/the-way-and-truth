The benefits of following Jesus's actual teachings are different from the benefits advertised by much of contemporary Christianity. The advertising tends toward comfort, community, and heaven after death. The actual benefits are deeper, stranger, and more useful in the middle of real life.

This article is honest about what following his teachings actually produces — and what it does not produce.

## Freedom From the Performance Treadmill

The performance treadmill is the condition of living under constant evaluation — internal or external — measuring your worth by your output, your behavior, your spiritual achievement, your comparative standing relative to others.

Most people are on this treadmill without recognizing it. It is the operating system of a culture built on merit, productivity, and measurable value. Churches often reinforce it with spiritual metrics: how often you attend, how much you give, how many books you've read, whether your sin count is improving.

Jesus's teaching interrupts this entirely. The Beatitudes are not achievements to work toward — they are declarations of who already has the Kingdom. Matthew 5:3: *"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."* The poor in spirit — those who know they have nothing to offer — already have the Kingdom. Not because they've worked for it. Because the King gives it to those who know they need it.

John 15:16: *"You did not choose me, but I chose you and appointed you so that you might go and bear fruit."* You were chosen before you performed. The fruitfulness comes from that prior appointment, not from earning the appointment by fruitfulness.

The freedom this produces is not laziness. It is the release from having to earn what you already have — which frees you to act from fullness rather than from the anxiety of scarcity. Generosity becomes natural when you are not trying to accumulate enough to feel secure. Love becomes possible when you are not managing your image.

The performance treadmill is exhausting and never terminates in satisfaction. Jesus's teaching offers the alternative: rest in what the Father has declared, then work from that rest rather than toward it. Matthew 11:28-30: *"Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls."*

## Clarity About What Matters and What Doesn't

One of the most practical benefits of Jesus's teachings is a hierarchy of importance that cuts through the noise of modern life.

Most people operate with an implicit priority structure they have inherited rather than chosen — social status, financial security, career advancement, approval of significant people in their lives. These priorities are never explicitly ranked, so they compete constantly, producing decision paralysis and chronic low-grade anxiety about whether you are prioritizing correctly.

Jesus gave explicit priority structure. Matthew 6:33: *"Seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well."* One thing first. The Kingdom — God's sovereign rule over your life and domain — comes first. Everything else is "all these things" that are provisioned as you live from Kingdom priority.

Matthew 22:37-39 reduces the entire ethical system to two commands: total love of God and total love of neighbor. When you have a decision to make, a conflict to navigate, a priority to choose — these two commands provide the evaluation criteria. Does this express love of God? Does this express love of neighbor? If yes to both, proceed. If not, reconsider.

This clarity is not simplistic — the application of love in complex situations requires wisdom and discernment. But it eliminates the confusion that comes from having no hierarchy at all. You know what the most important thing is. You know what the second most important thing is. Everything else is ordered beneath those two.

The result is not a life with fewer decisions, but a life where the basis for decisions is clear. That clarity alone reduces enormous amounts of anxiety and regret.

## A Framework for Suffering That Doesn't Collapse Under Pressure

Every worldview eventually encounters suffering. The test of a worldview is whether its explanation for suffering holds under the actual weight of real pain — not whether it sounds coherent in a seminar.

The most common modern responses to suffering: it's random chance (secular materialism), it's karma (Eastern frameworks), it's punishment from God (distorted religious framework), it's a test of faith that will end if you believe hard enough (prosperity theology).

None of these hold when the suffering is severe and prolonged.

Jesus's framework: suffering is the expected experience of Kingdom citizens in a world not yet fully aligned with the Kingdom, and the pattern of the cross (death that leads to life) is the template through which all suffering can be read.

John 16:33: *"In this world you will have trouble. But take heart! I have overcome the world."* He does not say: in this world you will have trouble unless you pray hard enough, unless you are faithful enough, unless you deserve exemption. He says you will have trouble. Period. And he says he has overcome the world. Period.

This framework does not eliminate the pain of suffering. It gives suffering a location in a larger story. The disciples who watched Jesus die were not spared the grief — they hid in fear (John 20:19). But the resurrection reorganized that grief into a different framework entirely. The death was not the end; it was the path through.

Romans 8:28 (Paul's expansion of this teaching): *"We know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him."* Not: God exempts the faithful from all things difficult. He works in all things — including the difficult ones — for the good of those aligned with him.

The man who builds his house on the rock does not avoid the storm. The storm comes. The difference is the foundation — the house stands (Matthew 7:24-25). That is the practical benefit: not an absence of storms, but a foundation that holds when they arrive.

## Relationships Built on Something Other Than Utility

Transactional relationships — relationships maintained as long as they are mutually beneficial — are the dominant relational model of modern culture. They are efficient and comfortable when working and brittle when stressed.

Jesus commanded a different relational architecture. John 13:34-35: *"A new command I give you: Love one another. As I have loved you, so you must love one another. By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another."*

The standard is "as I have loved you" — which in context (the night before the crucifixion) means self-giving love that persists through betrayal, denial, abandonment, and death. This is not the love of equal exchange. It is the love that initiates and persists regardless of whether it is matched.

Matthew 5:46-47: *"If you love those who love you, what reward will you get? Are not even the tax collectors doing that? And if you greet only your own people, what are you doing more than others?"*

The relational ethic Jesus teaches is not natural — it runs against the equity instinct that governs most human relationships. But the practical benefit is profound: relationships built on this ethic are much harder to destroy, because their foundation is not mutual benefit but chosen commitment. They survive disagreement, failure, and the inevitable unequal seasons of any long relationship.

Community formed around this ethic is what the early church produced — and it was visible enough to attract outsiders: *"See how they love one another"* (attributed to Tertullian, describing non-Christian observers of early Christian community).

## A Coherent Answer to "What Is My Life For?"

This is the deepest benefit and the one most conspicuously absent from secular culture.

The question of purpose — what am I here for, what gives my life meaning — has no coherent answer within a materialist framework. You are a temporary arrangement of matter that will return to entropy. You may have preferences, but you have no purpose in any meaningful sense. Whatever meaning you construct is invented, not discovered.

Jesus gave a different answer. John 10:10: *"I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full."* The Greek word translated "full" is *perissos* — abundant, exceeding the norm, more than sufficient. Life as Jesus defines it — *zoe aionios*, the eternal life that is present-tense relational knowledge of God — is not just duration. It is fullness.

Matthew 5:16: *"Let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven."* Your life is for God's glory, expressed through your specific good works in your specific place. This is not vague. It is concrete purpose — living in such a way that who the Father is becomes visible through who you are.

The cultural mandate of Genesis 1:28 (fill the earth, subdue it, exercise dominion) and the Kingdom mandate of Matthew 28:19-20 (make disciples of all nations) together provide a comprehensive answer: your life is for the expansion of Kingdom reality in your specific domain, in your specific time, through your specific capacities.

This answer is demanding. It is also inexhaustible. You never complete it, which means you never exhaust it. And unlike achievement-based purpose (accumulate enough, reach high enough), it does not evaporate when the achievement is reached. The horizon is always there.

---

These five benefits are real. They are not easy. The performance treadmill freedom comes after you have genuinely internalized Kingdom identity rather than performance identity — and that process is not quick. The clarity takes extended engagement with Jesus's teaching to become a functional hierarchy. The suffering framework gets tested before it holds.

But they are available. They are available to anyone who genuinely engages Jesus's actual teachings, not the softened version, not the tradition-filtered version, but the red-letter words of the man who said *"I am the way and the truth and the life"* (John 14:6) and meant it as a total claim on every human being.

The benefits are proportional to the engagement. Go deep.
