This article will make some people uncomfortable. That is not the goal, but it is an acceptable cost. The goal is precision — to distinguish between what Jesus of Nazareth actually said and taught, and what the institution of Christianity has layered on top of his words across two thousand years.

This is not an anti-church argument. The church exists because Jesus called it into being (Matthew 16:18), and many of its traditions carry genuine wisdom accumulated across centuries of lived faith. But tradition is not Scripture. The teachings of Jesus are not the same thing as Christian tradition. Conflating them is dishonest, and dishonesty in this domain has consequences.

Jesus himself drew this distinction with force. In Matthew 15:3, responding to Pharisees who challenged his disciples for violating the tradition of the elders, he answered: *"And why do you break the command of God for the sake of your tradition?"* He then quoted Isaiah 29:13 against them: *"These people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me. They worship me in vain; their teachings are merely human rules."*

The test he applied to religious tradition was simple: does it align with what God has commanded, or does it override it? The same test applies to Christian tradition today.

## The Test: Is It in Red Letters?

The simplest heuristic for this inquiry: is it in the Gospels? Did Jesus say it? If not, it may be true, useful, or warranted by theological inference — but it is not a teaching of Jesus. It should be presented as what it is.

Let us apply that test to four of the most widespread practices in Western Christianity.

## Sunday Sabbath

The Jewish Sabbath is Saturday — sundown Friday to sundown Saturday. This is clearly established in Genesis 2:2-3, Exodus 20:8-11, and throughout the Hebrew Bible. Jesus observed the Sabbath (Luke 4:16: *"He went to Nazareth, where he had been brought up, and on the Sabbath day he went into the synagogue, as was his custom"*).

Jesus never taught that the Sabbath should be moved to Sunday. He taught extensively about the *spirit* of Sabbath — pushing back against the Pharisaic hedge of laws that had made Sabbath observance a burden rather than a gift (Matthew 12:1-14, Mark 2:23-3:6, Luke 13:10-17). His famous statement is in Mark 2:27-28: *"The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath. So the Son of Man is Lord even of the Sabbath."*

He is claiming authority over Sabbath, not abolishing it. He is clarifying its purpose, not relocating it.

Sunday worship in the early church emerged from the resurrection — Jesus rose on the first day of the week (Matthew 28:1, Mark 16:2, John 20:1). Early Christian communities began gathering on Sunday to commemorate the resurrection (Acts 20:7, 1 Corinthians 16:2). By the second century this was a widespread practice.

It became the legally mandated day of rest throughout the Roman Empire under Constantine's Edict of 321 AD, which declared Sunday (the day of the sun) the official day of rest.

This is not a teaching of Jesus. It is a post-resurrection development solidified by Roman imperial law. There is nothing wrong with Sunday worship. But calling it "the Sabbath" and treating it as a command of Jesus is historically inaccurate.

## Tithing as Law for Christians

Tithing — giving ten percent of income — is a real Old Testament command (Leviticus 27:30, Deuteronomy 14:22-27, Malachi 3:10). Jesus acknowledged it: *"Woe to you, teachers of the law and Pharisees, you hypocrites! You give a tenth of your spices — mint, dill and cumin. But you have neglected the more important matters of the law — justice, mercy and faithfulness. You should have practiced the latter, without neglecting the former"* (Matthew 23:23).

Note carefully: Jesus affirmed the tithe as part of the law (*"you should have practiced the latter, without neglecting the former"*). He critiqued using tithing as a substitute for justice, mercy, and faithfulness — not as a replacement for tithing.

But he never commanded his disciples to tithe. The New Testament gives a different framework for generosity entirely. Paul's instruction in 2 Corinthians 9:7 — *"Each of you should give what you have decided in your heart to give, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver"* — is a significant departure from a fixed percentage mandate.

The practice of teaching tithing as a New Covenant requirement for Christians, with Malachi 3:10 as its proof text (*"Bring the whole tithe into the storehouse"*), is theologically problematic. Malachi was written to Israel under the Mosaic Covenant. The storehouse was the Temple treasury. Applying it as a law binding on Gentile believers in the church era requires significant hermeneutical assumptions that are never made explicit.

Jesus taught radical generosity — sell your possessions and give to the poor (Luke 12:33), give to everyone who asks (Luke 6:30), the widow who gave everything was praised above those who gave large amounts from their surplus (Mark 12:41-44). His standard for generosity is not ten percent. It is "everything you have" as a posture of Kingdom citizenship.

Teaching a ten-percent floor is not wrong. Calling it a command of Jesus is.

## The Altar Call

The altar call — the practice of inviting people to come forward at the end of a service to publicly commit to Jesus — was formalized by Charles Finney in the 1820s and popularized by D.L. Moody and Billy Graham in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

It is not found in the Gospels. Jesus did not hold altar calls.

His invitations were direct, personal, and specific to each individual. He said to Simon and Andrew: *"Come, follow me"* (Mark 1:17). He said to Matthew the tax collector: *"Follow me"* (Matthew 9:9). He told the rich young ruler to sell everything and follow him (Mark 10:21). He said to Nicodemus you must be born again (John 3:7). He told the Samaritan woman about living water (John 4:10-14).

In no instance did he invite a crowd to come forward to a specific location to make a public decision. His invitations were costly, specific, and followed up.

The altar call is not evil. Evangelistic invitation has its place. But when it is presented as the biblical model of conversion, or when a public walk to the front is treated as equivalent to the kind of genuine repentance and discipleship Jesus called for, the tradition has replaced the teaching.

## The Sinner's Prayer

The "sinner's prayer" — a formulaic prayer of confession and acceptance of Jesus — does not appear in the Gospels. Jesus never instructed anyone to pray a specific prayer to be saved.

What Jesus called for was *metanoia* — a complete reorientation of mind, allegiance, and life direction. *"Repent and believe the good news"* (Mark 1:15). The Greek *pisteuo* translated "believe" carries the weight of trust, allegiance, and commitment — not intellectual assent.

He called for a response that reorganized the whole life. *"If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me"* (Matthew 16:24). *"No one who puts his hand to the plow and looks back is fit for the kingdom of God"* (Luke 9:62).

The sinner's prayer emerged as an evangelistic tool to help people cross a decision threshold, formalized in its current common form in the twentieth century. Billy Sunday used similar formulas in the early 1900s. It serves a real purpose in helping people articulate a response to the gospel.

But treating it as the mechanism of salvation — *say these words and you're in* — is a transactional model that Jesus never taught and that arguably distorts the totality of what he called people to.

Matthew 7:21-23 is the most uncomfortable passage in this context: *"Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but only the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven. Many will say to me on that day, 'Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name and in your name drive out demons and in your name perform many miracles?' Then I will tell them plainly, 'I never knew you. Away from me, you evildoers!'"*

The Greek word for "knew" is *ginosko* — deep, relational knowledge. Not the performance of religious acts. Not the correct utterance of a prayer. A living relationship.

## The Honest Conclusion

None of these traditions are categorically evil. Some are genuinely useful. But every one of them — Sunday Sabbath, tithing as law, altar calls, the sinner's prayer — was invented by someone after Jesus. They are human additions to the teaching of Jesus, which means they must be held at arm's length from the teaching itself.

Jesus's warning applies here as much as it applied to the Pharisees: *"You have a fine way of setting aside the commands of God in order to observe your own traditions!"* (Mark 7:9).

The command is to follow Jesus. Read his words. Do what he said. The tradition serves you when it helps you do that. When it substitutes for that, it has become the problem Jesus warned about.

Be honest about which is which.
