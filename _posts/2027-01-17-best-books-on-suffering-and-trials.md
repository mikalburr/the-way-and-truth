Suffering is the question every man eventually has to answer. The answer he had before the suffering started will almost certainly not survive contact with it. Most Christian men discover, when the loss or the diagnosis or the betrayal arrives, that their working theology of suffering was sentimental and theoretical — not load-bearing.

These five books exist for that moment. They are not designed to make suffering pleasant. They are designed to give you a theological framework, a real-world witness, and a path forward that holds when you cannot.

I have read all five. Several I have returned to multiple times. Here they are.

---

## 1. Walking with God Through Pain and Suffering — Timothy Keller

{% include book-card.html
  title="Walking with God Through Pain and Suffering"
  author="Timothy Keller"
  asin="1594634408"
  description="Keller's comprehensive theological and practical treatment of suffering — drawing on Job, the Psalms, the cross, the resurrection, and the wisdom of multiple Christian traditions to provide a framework that holds under real pressure."
  verdict="The best comprehensive book on suffering in print. Start here. It is theological, pastoral, and substantive enough to serve as your reference for the rest of your life."
%}

**What it covers:** Keller develops a sustained treatment of suffering across three major sections. First, he surveys how different worldviews and Christian traditions have understood suffering — and shows why most modern accounts fail. Second, he develops a biblical theology of suffering rooted in the sovereignty of God, the gospel, and the resurrection hope. Third, he provides practical instruction for walking through suffering — including extensive engagement with the Psalms as the church's suffering vocabulary.

**Who it's for:** Men who want one comprehensive book on suffering that they can return to for decades. Keller is unusually good at synthesis — he draws on Augustine, Calvin, the Puritans, modern theologians, and his own pastoral experience without losing depth in any direction.

**What it adds:** Breadth and pastoral wisdom. The other books on this list are deep in one direction. Keller covers everything — the philosophical problem, the biblical theology, the practical walk, the role of the church, the work of prayer in suffering, and the place of lament. If you can only own one book on suffering, this is it.

**One honest note:** Keller wrote this book at the suggestion of his wife Kathy after he was diagnosed with cancer (he died of pancreatic cancer in 2023). The book is not abstract for him. The pastoral tone reflects a man who had been walking with sufferers for decades and was about to walk through suffering himself. Read it slowly.

## 2. A Grief Observed — C.S. Lewis

{% include book-card.html
  title="A Grief Observed"
  author="C.S. Lewis"
  asin="0060652381"
  description="Lewis's raw journal of the months following his wife Joy's death from cancer. Originally published under a pseudonym because Lewis was not sure he wanted his Christian reputation associated with such honest doubt."
  verdict="The single most honest account of Christian grief ever written. If you are in the middle of acute loss right now, read this one first."
%}

**What it covers:** Lewis kept journals in the months after his wife Joy Davidman died of cancer in 1960. The book is those journals — lightly edited but largely raw — tracking his actual psychological and spiritual experience of grief. The early sections are wracked with doubt: "Where is God? This is one of the most disquieting symptoms." The later sections track his slow re-emergence into faith, but the path is not direct.

**Who it's for:** Men in the middle of acute grief. The book is short, honest, and pastorally invaluable precisely because Lewis does not pretend to have arrived at peace. He is in the middle of the wreckage and he is reporting from there.

**What it adds:** Permission. Many Christian books on grief assume that real faith should produce serene acceptance. Lewis demonstrates that faith and devastation can coexist — that the honesty of grief is not a betrayal of Christianity but part of Christianity's actual record of how people walk through loss.

**One honest note:** This is not a book to read for theological framework. It is a book to read for company. If you are not currently in acute grief, read it briefly and shelve it. When grief comes — and it will — pull it back down.

## 3. The Problem of Pain — C.S. Lewis

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Problem of Pain"
  author="C.S. Lewis"
  asin="0060652969"
  description="Lewis's careful intellectual treatment of the philosophical problem of suffering — why a good and omnipotent God would permit pain. Written before Lewis experienced personal grief, and substantively different from A Grief Observed."
  verdict="The best short philosophical treatment of the problem of pain. Read it for the mind, not for the wound. Lewis's later A Grief Observed handles the wound."
%}

**What it covers:** Lewis addresses the classic problem of evil — if God is good and all-powerful, why does suffering exist? — across a series of chapters covering divine goodness, human wickedness, the fall, human pain, animal pain, hell, and heaven. The treatment is intellectual, careful, and rooted in classical Christian theology rather than personal experience.

**Who it's for:** Men whose doubts about Christianity are anchored in the problem of suffering and want to engage that problem with intellectual seriousness. Lewis is a careful philosophical writer and he takes the objection seriously before answering it.

**What it adds:** Intellectual ballast. Most personal-experience books on suffering rely on a theological framework that the author assumes rather than defends. Lewis defends the framework. After reading him, you have actual arguments for why suffering does not falsify Christianity — arguments that hold under pressure.

**One honest note:** Lewis later said, in *A Grief Observed*, that his earlier arguments about pain "now seemed flimsy." That is not an indictment of *The Problem of Pain* — it is a recognition that intellectual argument and personal grief are different categories. Both books are necessary. They do different work.

## 4. When God Weeps — Joni Eareckson Tada and Steven Estes

{% include book-card.html
  title="When God Weeps: Why Our Sufferings Matter to the Almighty"
  author="Joni Eareckson Tada and Steven Estes"
  asin="0310240425"
  description="Joni Eareckson Tada was paralyzed in a diving accident at 17 and has lived as a quadriplegic since 1967. With theologian Steven Estes, she develops a careful biblical theology of suffering rooted in her own decades of chronic affliction."
  verdict="The best book on sustained chronic suffering. Tada has lived inside the problem longer than most Christian writers and her witness carries weight that pure scholarship cannot."
%}

**What it covers:** The book interweaves Tada's personal experience of quadriplegia with Estes's theological framework on the sovereignty of God in suffering. The treatment is unflinching — Tada describes her actual physical and psychological experience without minimizing it — and theologically rigorous. The combination of personal witness and careful theology is rare and powerful.

**Who it's for:** Men facing sustained chronic suffering — disability, chronic illness, long-term caregiving — where the affliction is not going to lift and the question is how to live faithfully inside it for decades. Tada has done exactly that for over fifty years.

**What it adds:** Witness from inside long-term affliction. Most books on suffering are written by people whose suffering eventually ended or who write about other people's suffering. Tada writes from inside an affliction that has not ended in over half a century. The credibility is unmatched.

**One honest note:** The book engages strongly with the doctrine of God's sovereignty in suffering — including the difficult question of how God can be sovereign over evil without being morally implicated in it. Estes does not shy from the hard questions. Some readers will find the theological position bracing; others will find it harsh. Engage the argument carefully.

## 5. Don't Waste Your Cancer — John Piper

{% include book-card.html
  title="Don't Waste Your Cancer"
  author="John Piper and David Powlison"
  asin="1433502135"
  description="A short, direct booklet written by John Piper on the eve of his prostate cancer surgery, expanded with David Powlison's reflections after his own cancer diagnosis. Ten ways to ensure that suffering is not wasted spiritually."
  verdict="The shortest book on this list and one of the most concentrated. Read it in 30 minutes. Return to it whenever you are tempted to waste your own suffering."
%}

**What it covers:** Piper wrote the original short piece in 2006 on the eve of his cancer surgery, as a personal meditation on how to not let the suffering become spiritually wasted. The piece is structured as ten warnings: do not waste your cancer by believing it is not designed for you by God, by believing it is a curse and not a gift, by seeking comfort from your odds rather than from God, and so on. David Powlison added reflections after his own cancer diagnosis years later.

**Who it's for:** Men facing a specific named affliction — cancer, but the same framework applies to any sustained suffering — who want a concentrated theological recalibration of how to walk through it. The piece can be read in 30 minutes and reread regularly.

**What it adds:** Concentration. Where Keller and Tada give you comprehensive treatments, Piper gives you a focused, almost surgical, intervention. Ten warnings. Ten ways suffering can be wasted. Read it as a checklist for your own affliction.

**One honest note:** The piece is short — closer to a long essay than a book. The print edition is brief and inexpensive; the same content is also freely available from Desiring God in PDF form. Buy the print version if you want a physical copy to mark up and return to.

## How to Use This List

The order depends entirely on where you are.

**If you are in acute grief right now:** Lewis, *A Grief Observed*. Read nothing else first. Return to the others when the acute phase passes.

**If you are intellectually wrestling with the problem of pain:** Lewis, *The Problem of Pain*, then Keller.

**If you are walking through sustained chronic affliction:** Tada and Estes, *When God Weeps*. Then Keller.

**If you have just received a specific diagnosis:** Piper, *Don't Waste Your Cancer*. Then Tada.

**If you are not currently in suffering and want to build the framework before you need it:** Keller, then Lewis (*The Problem of Pain*). Build the foundation when you are not in crisis. Suffering will come, and the framework you built in calm seas will be the framework that holds you in the storm.

A man with a serious theology of suffering does not avoid pain. He does not minimize it. He walks through it with a framework that holds and a hope that does not collapse. Build that framework now. You will need it.
