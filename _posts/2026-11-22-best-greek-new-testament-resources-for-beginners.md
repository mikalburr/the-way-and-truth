Reading the New Testament in Greek is not for academics only. It is the most directly available way to encounter the actual words the apostles wrote, in the order they wrote them, with the precision they wrote them with. Every English translation is an interpretive decision. Greek is the source.

The barrier is not intelligence. It is the right resources and the discipline to use them daily for about eighteen months. After that, you can read the simpler New Testament books — 1 John, John's Gospel, large portions of the Synoptics — in their original language.

These five resources are the ones I recommend to anyone starting Greek. Here they are, with honest assessments of what each one delivers.

---

## 1. Basics of Biblical Greek Grammar — William D. Mounce

{% include book-card.html
  title="Basics of Biblical Greek Grammar (Fourth Edition)"
  author="William D. Mounce"
  asin="0310537029"
  description="The standard introductory Greek textbook in English-language seminaries. 36 chapters, paced for adult learners, with extensive support materials including videos, workbook, vocabulary cards, and online resources."
  verdict="Start here. The textbook that has trained more pastors and serious laymen in Greek than any other resource in publication."
%}

**What it covers:** Mounce introduces the full grammar of New Testament Greek across 36 chapters, organized by grammatical category. The verb system — which is the heart of Greek and the hardest part for English speakers — is introduced incrementally and reinforced extensively. Every chapter includes vocabulary, practice exercises, and translation practice from the actual Greek New Testament.

**Who it's for:** Adults learning Greek for serious Bible study. Mounce's pace and explanations assume a motivated adult learner, not a college student in a classroom. Self-study works with this textbook if you commit to daily work.

**What it adds:** A complete learning ecosystem. The textbook is supported by a workbook, vocabulary cards, video lectures (available free at billmounce.com), and a teacher's manual. No other Greek resource has this level of supplementary support.

**One honest note:** Mounce is not the only good Greek textbook. Some prefer Black's *Learn to Read New Testament Greek* or Croy's *A Primer of Biblical Greek*. Mounce is the most widely used and the best supported. If you are uncertain, start here — and if it does not click, switch.

## 2. New Testament Greek for Beginners — J. Gresham Machen

{% include book-card.html
  title="New Testament Greek for Beginners (Second Edition)"
  author="J. Gresham Machen"
  asin="083087055X"
  description="The classic Greek textbook by the founder of Westminster Theological Seminary. More demanding than Mounce, but produces unusually thorough grammatical foundations in students who finish it."
  verdict="The rigorous classical alternative to Mounce. Choose Machen if you want a more demanding pace and a deeper grammatical foundation."
%}

**What it covers:** Machen develops Greek grammar across 33 lessons with a more compressed pace and a more grammatically rigorous approach than Mounce. The original 1923 edition has been updated by Dan McCartney, but the core pedagogical approach is preserved. Lessons emphasize translation in both directions — Greek to English and English to Greek — which produces unusually strong active command of the grammar.

**Who it's for:** Self-disciplined learners who want the most thorough grammatical foundation available and are willing to work at a more demanding pace. Machen is the textbook used at Westminster Seminary and similar institutions where students are expected to do substantial original-language work.

**What it adds:** Depth. Mounce gets you reading sooner. Machen produces stronger grammatical mastery in students who complete it. Both are sound; the choice depends on your goals.

**One honest note:** Machen lacks the video lectures, vocabulary apps, and online community that surround Mounce. If you are self-taught and need external support, Mounce is the better choice. If you have access to a teacher or a study group, Machen rewards the additional effort.

## 3. Greek for Everyone — A. Chadwick Thornhill

{% include book-card.html
  title="Greek for Everyone: Introductory Greek for Bible Study and Application"
  author="A. Chadwick Thornhill"
  asin="0801019060"
  description="A non-traditional introduction to Greek aimed at readers who want to engage Greek for Bible study without committing to a full year of grammar study. Focuses on key terms, structures, and translation evaluation rather than mastery of paradigms."
  verdict="The best 'gateway' resource for men who are not sure they want to commit to Mounce's 36-chapter curriculum. Start here if you want to test whether Greek study is for you."
%}

**What it covers:** Thornhill introduces Greek vocabulary, basic grammar, and the use of common reference tools (lexicons, interlinears, original-language Bible software) in a manner aimed at lay Bible study rather than full reading proficiency. The book covers enough Greek to understand commentary references, evaluate translation choices, and use original-language tools effectively.

**Who it's for:** Men who want to engage Greek tools and commentaries seriously but are not committing to becoming fluent readers. Many pastors, teachers, and serious laymen operate at this level — capable enough to use the language meaningfully without aspiring to read the New Testament in Greek cover to cover.

**What it adds:** A defined intermediate level. Most Greek resources assume you are either entirely outside the language or training to read it fluently. Thornhill defines a useful middle path and provides the tools to operate there competently.

**One honest note:** This book will not produce reading proficiency. If your goal is to read the Greek New Testament directly, start with Mounce or Machen and use Thornhill as a supplement. If your goal is to use Greek tools well for Bible study, Thornhill alone may be enough.

## 4. The Greek New Testament: Reader's Edition (ESV English-Greek)

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Greek New Testament Reader's Edition (ESV English-Greek)"
  author="Crossway"
  asin="1433552159"
  description="The complete Greek New Testament with vocabulary glosses at the bottom of every page for words occurring 30 times or fewer in the NT. Includes the ESV English text in a parallel volume layout."
  verdict="The reader's edition that gets new Greek readers actually reading. The vocabulary glosses eliminate the constant dictionary lookups that kill momentum."
%}

**What it covers:** The full Greek New Testament with bottom-of-page vocabulary glosses for low-frequency words. Once you have learned approximately 320 of the most common Greek words (which Mounce or Machen will teach you in the first year of study), the reader's edition allows you to read continuous Greek text without constantly looking up vocabulary.

**Who it's for:** Greek students transitioning from textbook exercises to reading the actual New Testament. The first 6-12 months of Greek study are heavily focused on grammar and vocabulary. The reader's edition is the bridge from learning Greek to using Greek.

**What it adds:** Reading momentum. Without a reader's edition, new Greek readers spend 80% of their reading time looking up vocabulary, which kills both speed and comprehension. The reader's edition restores reading flow.

**One honest note:** A reader's edition is a transitional tool. As your vocabulary expands, you will outgrow it. Most serious students eventually graduate to the unaided Nestle-Aland or UBS Greek New Testament. Use the reader's edition as long as it serves you, then move on.

## 5. Mounce Reverse-Interlinear New Testament (digital, via Logos Bible Software)

  author="William D. Mounce"
  asin="0310539110"
  logos_url="https://www.logos.com/product/4604/mounce-reverse-interlinear-new-testament"
  description="An English-text-primary interlinear that maps each English word to its underlying Greek term, with full lexical data and morphological tagging. Available in print and as the foundational interlinear in Logos Bible Software."
  verdict="The best reference tool for Greek students who want instant access to underlying Greek data while reading English. Especially powerful in Logos where everything is hyperlinked."
%}

**What it covers:** The reverse-interlinear format presents the English text in normal reading order with the underlying Greek word listed below each English word, along with grammatical tagging (case, number, tense, mood, voice, etc.) and lemma form. This allows readers to see what Greek word stands behind any English term they encounter, without requiring them to read continuous Greek.

**Who it's for:** Greek students who want a study aid that connects their growing Greek knowledge to their English reading. Also valuable for pastors and teachers who want to evaluate specific translation choices without doing full Greek study.

**What it adds:** Integration. Reading Greek in continuous form (Reader's Edition) and using interlinear data are complementary practices, not competing ones. The reverse-interlinear is the most efficient way to bring Greek data into your normal English Bible reading.

**One honest note:** Interlinears are tools, not destinations. Over-reliance on an interlinear can prevent the deeper learning that produces real Greek competence. Use it as a study aid alongside continuous Greek reading, not as a replacement for learning the language.

## How to Build Your Greek Library

The path that actually produces Greek readers:

**Months 1-12:** Mounce's *Basics of Biblical Greek Grammar* with workbook and video lectures. Daily, 30-60 minutes. Complete all 36 chapters and the exercises.

**Months 13-18:** Greek New Testament Reader's Edition. Read 1 John first (simplest Greek in the NT), then John's Gospel, then the Synoptics. Daily, 20-30 minutes.

**Ongoing reference:** Mounce Reverse-Interlinear in Logos. Use it daily for English Bible reading to keep building familiarity with the underlying Greek.

**Alternative starting points:** Machen if you want a more rigorous textbook. Thornhill if you want a lower-commitment gateway resource.

**Be realistic:** Most adults who commit to daily Greek study reach basic reading proficiency in 12-18 months. The men who finish are not the smartest; they are the ones who showed up every day. The first six months are the hardest. Push through them.
