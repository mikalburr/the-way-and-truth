"Whatever you do, work heartily, as for the Lord and not for men, knowing that from the Lord you will receive the inheritance as your reward. You are serving the Lord Christ." — Colossians 3:23-24

Paul wrote this to slaves. Not to executives. Not to entrepreneurs. To men whose work was difficult, often degrading, and not chosen. And to those men, he said: do the work for the Lord. The Lord is the one you are actually serving. The Lord is the one who will reward you.

The implication is staggering. If a slave's work can be Kingdom work, no man's work is exempt. The IT manager, the framer, the surgeon, the truck driver, the entrepreneur, the teacher, the unemployed man between jobs — all of them are serving Christ in their work, or they are not. The work itself is not the variable. The orientation is.

This means two things at once. Your job matters more than you think — and your calling is bigger than your job. Both of these are true, and confusing them is how most men get the vocational question wrong.

## Work in Scripture

Scripture treats work as fundamental to human identity. Not as punishment. Not as necessary evil. As part of how we image God.

**Before the fall.** Genesis 2:15 — God put the man in the garden "to work it and keep it." Work precedes sin. Work is part of what it means to be made in God's image. The God who labored for six days made men who labor.

**After the fall.** Genesis 3:17-19 — work is now under the curse. Thorns. Sweat. The ground resists the man's effort. Work is still good, but it is now hard. This is the world we work in.

**Redemption of work.** The New Testament reorients work without abolishing it. Colossians 3:23. Ephesians 6:7. 1 Thessalonians 4:11 — "aspire to live quietly, and to mind your own affairs, and to work with your hands, as we instructed you." The Christian works — not despite the gospel, but as part of it.

**Eternal work.** Revelation 22:3 says that in the new creation, "his servants will worship him." The word is *latreuō* — serve, render service. Work is part of the eternal state. We will not retire. We will work, in a redeemed mode, forever.

This frames the vocational question. Work is not optional. It is also not the destination. It is a primary arena in which the Christian man lives out his Kingdom citizenship.

## Job, Career, Calling

The three concepts are related but distinct.

**Job.** The specific position you currently hold. The thing you do for compensation right now. It may or may not be what you want to be doing. It is what you are doing.

**Career.** The longer trajectory across multiple jobs. The arc of vocational development. The accumulation of skills, reputation, and responsibility over time.

**Calling.** The deeper assignment God has placed on your life. The work you were made for. The place where your wiring, your gifts, your formation, and the Kingdom's needs intersect.

For some men, the three align — the current job is part of the career that is moving toward the calling. For most men, there is some distance. The current job pays the bills but is not the calling. The career is heading one direction; the calling whispers another.

The Christian man holds all three honestly. He does the current job faithfully — because Colossians 3:23 still applies, regardless of whether the job is the dream. He builds the career strategically — because stewardship requires forward planning. And he listens for the calling — because God may be sending him somewhere the current job does not yet reach.

## Faithfulness Over Ambition

The dominant cultural narrative says: chase your dream. Find your passion. Maximize your potential. If you are not loving every day of work, you are in the wrong job.

The biblical narrative is different. Faithfulness in the current assignment outweighs ambition for the next one.

The parable of the talents in Matthew 25 makes this concrete. The master commends the servants who were "faithful over a little" by promoting them to "much." The promotion was not a function of ambition. It was a function of faithfulness in the current assignment.

This reframes the vocational anxiety most men carry. The question is not primarily "what should I be doing instead?" The question is "am I being faithful where I am?" The first question often does not have an answer. The second one always does.

Faithfulness includes:

- Showing up on time.
- Doing the work to the standard of someone who is serving Christ, not the standard of someone who is gaming a performance review.
- Treating coworkers with the dignity that comes from their being image-bearers.
- Refusing to participate in dishonesty, gossip, or shortcuts.
- Bringing real skill, sharpened over time, to whatever the work requires.
- Being a man others can trust with weight.

A man who is doing these things is being faithful, regardless of whether the job is his dream. And the promotion — to greater scope, deeper calling, more aligned work — usually comes through the faithfulness, not around it.

## The Long Game of Calling

Calling clarifies over time. Most men do not have a Damascus-road moment that hands them their vocation. They have a series of small clarifications across decades.

The pattern looks like this:

- In the twenties, the man tries things. He gets exposure. He builds skills. He learns what he is good at and what he is not.
- In the thirties, the wiring becomes clearer. Certain problems pull him in. Certain people he is drawn to serve. Certain work lights him up while other work drains him.
- In the forties, the calling sharpens. He sees the convergence of his gifts, his experience, his relationships, and the work God seems to keep putting in front of him.
- In the fifties and beyond, the calling is operational. He is doing the work he was made for, often in a more focused way than he could have done in his thirties.

This is not a guaranteed timeline. Some men know their calling at 22. Some discover it at 60. But the pattern of clarification over time is more common than the pattern of immediate clarity.

The mistake is forcing premature certainty. The man who feels he must have his calling figured out by 30, and who panics when it has not clarified, often makes worse decisions than the man who is content to be faithful while the calling unfolds.

## Work as Worship

Colossians 3:23 elevates work to worship. "Work heartily, as for the Lord." The work itself becomes an act of service to Christ.

This is true regardless of the prestige of the job. The Christian custodian who is cleaning floors as service to Christ is doing more Kingdom work than the Christian CEO who is running his company for himself. The orientation determines the spiritual weight, not the job description.

Practically, this means:

- The quality of your work is a witness. People who interact with your work are seeing something about the God you serve.
- The integrity of your conduct at work is a witness. The man who does not cut corners, who tells the truth, who treats people well, is preaching without a pulpit.
- The faithfulness of your daily showing up — through good seasons and hard ones — is part of how the Kingdom advances in the world.

The man who treats his work as worship for 40 years has done significant Kingdom work even if he never preached a sermon, started a ministry, or held a church office. He has been a missionary in a place most missionaries do not go — the ordinary daily marketplace where most of life happens.

## Where to Land

If you are in a job that does not feel like your calling, do this: be faithful where you are. Sharpen the skills. Show up well. Listen for the next assignment. Do not panic about the gap between job and calling.

If you sense a calling that does not match your current job, do this: keep doing the current job faithfully while exploring the calling on the side. Build the skills the calling requires. Make the connections. Let the calling develop alongside the work, until the timing clarifies for transition.

If you are not sure of your calling, do this: keep walking with God, keep doing the work in front of you, keep paying attention. The calling will clarify over time, through faithfulness, not through anxiety.

This is the [warrior mindset](/2026/05/12/christian-warrior-mindset/) applied to vocation. The warrior does not abandon his post because the post is not glamorous. He holds the ground assigned to him. He does the work in front of him. And when the orders come for a different assignment, he is ready — because he has been faithful in this one.

Work heartily. Work for the Lord. Trust him with the timing of the rest.
