Genesis 1:28 is one of the most consequential verses in the Bible. It is also one of the most misread — sometimes weaponized to justify exploitation of the natural world, sometimes stripped of meaning by those overcorrecting against that abuse. Both errors lose the actual command. Let us read what it says.

> "And God blessed them. And God said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over every living thing that moves on the earth.'" — Genesis 1:28 (BSB)

Five imperatives: be fruitful, multiply, fill the earth, subdue it, have dominion. This is not a suggestion or a cultural observation. This is a command. It is the first thing God says to human beings after creating them. The first instruction defines purpose. God's first word to humanity is a mandate — a royal assignment.

## The Context: Who Is Being Addressed

To understand the command, you must first understand who receives it. Verse 27 precedes it: "So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them."

The dominion mandate is given to image-bearers. The Hebrew is *tselem Elohim* — the image of God. In the ancient Near East, kings placed their images — statues — throughout their territories to represent their authority and presence. The image of the king signified the king's domain. Where the image stood, the king's rule extended.

God does not place a statue in his creation. He places people. Human beings are the living image-bearers of God — his representatives in the created order, appointed to govern it in his name and according to his character. This is not a small claim. Every human being carries the royal insignia of the Creator.

This radically reframes the dominion mandate. You are not given authority over creation because you are powerful enough to take it. You are given authority as a delegated stewardship from the One who actually owns it. The earth is the Lord's (Psalm 24:1). You govern it on his behalf. This is the difference between a king who owns his kingdom and a governor who administers it under the king's law.

## What "Subdue" and "Dominion" Actually Mean

The two most charged words in the verse are *radah* (dominion) and *kabash* (subdue). Critics of this verse have argued that these terms license exploitation — that the ecological devastation of industrial civilization can be traced to this mandate. Defenders have sometimes accepted that framing and tried to soften the words. Neither approach reads the Hebrew carefully.

*Radah* — translated "have dominion" or "rule" — is the word used for royal governance. It appears in Psalm 72:8 describing the king ruling "from sea to sea," and in 1 Kings 4:24 describing Solomon's dominion over surrounding kingdoms. This is the language of responsible governance: maintaining order, ensuring flourishing, providing security, administering justice. A king who destroys his own kingdom is not exercising *radah* — he is betraying it.

*Kabash* — "subdue" — is stronger. It can mean to press down, to bring under control. It acknowledges that creation has elements that resist order — wilderness, chaos, entropy. The mandate to subdue is the mandate to bring structured order to what is disordered. This is what farmers do to wild land. What engineers do to raw material. What teachers do to ignorance. What leaders do to chaos. The exercise of dominion is the creative imposition of God-like order on a world that tends toward disorder.

This is why human creativity and culture are theological acts. When you build something, order something, cultivate something, govern something — you are exercising the image of God in the activity for which you were designed. Work is not a consequence of the fall. Work is the pre-fall assignment.

## The Imago Dei and Human Authority

The image of God (*imago Dei*) has been debated by theologians for centuries. What exactly does it mean to be made in God's image? The most defensible answer draws together three dimensions:

**Functional:** Humans function as God's representatives in creation. This is the most clear from the context — the image is given and immediately followed by the dominion mandate. The image explains the authority. You rule because you represent.

**Relational:** Humans are created for relationship — with God, with each other, with creation. "Male and female he created them" (v.27) — the image is expressed in community, not in isolation. No man fully embodies the image of God alone.

**Moral/Rational:** Humans reflect God's rationality, moral capacity, and creativity. The capacity to name (Genesis 2:19-20), to build, to reason, to choose — these are image-of-God capacities.

The dominion mandate, then, is not the authority of a brute over weaker creatures. It is the authority of a rational, relational, morally responsible representative of the Creator, entrusted with the governance of a world that belongs to God.

## How the Fall Distorted Dominion

Genesis 3 records the catastrophe. The man and woman who were appointed to govern creation in obedience to the Creator chose to operate outside that relationship — to "be like God" (v.5) on their own terms. The irony is devastating: they already bore the image of God. The serpent's temptation was to abandon what they had in pursuit of what they already were.

The consequences restructure everything. The ground that was designed to yield to human governance now yields thorns and resistance (v.17-18). The authority relationship between man and woman, designed for partnership, becomes distorted into domination (v.16). The fruitfulness and multiplication designed to fill the earth with image-bearers becomes a process marked by pain (v.16). Death enters a system designed for life.

Dominion does not disappear — humans still shape their world, still build, still govern. But fallen dominion is corrupted dominion: exploitative rather than stewardly, self-serving rather than God-representing, short-sighted rather than generative. The ecological destruction, the abuse of power, the extraction of resources without regard for the future — these are not the dominion mandate. These are what happens when the dominion mandate is exercised by image-bearers who have forgotten whose image they bear.

## How Christ Restores It

The New Testament presents Jesus as the Second Adam — the one who does what the first Adam failed to do. Paul explicitly develops this typology in Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15. Jesus lived the human life in full obedience to the Father, exercised authority over creation (calming storms, walking on water, commanding demons, raising the dead), and then through his death and resurrection defeated sin and death — the two powers that corrupted dominion in the first place.

The risen Christ declares: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me." (Matthew 28:18) This is the language of the dominion mandate restored in the Second Adam. And what does he do with that authority? He commissions his people — his image-bearers — to "make disciples of all nations" (v.19). The Great Commission is the dominion mandate in its Kingdom form: filling the earth not just with human beings but with Kingdom citizens who govern according to the King's character.

The new creation is the dominion mandate fulfilled. Revelation 22 shows the renewed earth, the throne of God, and his servants *serving him* and *reigning forever and ever* (v.5). Dominion was never cancelled. It was purified.

## What This Means for Your Life Today

The dominion mandate is not abstract theology. It has direct, practical implications for a man serious about the Kingdom.

**Your body.** You are a steward of your physical life — the one domain you have been given most directly. Exercising the body, disciplining appetite, sleeping, building strength — these are not vanity. They are stewardship of the instrument through which you govern your sphere.

**Your family.** The most immediate territory any man is given is his household. Leading your family well — spiritually, financially, relationally — is the primary exercise of the dominion mandate for most men. A man who neglects his household to pursue influence elsewhere has abandoned his first territory.

**Your work.** All legitimate work is an exercise of the dominion mandate — bringing order, producing value, creating things that did not exist before, solving problems. The man who does his work with excellence and integrity is doing theology. He is acting as an image-bearer.

**Your sphere of influence.** Beyond the household, every man has a sphere — a network of relationships, a community, a field of expertise, a platform. That sphere is territory. The question is whether you are exercising godly dominion in it — bringing truth, order, justice, and flourishing — or whether you are letting it be shaped by something else.

**Creation.** The original mandate has not been rescinded. Concern for the natural world, responsible use of resources, refusal to exploit for short-term gain — these are not political positions. They are expressions of the dominion mandate rightly understood.

The dominion mandate is your calling. Not because you are powerful or important, but because you bear the image of a God who cares about his world and has appointed representatives to govern it well. That appointment has not been revoked. The question is whether you take it seriously.
