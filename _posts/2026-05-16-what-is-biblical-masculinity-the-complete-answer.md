The conversation about masculinity is broken from both directions. On one side: a definition of manhood built on dominance, stoicism, and emotional suppression — a performance of toughness that never asks whether the toughness is actually strong or just defended. On the other side: a rejection of any distinct definition of manhood at all, as if the category is itself the problem.

Neither reads the text. The Bible has a great deal to say about what men are and what men are for — and what it says does not fit neatly into either cultural box.

## Starting with the Image of God

The foundation of biblical masculinity is not culture, tradition, or biology alone. It is theology.

Genesis 1:27: "So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them."

The image of God — *tselem Elohim* — is expressed in the human being as gendered. Male and female together bear the image. This means two things simultaneously: (1) neither sex alone exhausts the image of God, and (2) each sex expresses that image distinctively. Masculinity is not a cultural accident. It is a created reality through which one dimension of the image of God is expressed in the world.

What does this mean for a man? It means that your masculinity is not a problem to be managed or a liability to be neutralized. It is the form through which you bear the image of God — the form through which you exercise dominion, relate to God, serve others, and reflect the Creator's character. The question is not whether to be masculine but what kind of masculine you are going to be.

## What Jesus Shows Us

If you want to know what the fullest expression of masculinity looks like, you look at Jesus — the Second Adam, the man who lived the human life in complete fidelity to the image of God without distortion or compromise. What do you see?

**Decisive authority.** Jesus did not ask for permission. He commanded the weather and it obeyed (Mark 4:39). He entered the Temple and drove out the money changers with a handmade whip (John 2:15). He called twelve men to follow him with two words and they left their livelihoods immediately (Mark 1:17-20). Jesus was not passive, not deferential, not waiting to see how situations resolved themselves. He acted with the authority he carried.

**Genuine tenderness.** The same Jesus wept at the tomb of Lazarus (John 11:35) — publicly, with Mary and the mourners watching, before he was about to raise Lazarus from the dead. The tenderness was not performance and it was not weakness. It was honest engagement with human grief from a man who was about to demonstrate absolute power over death. He touched lepers (Mark 1:41) — men no one else touched. He stopped for the one woman bleeding in a crowd (Mark 5:25-34), for the one blind man by the roadside (Mark 10:46-52), for the child no one thought worth his time (Matthew 19:14).

**Moral courage under pressure.** When the religious establishment tried to trap him in front of crowds, Jesus did not equivocate or retreat. He answered with questions that exposed their hypocrisy and shamed their sophistication (Matthew 22:15-46). When Peter tried to prevent his arrest, Jesus rebuked him: "Put your sword back into its place" (Matthew 26:52). He walked into Jerusalem knowing exactly what was waiting for him.

**Complete honesty.** Jesus said things that lost him followers (John 6:60-66). He told a wealthy young man to sell everything he had (Matthew 19:21-22) and did not adjust the message when the man walked away. He called the Pharisees "whitewashed tombs" (Matthew 23:27) to their faces, publicly, in the Temple. He did not soften truth to manage relationships.

**Servant-heartedness.** "For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." (Mark 10:45) Jesus washed the feet of his disciples at the Last Supper (John 13:1-17) — the most menial, dirty task in the household — and told them to do the same. The greatest man who ever lived defined greatness as service.

This is the template. Decisive. Tender. Courageous. Honest. Servant-hearted. The cultural versions of masculinity — all of which emphasize some of these and suppress others — are partial. Jesus is the whole.

## What Biblical Masculinity Is Not

**Not stoicism.** The Stoic ideal — the man who suppresses emotion to achieve rational control — is a Greek philosophical framework, not a biblical one. Jesus did not suppress emotion. He expressed it fully and appropriately. He was angry in the Temple. He wept at a tomb. He was troubled in his spirit (John 13:21). He expressed affection for his disciples (John 13:1). Emotional capacity is not weakness. Emotional deception — pretending you feel nothing — is a form of dishonesty that damages the people closest to you and cuts you off from yourself.

**Not dominance.** The man who leads through fear, manipulation, or size is not exercising biblical authority — he is exercising fallen authority. Jesus's model of leadership is explicit: "You know that the rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their great ones exercise authority over them. It shall not be so among you. But whoever would be great among you must be your servant, and whoever would be first among you must be your slave." (Matthew 20:25-27) Leadership that does not serve is not Kingdom leadership. It is the opposite.

**Not passivity.** The equal and opposite error from dominance. The man who abdicates responsibility — who is absent from his family, disengaged from his calling, passive in the face of evil — is not humble. He is failing his assignment. Adam's failure in the Garden was not aggression. It was silence when he should have acted: he was "with her" (Genesis 3:6) while the serpent deceived his wife and said nothing.

**Not performance.** The man who performs masculinity for an audience — who is tougher, louder, more dominant in public than in private — is not strong. He is afraid. True masculine strength does not need an audience to exist.

## The Three Callings of a Kingdom Man

The biblical framework for masculine calling can be organized around three roles, all of which appear in Scripture and all of which require genuine strength to fulfill:

### Provider

"But if anyone does not provide for his relatives, and especially for members of his household, he has denied the faith and is worse than an unbeliever." (1 Timothy 5:8) The provision mandate is explicit and serious. But it is not only financial. The man who earns income but provides nothing of his time, attention, wisdom, or presence is not providing. Provision is the full commitment of your resources — all of them — to the flourishing of those entrusted to your care.

### Protector

Protection in the Kingdom is primarily spiritual. The husband and father who stands against spiritual threats to his family — who prays for them, who fights on their behalf in the spiritual realm, who guards what enters their home and what shapes their minds — is exercising the primary dimension of protection. Physical protection is real and required. Spiritual protection is more demanding and more often neglected.

### Priest

In the Old Testament, the priest was the mediator — the one who brought the presence of God to the people. In 1 Peter 2:9, all believers are called a "royal priesthood." For the man leading a household, this priestly function is specific: he mediates the presence of God in his home. He creates an environment where God is known, honored, and encountered. He does not outsource this to a church building or a pastor. He inhabits it.

## The World Needs This

The cultural crisis around masculinity is not solved by softening men or by hardening them. It is solved by men who are strong enough to be genuinely gentle, honest enough to be consistently trustworthy, present enough to be actually known, and anchored enough to lead without needing to control.

That man exists in the world. The world is hungry for him — even when it does not know that is what it is hungry for.

Be that man. Not because the culture needs a corrective. Because you bear the image of God and you were made for this.
