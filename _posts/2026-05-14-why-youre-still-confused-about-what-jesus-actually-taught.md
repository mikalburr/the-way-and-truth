There is a specific kind of confusion that afflicts committed Christians — people who have been in churches for years, who attend regularly, who own multiple Bibles and have read plenty of Christian books, and who are nonetheless genuinely uncertain about what Jesus actually taught on some of the most important questions they face.

They are confused about money — did Jesus require his followers to give up everything, or is prosperity a sign of blessing? They are confused about judgment — are they supposed to evaluate other people's behavior or not? They are confused about suffering — is it a sign of God's discipline, a test of faith, a result of insufficient faith, or just the normal experience of Kingdom citizens in a broken world? They are confused about prayer — why does it sometimes seem to work and sometimes not?

This confusion has a specific cause. And the cause is not that the teachings of Jesus are unclear.

## The Cause: Secondary Sources Have Replaced Primary Reading

Most Christians have heard more *about* Jesus than they have heard *from* Jesus. Their understanding of Jesus has been assembled primarily from sermons — which are one teacher's interpretation of selected passages, shaped by that teacher's theological tradition, cultural context, and communication goals. From devotionals — which are typically 200-word reflections on isolated verses, optimized for accessibility and emotional resonance rather than depth or context. From Christian books — which are valuable but are always filtered through the author's framework.

None of these sources are the problem individually. The problem is the substitution — when these secondary sources become the primary encounter with Jesus's teaching, rather than the Gospels themselves.

The result is a portrait of Jesus assembled from fragments, filtered through multiple interpreters, and missing the coherence and architecture that only comes from sustained direct engagement with the primary text.

Consider an analogy: imagine trying to understand Lincoln's thought by reading only other people's summaries of his speeches. You would get some accurate information, but you would also get distortions, omissions, and the interpretive biases of every summarizer. Eventually you would have a working picture that felt accurate but was significantly shaped by sources other than Lincoln himself.

This is the situation of many Christians with Jesus.

## Most People Have Heard About Jesus, Not From Jesus

Mark 1:14-15 records the first words of Jesus's public ministry: *"The time has come. The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news."*

How many Christians, if asked to summarize Jesus's core message, would say: "the Kingdom of God is near"? Most would say something like: "Jesus came to save us from our sins" — which is true but is not what Jesus himself led with. That formulation comes primarily from Paul's letters, particularly Romans 3-5.

Jesus led with the Kingdom. That is not a minor distinction. The Kingdom is the frame that makes every other teaching coherent. His ethics are Kingdom ethics — how citizens of the Kingdom behave. His parables are Kingdom parables — how the Kingdom works, grows, judges, and rewards. His demands are Kingdom demands — the operating system of citizens under a specific King.

Without the Kingdom frame, the teachings of Jesus become a random collection of moral advice, difficult ethical demands, and comforting promises — all sitting in tension with each other without an organizing principle.

With the Kingdom frame, they form a coherent whole. And you get the Kingdom frame from reading Jesus, not from reading about him.

## The Disciples Were Confused Too

One thing that rarely gets emphasized: the disciples who walked with Jesus in person, hearing him teach, watching him perform miracles, living in his company — they were frequently confused.

Mark 9:32: *"But they did not understand what he meant and were afraid to ask him."* (After Jesus predicted his death and resurrection for the second time.)

Luke 18:34: *"The disciples did not understand any of this. Its meaning was hidden from them, and they did not know what he was talking about."* (After the third passion prediction.)

John 12:16: *"At first his disciples did not understand all this. Only after Jesus was glorified did they realize that these things had been written about him and that these things had been done to him."*

These are the men who had the most access to Jesus any human beings have ever had. They still didn't fully understand until after the resurrection and the coming of the Holy Spirit (John 16:13: *"When he, the Spirit of truth, comes, he will guide you into all the truth"*).

If the Twelve were confused, you being confused is not a sign of failure. It is the normal experience of someone genuinely engaging with a teacher who is regularly saying things that disrupt existing categories.

Jesus was patient with his disciples' confusion (though not always gently — see Mark 8:17-21). He kept teaching. He repeated. He used different illustrations for the same truth. He asked questions to surface their misunderstanding. He was patient because the teaching mattered and the people mattered.

He is not less patient with you.

## The Solution Is Brutally Simple

Read the Gospels. All four of them. Slowly. Repeatedly. Without rushing to commentary, without deferring to your pastor's interpretation, without filtering through the theological system you inherited.

Not as a religious duty. As a primary source encounter with a person making total claims on your life.

The Berean Jews in Acts 17:11 were commended specifically for this: *"Now the Berean Jews were of more noble character than those in Thessalonica, for they received the message with great eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day to see if what Paul said was true."*

They received the apostolic preaching and then went to the primary source to verify it. That is the posture. Not suspicious rejection of teaching, but primary source verification.

The practical method: read one Gospel per week for four weeks. No other reading. No devotionals. No commentary. Just the text, read straight through, with a pen or notebook nearby for observations and questions.

At the end of four weeks, read it again. Then add the commentary layer — Matthew Henry for application, N.T. Wright for the Jewish context of the Kingdom, Myles Munroe's *Rediscovering the Kingdom* for the Kingdom framework applied to modern life.

The confusion will not vanish. But it will become productive confusion — the kind that comes from genuine engagement with a demanding teacher, rather than the fog of fragmented secondary encounter.

## What to Do With Persistent Confusion

Some passages will remain genuinely unclear even after careful reading and good commentary. This is normal. The New Testament itself acknowledges it — 2 Peter 3:16 says Paul's letters contain "some things that are hard to understand."

For persistent confusion, three practices help:

**Sit with it.** Don't rush to resolve it. Some confusion is productive — it keeps you engaged with the text and prevents premature closure on a meaning that isn't accurate. The disciples sat with confusion for three years before the resurrection resolved it.

**Look for parallel passages.** The Gospels often treat the same teaching from different angles. Matthew's version of the Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13) and Luke's version (Luke 11:2-4) illuminate each other. Mark's version of a teaching is often the shortest and most immediate; Matthew's often has the most context. Comparison across Gospels frequently resolves confusion.

**Ask whether the confusion is intellectual or moral.** Some passages are unclear because the language or context is genuinely complex. But other passages appear to be unclear because they make a demand you don't want to meet, and your mind is generating complexity to avoid the plain meaning. Jesus's command to love enemies, to sell possessions, to not worry about tomorrow — these are not actually confusing. They are demanding. The "confusion" is often resistance in disguise.

John 8:31-32: *"If you hold to my teaching, you are really my disciples. Then you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free."*

The Greek word for "hold" is *meno* — remain, abide, stay. Not visit occasionally. The knowing comes from remaining. Stay in the teaching long enough, honestly enough, and the confusion gives way to clarity.

You already have what you need. The Gospels are available. Jesus is speaking. The question is whether you will go to him directly and stay long enough to hear him.
