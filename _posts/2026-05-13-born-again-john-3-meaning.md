"Born again" is one of the most familiar phrases in American religious vocabulary. It is also one of the most emptied. Through four decades of political association, bumper-sticker theology, and cultural shorthand, the phrase has been stripped of the specific, confrontational content Jesus gave it in John 3.

Let's put it back.

## Nicodemus Came at Night — Why That Matters

John 3:1 introduces Nicodemus as "a man of the Pharisees" and "a ruler of the Jews" — a member of the Sanhedrin, the ruling religious council. He came to Jesus *nyktós* — by night (v. 2).

Commentators have proposed various reasons for the nighttime visit. Some suggest he came at night to avoid being seen associating with Jesus — a socially and professionally risky association for a Sanhedrin member. Others note that in Jewish tradition, evening was considered an ideal time for study and discussion of Torah. Still others argue that John uses "night" symbolically throughout his Gospel as a metaphor for spiritual darkness (John 13:30 — "and it was night" when Judas left the Last Supper).

All three readings may be simultaneously true, and John often writes on multiple levels. What is clear is that Nicodemus is a man of enormous religious credentials — a teacher, a ruler, a Pharisee — who has seen enough of Jesus to seek him out privately. He opens with a compliment: "Rabbi, we know that you are a teacher come from God, for no one can do these signs that you do unless God is with him" (v. 2, ESB).

Jesus does not receive the compliment. He answers a question Nicodemus hasn't asked yet — which in John's Gospel is a consistent pattern (cf. 4:13-14, 6:26-27). Jesus moves past the surface to the real issue.

## Anothen: The Deliberate Double Meaning

"Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born *anothen*, he cannot see the kingdom of God" (v. 3, ESV).

The Greek word *anothen* is the fulcrum of the entire exchange. It appears in two places in John 3 (vv. 3 and 7), and it carries a genuine lexical double meaning: it can mean "again" (*palin*) in the temporal sense, or it can mean "from above" (*anōthen*) in the spatial/directional sense. This is not a translator's choice between two options — both meanings are simultaneously active in the Greek, and Jesus exploits the ambiguity deliberately.

Nicodemus hears "again" — the temporal reading — and responds with his famous question: "How can a man be born when he is old? Can he enter a second time into his mother's womb and be born?" (v. 4). This is not mere literalism or stupidity. It is Nicodemus correctly grasping one dimension of the word and pressing Jesus on its impossibility.

But Jesus's intended meaning is "from above" — a birth whose origin is not human, not earthly, not achievable through religious performance or ethnic lineage. It is a birth that God initiates and accomplishes. Nicodemus should have understood both meanings; the fact that he fixes on the literal-temporal reading reveals that he is operating entirely in natural categories, unable to perceive the vertical dimension Jesus is introducing.

This is the diagnosis at the heart of the passage: religious expertise, even at the highest level, does not produce spiritual perception. You can be a Pharisee, a Sanhedrin member, a recognized teacher in Israel — and be entirely blind to what God is actually doing.

## Water and Spirit: What This Meant in Jewish Context

"Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God" (v. 5).

The phrase "born of water and Spirit" (*ek hydatos kai pneumatos*) has generated centuries of debate. The two main interpretive traditions:

**Baptismal interpretation**: Water refers to Christian baptism, Spirit to the internal work of the Holy Spirit. Strong in Catholic and mainline Protestant traditions, but difficult to sustain in this context — Christian baptism had not yet been established, and Nicodemus would have had no frame of reference for it.

**Jewish prophetic interpretation**: This is far more likely the correct frame. Jesus tells Nicodemus in verse 10 that as "the teacher of Israel," he *should* have understood this. That expectation is only meaningful if the concepts Jesus is using are already present in the texts Nicodemus knew.

The key passage is Ezekiel 36:25-27: "I will sprinkle clean water on you, and you will be clean; I will cleanse you from all your impurities and from all your idols. I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit in you; I will remove from you your heart of stone and give you a heart of flesh. And I will put my Spirit in you and move you to follow my decrees and be careful to keep my laws" (BSB).

Water (cleansing from impurity) and Spirit (internal transformation, divine empowerment to obey) appear together in Ezekiel's covenant promise. This is the eschatological new covenant — the transformation God promised to accomplish at the end of the age. Jesus is telling Nicodemus: that transformation has arrived. You cannot enter the new covenant community through ethnicity or Torah observance alone. You must be transformed by the Spirit from above.

Isaiah 44:3 and Joel 2:28-29 reinforce the same cluster of imagery. A serious student of the Hebrew prophets would have recognized the terrain. Nicodemus should have known what Jesus was talking about — and that is exactly Jesus's point.

## The Spirit Like Wind: John 3:8

"The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear its sound, but you do not know where it comes from or where it goes. So it is with everyone who is born of the Spirit" (v. 8).

The Greek *pneuma* means both "wind" and "spirit" — as does the Hebrew *ruach*, which Nicodemus would have known from Genesis 1:2, where the Spirit of God hovers over the waters. Jesus is playing on this shared word: the wind is invisible in itself but undeniable in its effects. You don't control it, predict it, or manufacture it. You only experience it and see what it does.

The new birth from above operates on the same terms. It is not manufactured by religious performance. It is not achieved through the right prayers or the right church or the right upbringing. It comes from above, like wind, according to God's sovereign initiative. You receive it or you don't. And when it has come, its effects are visible — not the origin, but the results.

## Why Nicodemus Should Have Understood

Verse 10 is one of the most pointed things Jesus says in John's Gospel: "Are you the teacher of Israel and yet you do not understand these things?" The Greek is emphatic — *su ei ho didaskalos tou Israēl* — "You — you are the teacher of Israel?"

The expected knowledge base was already in the prophets. The eschatological new covenant, the promise of the Spirit, the cleansing of water — these were not novel Christian inventions. Jesus was not introducing new material. He was announcing that the old material had arrived. Nicodemus's failure to connect the dots was not a failure of access to information. It was a failure of spiritual perception — precisely the condition that requires the new birth to correct.

This is a deeply uncomfortable passage for religious insiders. Nicodemus represents the most educated, most devout, most institutionally credentialed form of first-century Judaism. And Jesus tells him he cannot even *see* the Kingdom of God, let alone enter it, without something he does not yet have.

## What This Demands of a Modern Reader

The challenge of John 3 is not intellectual. The challenge is that it removes all the categories by which religious people typically measure themselves.

Church membership: not the issue.
Biblical knowledge: not the issue (Nicodemus had it).
Moral performance: not the issue (Pharisees were rigorous).
Family heritage: not the issue.

The issue is whether something has happened to you from above — a work of God in you that you did not produce and cannot fake. A new heart. A new orientation. The Spirit of God moving in you the way wind moves through trees, undeniable in its effects.

The evidence of that birth, Jesus implies elsewhere, is not a conversion experience you can point to on a calendar. It is transformation. New loves. New fears. New allegiances. A different relationship to the kingdom than you had before.

Nicodemus appears two more times in John's Gospel. In 7:50-51, he defends Jesus before the Sanhedrin, suggesting due process. In 19:39, he comes — again — with a mixture of myrrh and aloes, and with Joseph of Arimathea, takes Jesus's body for burial. Nicodemus, who came by night, ends up at the cross in public, at enormous personal and professional risk.

The night appears to have ended.

That's what being born from above looks like.
