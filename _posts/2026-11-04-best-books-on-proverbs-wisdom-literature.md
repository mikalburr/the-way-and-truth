A wise man without book-learning is rare. A book-learned man without wisdom is everywhere.

The biblical wisdom literature — Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes, parts of Psalms, the wisdom sayings of Jesus — is the part of Scripture explicitly designed to make you practically wise. Not just informed. Not just theologically correct. Actually wise: skilled at living well in a world that is fallen, complex, and often unjust.

These five books help you read the wisdom literature with depth. Each takes wisdom seriously as both theology and craft.

---

## 1. Proverbs (Tyndale) — Derek Kidner

{% include book-card.html
  title="Proverbs (Tyndale Old Testament Commentaries)"
  author="Derek Kidner"
  asin="0830842268"
  description="The classic short commentary on Proverbs. Kidner's prose is precise, his theological instincts sound, and his thematic arrangement of the proverbs makes the book usable as a teaching tool, not just a reference."
  verdict="The best concise commentary on Proverbs in print. Buy it, work through it, return to it."
%}

**What it covers:** A verse-by-verse commentary on Proverbs in the Tyndale Old Testament Commentaries series. Kidner provides a substantial introduction that orients the reader to the structure of the book, the major themes, and the theological framework of biblical wisdom. He then comments on the proverbs themselves, often grouping them thematically to show how the individual sayings relate.

**Who it's for:** Any serious reader of Proverbs. Kidner is accessible to laymen and substantial enough for pastors. The thematic groupings in the introduction make the book unusually useful for teaching.

**What it adds:** Density. Kidner says more per page than most commentators say in a chapter. His introductory essays on the "fear of the Lord," on the role of wisdom in the cosmos, and on the relationship between Proverbs and the New Testament are genuinely formative.

**One honest note:** Like all Tyndale volumes, Kidner is short by design. For longer treatment of any single proverb, supplement with Waltke or Longman. Kidner is the foundation.

## 2. Proverbs (BECOT) — Tremper Longman III

{% include book-card.html
  title="Proverbs (Baker Commentary on the Old Testament Wisdom and Psalms)"
  author="Tremper Longman III"
  asin="080102692X"
  description="Longman's mid-level scholarly commentary on Proverbs. Strong on the literary structure of the book, the relationship between wisdom and the rest of the Old Testament, and the theological coherence of Proverbs as a unified work."
  verdict="The best single-volume scholarly commentary on Proverbs. More accessible than Waltke, more thorough than Kidner."
%}

**What it covers:** A single-volume scholarly commentary on Proverbs that treats the literary structure of the book, the historical setting of Israelite wisdom, and the theological message at length. Longman engages the Hebrew text but explains terms accessibly. The introduction is unusually strong on how Proverbs relates to the rest of the Old Testament canon.

**Who it's for:** Pastors, teachers, and serious students who want more depth than Kidner provides without committing to Waltke's two-volume set. Longman is the right "second commentary" on Proverbs for most readers.

**What it adds:** Literary and theological integration. Longman shows that Proverbs is not a random collection of sayings but a carefully structured book with a theological argument. He also treats the relationship between Lady Wisdom in Proverbs 8 and Christ in the New Testament with care.

**One honest note:** Longman is moderate and balanced in tone, which can feel less pointed than Kidner or Waltke. The exegesis is sound and the integration is thoughtful.

## 3. The Book of Proverbs (NICOT) — Bruce Waltke

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Book of Proverbs (NICOT, 2 vols.)"
  author="Bruce K. Waltke"
  asin="0802825451"
  description="The most thorough scholarly commentary on Proverbs in print. Two volumes totaling over 1,200 pages. Waltke is one of the great Old Testament scholars of his generation, and this commentary represents the culmination of his life's work on Hebrew wisdom."
  verdict="The reference work for Proverbs. Heavy, exhaustive, and the volume to consult when you want maximum depth."
%}

**What it covers:** A full two-volume commentary on Proverbs in the New International Commentary on the Old Testament series. Volume 1 covers Proverbs 1–15; Volume 2 covers Proverbs 16–31. Waltke engages the Hebrew text in detail, treats the major scholarly debates, and develops a theological reading of Proverbs as a unified work with a coherent message.

**Who it's for:** Pastors, teachers, and serious students who want the fullest treatment available. Waltke's commentary is the volume you reach for when a popular treatment leaves you with questions.

**What it adds:** Comprehensiveness and Hebrew detail. Waltke handles the textual issues, the comparative ancient Near Eastern material, the literary structure, and the theology with a thoroughness no other Proverbs commentary matches. His introductions to each section are genuinely orienting.

**One honest note:** The two-volume set is a significant investment. Most readers do not need this level of depth on every passage. Use it as a reference rather than reading straight through.

## 4. Handbook on the Wisdom Books and Psalms — Daniel Estes

{% include book-card.html
  title="Handbook on the Wisdom Books and Psalms"
  author="Daniel J. Estes"
  asin="0801026993"
  description="The best single-volume introduction to the entire biblical wisdom corpus. Estes covers Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs, and Psalms with attention to literary structure, historical setting, and theological themes."
  verdict="The right starting point for a serious study of biblical wisdom literature. Read this before going deeper into any single book."
%}

**What it covers:** A book-by-book handbook to the entire biblical wisdom corpus — Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs, and Psalms. Each book gets a substantial chapter covering authorship, structure, major themes, theological message, and the relationship to other wisdom literature. The chapters function as extended introductions, not commentaries.

**Who it's for:** Men who want to understand biblical wisdom as a whole before committing to a particular book. Also excellent for pastors teaching through any of the wisdom books, since the orientation Estes provides is what most commentaries assume rather than supply.

**What it adds:** Integration. Estes treats the wisdom corpus as a unified theological tradition with internal tensions (especially between Proverbs and Ecclesiastes) that are themselves part of the canon's wisdom. Reading Proverbs after Estes is different from reading it cold.

**One honest note:** The handbook is not a commentary. It does not walk through individual passages. Use it as orientation, then go to Kidner, Longman, or Waltke for verse-level work.

## 5. Job: The Wisdom of the Cross — Christopher Ash

{% include book-card.html
  title="Job: The Wisdom of the Cross (Preaching the Word)"
  author="Christopher Ash"
  asin="1433513110"
  description="The best modern commentary on Job for serious readers. Ash treats Job as wisdom literature pointing forward to the cross — the suffering of the righteous Servant whose vindication answers the questions Job could not."
  verdict="The book to read on Job. Theological, pastoral, and saturated with the gospel."
%}

**What it covers:** A pastoral commentary on Job in the Preaching the Word series. Ash walks through the book of Job as a unified theological argument culminating in the appearance of God and the restoration of Job. He reads Job within the wisdom tradition and shows how the book points forward to Christ as the truly righteous sufferer whose vindication answers the questions Job's friends could not.

**Who it's for:** Anyone reading or teaching Job. Also for men who have suffered and need a careful biblical theology of suffering that does not minimize the pain or short-circuit the questions. Ash takes the agony of Job seriously and refuses easy answers.

**What it adds:** A Christological reading of Job that is exegetically careful rather than allegorical. Ash shows that Job is not just an Old Testament problem text — it is a wisdom book that prepares the reader for the gospel of the suffering and vindicated Christ.

**One honest note:** Ash's commentary is in the Preaching the Word series, which means it is structured for pastoral use rather than technical exegesis. For Hebrew detail, supplement with one of the major scholarly commentaries on Job. For theological and pastoral depth, Ash is unmatched.

## How to Use This List

Wisdom literature is read slowly, repeatedly, and across a lifetime. There is no rush.

**Read first:** Estes, *Handbook on the Wisdom Books and Psalms*. Orient yourself before going deeper.

**Read second:** Kidner, *Proverbs*. Begin a slow walk through Proverbs — a chapter a day, repeated.

**Read third:** Ash, *Job: The Wisdom of the Cross*. Job is where wisdom meets suffering and the gospel.

**Use as reference:** Longman or Waltke on Proverbs when Kidner needs supplementing.

Wisdom is not a download. It is a discipline. These books help you build it.
