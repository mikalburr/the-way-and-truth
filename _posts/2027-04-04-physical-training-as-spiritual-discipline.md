"But I discipline my body and keep it under control, lest after preaching to others I myself should be disqualified." — 1 Corinthians 9:27

The Greek verb Paul uses is *hupopiazo*. The literal meaning is to strike under the eye — the language of a boxer who lands a blow to subdue an opponent. Paul is not describing gentle self-care. He is describing combat with his own flesh. He treats his body the way a fighter treats an opponent he intends to defeat.

The same verse uses *doulagogeo* for "keep it under control" — to lead as a slave. Paul is making his body serve him, not the other way around. The body has appetites, demands, weaknesses, preferences. If it rules, the man is at its mercy. If it serves, the man can spend its capacity on what actually matters.

This is the theological frame for physical training that most Christian men have never been given. The body is not the enemy. It is also not the master. It is the instrument — God-given, sin-affected, requiring deliberate stewardship to operate at the capacity its work requires.

## The Two Truths About the Body

Scripture holds two things about the body in tension. Most men collapse one into the other and miss the balance.

**The body is temple.** 1 Corinthians 6:19-20 — "Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit within you, whom you have from God? You are not your own, for you were bought with a price. So glorify God in your body." The body is sacred space. It belongs to God. Honoring it is part of worship.

**The body is tool.** 1 Corinthians 9:27 — discipline it, keep it under control. The body is the instrument through which the man's work in the world happens. It can be weak or strong, capable or limited, ready or unprepared. The Christian is responsible for how it is conditioned.

Both are true at the same time. The body is sacred and it is functional. It deserves honor and it requires discipline. The man who treats his body as only temple becomes precious about it — afraid to push it, unwilling to make it uncomfortable. The man who treats his body as only tool becomes utilitarian — abusing it for goals, neglecting health, treating it as disposable.

The balanced view: this is the body God gave you, and you are accountable for what you do with it. Steward it well. Train it deliberately. Use it for Kingdom purposes.

## Why Most Men Avoid This

Several reasons.

**It is associated with vanity.** Men see fitness culture and assume training is about appearance. So the Christian man, wanting to avoid vanity, avoids training altogether. This is the wrong response. The fix for misdirected motivation is right motivation, not no motivation.

**It is uncomfortable.** Training is hard. It hurts. It requires showing up when you do not want to. Most men avoid discomfort by default, and physical training is voluntary discomfort.

**It feels secondary.** "I should be reading my Bible" or "I should be with my family" — these become reasons not to train. As if the body is somehow disconnected from the man's capacity to do those things.

**It seems separate from the spiritual life.** The man has not been taught that physical discipline is spiritual discipline. He treats them as different categories, and the spiritual one wins by default — except it does not actually win, because the man without physical discipline often does not have the will for the spiritual disciplines either.

The reality is that the disciplines reinforce each other. A man who is training his body is building the will that prayer, Scripture, and other disciplines require. Conversely, a man whose spiritual disciplines are weak often finds his physical training collapses for the same reasons.

## What Training Builds

Beyond the obvious physical results, training builds several things that transfer.

**Will.** The capacity to do what you said you would do when you do not feel like doing it. This is the master skill of the disciplined life, and it is built directly in the gym, on the run, in the weight room. Every training session you complete when you did not want to is a deposit in the will-bank that you can draw on elsewhere.

**Capacity.** A stronger body can do more. It can carry more weight, work longer hours, recover faster, handle stress better. The man who has trained for 20 years has more usable capacity at 50 than the untrained man at 35.

**Margin.** Untrained men live closer to their limits. A minor illness, a hard week, a sleep-deprived stretch, and they are at zero. Trained men have margin. The same week that breaks the untrained man is hard but survivable for the trained one.

**Modeling.** If you have kids, especially sons, your relationship with your body is being learned. Boys watch how their fathers handle their bodies. The father who trains models that the body is to be developed, not neglected. The father who has let his body collapse models the opposite.

**Longevity.** This is not about adding years — it is about adding capacity to the years you have. The man who trains is more likely to be physically capable at 70 than the man who does not. The grandchild who needs to be lifted, the wife who needs you healthy, the work that needs your presence — all of these require a body that has been kept up.

## The Framework

A working framework for a serious Christian man. Adapt to your starting point.

**Resistance training: 3-4x per week.** Compound movements — squat, deadlift, press, pull, hinge variations. Progress over time. The goal is functional strength and structural integrity, not maximum aesthetics. Start lighter than you think and progress slowly. Most injuries come from rushing the progression.

**Conditioning: 2-3x per week.** Cardiovascular work that elevates the heart rate. This can be running, rowing, cycling, hiking, or interval training. Mix steady-state aerobic work with higher-intensity intervals. The heart is a muscle — train it.

**Mobility and recovery: daily.** Five to ten minutes of stretching, mobility work, or yoga-style movement. This is what keeps the rest of the training sustainable. Most men skip this until they get hurt.

**Walking: daily.** Underrated. A 30-minute walk most days does more for cardiovascular health, mental clarity, and longevity than most men understand. Pair it with prayer or silence — two disciplines in one.

**Sleep: non-negotiable.** Seven to eight hours. Training is destroyed by poor sleep. So is everything else. Protect sleep with the same discipline you protect any other practice.

**Nutrition: simple and consistent.** Eat real food. Mostly plants and animals. Adequate protein. Limit processed garbage. You do not need a complicated diet — you need to actually eat what you know you should.

This framework, executed for years, produces a fundamentally different physical reality than the standard sedentary path. The man at 50 who has done this for 15 years is in a different category than the man who has not.

## Where the Spiritual Comes In

The training itself becomes a spiritual practice when it is engaged with the right orientation.

- The morning workout is also an offering — the first effort of the day, given before the day claims it.
- The hard set you complete when you do not feel like it is a small rehearsal for the harder work the spiritual life will require.
- The discipline that gets you to the gym on the third hard day in a row is the same discipline that gets you to Scripture on the day you do not feel like reading.
- The body you are stewarding is the one that will carry your prayer, your service, your fathering, your work, your worship through the rest of your years.

Paul saw all of this. He used athletic language constantly — running the race, fighting the fight, beating the body, finishing the course. The metaphor was not decoration. It was structural. The Christian life is engineered like an athletic season, and the body is the equipment.

This is the [warrior mindset](/2026/05/12/christian-warrior-mindset/) at the level of the flesh. The warrior does not neglect his weapon. He does not let his body atrophy. He keeps it ready, because he does not know what the calling will require, and he intends to be ready when the call comes.

See [physical training and spiritual formation](/2026/05/13/physical-training-spiritual-formation/) for the deeper integration of these two.

## Start This Week

If you are not training, start. This week. Three resistance sessions. Two conditioning sessions. A daily walk. The framework is not complicated. The discipline of doing it is the entire game.

If you have been training but the spiritual orientation has been off — vanity, performance, comparison — reorient. The same training, with the right motivation, becomes worship. The body is sacred. The training is stewardship. The capacity you are building is for service.

Discipline the body. Keep it under control. Make it serve the man, so the man can serve the Lord. This is the call. This is the work. Get to it.
