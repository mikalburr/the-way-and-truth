The Great Commandment is so familiar it has become wallpaper. Love God, love your neighbor — pastors say it, Christians nod, and everyone moves on without sitting with what Jesus actually claimed. He said that *all the law and the prophets hang on these two commandments* (Matthew 22:40). That is either the most important thing ever said about ethics, or it is a throwaway remark.

It is not a throwaway remark.

## The Setup: A Lawyer's Trap

Matthew 22:34-40 positions this exchange as a test. "One of them, a lawyer, asked him a question to test him: 'Teacher, which is the great commandment in the Law?'" (vv. 35-36, BSB).

The Greek *nomikos* — lawyer — refers to an expert in the Torah and its oral interpretation, not a legal professional in the modern sense. The question was not unusual in Jewish discourse. Rabbis regularly debated which commandment was the "weightiest" (*gadol*, the greatest). Various answers circulated in Second Temple Judaism: some prioritized Sabbath observance, others the prohibition of idolatry, others the command to honor parents.

The lawyer is not asking an open-ended question out of genuine curiosity. The verb *peirazōn* — "to test" — is the same word used for Satan's testing of Jesus in Matthew 4:1. He is looking for an answer he can use against Jesus — either to accuse him of diminishing the Torah or to discredit him with a non-standard answer.

Jesus gives him neither. He gives him the Shema.

## The Shema: What Jesus Was Quoting

"You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind." (Matthew 22:37, ESV, quoting Deuteronomy 6:5)

The Shema is the central confession of Jewish faith, recited twice daily by observant Jews: *Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad* — "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one." It is not a background text. It is the foundational declaration of Jewish identity, the words written on the doorpost (*mezuzah*), placed in phylacteries (*tefillin*), and spoken as the last words before death.

By citing the Shema, Jesus is not giving a novel answer. He is claiming that the entire Torah is already summarized in what every faithful Jew prays morning and evening. The answer was hiding in plain sight. Which means the failure to live by it was not an ignorance problem — it was a heart problem.

The command in Deuteronomy 6:5 is to love with *all* your heart, *all* your soul, and *all* your strength (Hebrew: *lebab*, *nephesh*, *me'od*). Matthew's version adds "mind" (*dianoia*) — either as an expansion of "strength" in Greek categories of the person, or as a distinct faculty drawing from the parallel passage in Mark 12:30. The effect is totalizing: every dimension of the human person — emotion, will, energy, thought — is called to be directed toward God.

This is not moderation. It is not balance. It is total reorientation.

## Agapaō: The Word for Love

The Greek verb here is *agapaō* — not *phileō* (friendship-love, affectionate attachment) or *eroō* (desire). *Agapē* in the New Testament and in the Septuagint (the Greek translation of the Hebrew Old Testament) frequently carries the meaning of committed, volitional love — love as action and posture, not primarily as feeling.

This matters enormously for how the command is heard. If Jesus had commanded a feeling, the command would be irrational — you cannot command an emotion. But *agapaō* is a decision, a direction of the will. You can be commanded to love God in this sense because love of this kind is an act of the will, expressed in allegiance, worship, attention, and obedience.

This also means the command has teeth. You cannot excuse the absence of love for God by saying "I just don't feel it." The command is not about feeling. It is about orientation, direction, and total commitment. You can act in love even when the emotional register is dry.

## The Second Commandment: Like Unto the First

"And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself." (Matthew 22:39, ESV, quoting Leviticus 19:18)

The Greek *homoios autē* — "like it" — places the second commandment in the same category and on the same level as the first. This is itself a move that goes beyond what any rabbinic tradition had said. Jesus is not simply ranking the commandments. He is saying that love for neighbor is inseparable from love for God — they are cut from the same cloth.

Leviticus 19:18 in its Hebrew context referred to fellow Israelites: "You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself." Jesus expands this radically — Luke 10:25-37, the Parable of the Good Samaritan, is Jesus's own commentary on who counts as neighbor. The answer: your enemy, the person your culture despises, the one you least want to help.

## "As Yourself": The Self-Regard Measure

"Love your neighbor *as yourself*" (*hōs seauton*) is one of the most practically instructive phrases in the New Testament. It establishes the measure of neighbor-love by analogy with self-regard.

This is not a command to love yourself first. It is a command that takes for granted that normal human beings exercise a baseline level of self-care, self-concern, and self-interest — you eat when you're hungry, you sleep when you're tired, you protect yourself from harm. The command says: apply that same level of attentiveness, concern, and practical care to your neighbor.

This standard is simultaneously accessible and demanding. Everyone knows what it feels like to want their needs met, to want to be heard, to want to be helped in a moment of crisis. The command says: want that for others with the same urgency and act accordingly.

## "On These Two Commandments Hang All the Law and the Prophets"

"On these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets." (Matthew 22:40, ESV)

The Greek *kremantai* — "hang" or "depend" — is the same word used in Acts 5:30 for hanging on a tree. Everything in the Torah and the Prophets — every specific command, every narrative, every prohibition — derives its meaning from and finds its goal in these two loves.

This is a hermeneutical claim of enormous significance. Jesus is not abolishing the law (Matthew 5:17 — he came to fulfill, not abolish). He is saying that the law is correctly read only when you read it as an expression of love for God and love for neighbor. Any interpretation of Torah that produces cruelty, exploitation, or contempt for persons has misread the text, because it has missed the axis on which the whole thing turns.

This also means that the Great Commandment is a test for all religious practice. Does your prayer life increase your love for God and your attentiveness to neighbors? Then it's functioning correctly. Does it become self-referential performance? It has drifted from its purpose. Does your Sabbath observance (or its Christian equivalent) create rest and renewal that increases your capacity to love? Or has it become a rule that justifies neglecting human need? (cf. Matthew 12:1-14).

## Why This Is Harder Than It Sounds

The Great Commandment is not a warm summary that lets everyone off the hook. It is the hardest possible demand placed on the whole person.

Total love for God means: nothing competes. Not career. Not comfort. Not reputation. Not safety. Not even family, in the ultimate sense (Luke 14:26). God gets the whole heart, soul, mind, and strength — which means there are no exceptions, no reserved zones, no departments of your life where God's claim does not reach.

Total love for neighbor means: the other person's interests are as real to you as your own. This eliminates indifference (I don't hate them, I just don't think about them), selective compassion (I help people like me), and convenient non-involvement (it's not my problem).

Taken seriously, the Great Commandment rearranges everything. It is supposed to.

The lawyer asked which commandment was the greatest. Jesus gave him the two commandments that contain all the others — and then watched him walk away. Matthew doesn't record what the lawyer said next.

Sometimes the right answer is also the hardest one to live with.
