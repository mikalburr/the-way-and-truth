John 17 is the longest recorded prayer of Jesus, and it is the only place in the gospels where we hear, in his own words, what he prayed for his future followers — people who had not yet believed when he prayed. That includes everyone reading this. It is worth knowing what he asked.

The prayer comes at the end of the Upper Room discourse (John 13-16), spoken on the night Jesus was betrayed, hours before the cross. It is divided into three movements: prayer for himself (vv. 1-5), prayer for the disciples present (vv. 6-19), and prayer for all who would believe through their witness (vv. 20-26).

## The Setting — "The Hour Has Come"

Verse 1: "Father, the hour has come." Throughout John's gospel, Jesus has referred to "the hour" as a future appointment — "my hour has not yet come" (2:4, 7:30, 8:20). In John 17, the hour arrives. This prayer is spoken on the threshold of the passion, and everything in it is framed by what is about to happen.

The Greek *epēren tous ophthalmous* — "he lifted up his eyes" — is a posture of address, not bowed reverence. Jesus prays facing the Father directly, as a Son.

## First Movement — For Himself (vv. 1-5)

The first petitions are about glory. "Glorify your Son that the Son may glorify you" (17:1). The glorification Jesus asks for is the cross. In John's gospel, the cross and the resurrection together are the moment of glory — the revelation of who God is.

Verse 3 contains the only definition of eternal life Jesus ever explicitly gives: "This is eternal life, that they know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent." Eternal life is not duration alone. It is relational knowledge of the Father and the Son. The Greek *ginōskōsin* is present subjunctive — ongoing knowing.

Verse 5: "And now, Father, glorify me in your own presence with the glory that I had with you before the world existed." This is one of the clearest pre-incarnation claims in the gospels. Jesus prays to return to a glory he held before creation.

## Second Movement — For the Disciples Present (vv. 6-19)

Jesus prays five things for the disciples in the room:

**Protection (v. 11):** "Holy Father, keep them in your name." The protection requested is being kept *in the name* — kept inside the identity and authority of God. The threat is not primarily physical. It is the danger of losing identity in the world Jesus is leaving them in.

**Unity (v. 11):** "that they may be one, even as we are one." The model of Christian unity is the unity of the Father and the Son — not organizational, but relational and mutual.

**Joy (v. 13):** "that they may have my joy fulfilled in themselves." His joy in them, mid-prayer, on the threshold of crucifixion. Not happiness about circumstances. Settled joy in the Father.

**Preservation from the evil one (v. 15):** "I do not ask that you take them out of the world, but that you keep them from the evil one." Removal is not the answer. Preservation in the middle of the world is.

**Sanctification in truth (v. 17):** "Sanctify them in the truth; your word is truth." *Hagiason* — set apart. The instrument of sanctification is the word. The word is identified as truth.

## Third Movement — For All Who Will Believe (vv. 20-26)

Verse 20 is the pivot: "I do not ask for these only, but also for those who will believe in me through their word." Jesus expands the prayer to include everyone who will come to faith through the apostolic witness. That is the entire historical church, including every reader.

Then five petitions:

**One — Unity (vv. 21-23).** "That they may all be one, just as you, Father, are in me, and I in you, that they also may be in us, so that the world may believe that you have sent me." The Greek *kathōs* — "just as" — establishes a comparison, not just a similarity. The unity Jesus prays for is patterned on the inner-Trinitarian unity. And the purpose is missional: the world believes when the church is one.

Verse 23 repeats with intensification: "I in them and you in me, that they may become perfectly one, so that the world may know that you sent me and loved them even as you loved me." The same love the Father has for the Son is extended to believers. The structure of mutual indwelling — "you in me, I in them" — is what unity is built on.

**Two — Presence (v. 24).** "Father, I desire that they also, whom you have given me, may be with me where I am." Jesus asks the Father that his followers would be where he is. This is the petition that secures the believer's ultimate destination — not as a transaction but as a request from the Son.

**Three — Vision of Glory (v. 24).** "To see my glory that you have given me because you loved me before the foundation of the world." The eschatological promise is sight — to actually see the glory of the Son. The believer's future is not just survival but vision.

**Four — The Father's Love in Them (v. 26).** "That the love with which you have loved me may be in them." Not merely the Father's love directed toward them, but the Father's love *for the Son* indwelling them. The believer is brought inside the love that the Father has for the Son.

**Five — Christ Himself in Them (v. 26).** "And I in them." The prayer ends with the mutual indwelling that began the petition — the Son in the believer, the believer in the Father, the Father's love in the believer, all bound up together.

## The "In" Structure

The repeated grammatical structure of John 17 is mutual indwelling. The Father is in the Son. The Son is in the Father. The Son is in the believer. The believer is in the Father and the Son. The Father's love is in the believer. The Son's glory is given to the believer.

This is not poetic flourish. It is theological grammar. The believer's life is structurally inside the relationship that the Father and the Son have with each other. (Compare [The Lord's Prayer](/true-teachings/2026/05/12/lords-prayer-breakdown/) for how this indwelling shapes prayer itself.)

## What This Prayer Means for You

If you have come to faith through the apostolic witness — through the New Testament — Jesus prayed verses 20-26 explicitly for you. On the night before he died, he prayed that you would be one with other believers, that you would be with him, that you would see his glory, that the Father's love would be in you, and that he himself would be in you.

This is not a theory about prayer. It is a record of what was prayed. The petitions are answered progressively — some now, some at his return, some eternally.

The prayer is the foundation for everything the apostles teach later about union with Christ. It happened first. It was prayed before it was theologized.
