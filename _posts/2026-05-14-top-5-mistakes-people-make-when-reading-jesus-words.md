Bad reading produces bad theology. Bad theology produces a distorted life. The stakes of how you read Jesus's words are not academic — they are existential.

These five mistakes are common, correctable, and consequential.

## Mistake 1: Reading Jesus's Words as Advice Rather Than Authority

Advice is something you consider, weigh, and adopt if it seems useful. Authority is something you obey because of who is speaking.

Most modern readers come to the Gospels looking for insights to improve their lives. The assumption is that the reader is the judge of what is worth applying. This posture is incompatible with who Jesus claimed to be.

Matthew 7:28-29: *"When Jesus had finished saying these things, the crowds were amazed at his teaching, because he taught as one who had authority, and not as their teachers of the law."* The Greek word is *exousia* — authority, the right and power to act. The teachers of the law derived their authority from Moses. Jesus derived his authority from himself. He didn't say "Moses commanded." He said "I tell you."

John 12:48: *"There is a judge for the one who rejects me and does not accept my words; the very words I have spoken will condemn them at the last day."*

His words are not self-help. They are the words of a King. The correction: approach the Gospels asking "What is Jesus commanding?" — not "What advice is Jesus offering?"

## Mistake 2: Spiritualizing What Was Meant Literally — and Literalizing What Was Meant Spiritually

This interpretive error runs in both directions.

**Spiritualizing the literal:** When Jesus says *"Sell your possessions and give to the poor"* (Luke 12:33), many readers immediately translate it to mean "hold things loosely in your heart." This is comfortable because it costs nothing. But the context is a real inheritance dispute, a real parable about a real man, and a direct command to real disciples. The spiritualized reading is a way of avoiding the concrete demand.

**Literalizing the spiritual:** When Jesus says *"Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you"* (John 6:53), many first-century hearers took it literally and were scandalized. The discourse is clearly operating at a symbolic level — Jesus is talking about total dependence on him, not physical cannibalism.

The key is genre and context. Parabolic teaching operates differently from direct ethical instruction. Ask: is the surface meaning the intended meaning? If Jesus is using a story to make a Kingdom point, the surface narrative is the vehicle. If he is giving direct instruction — *"Love your enemies"* — the surface demand is what he means.

## Mistake 3: Reading Individual Verses Without Literary Context

The devotional verse-by-verse culture has produced people who know many individual verses but have little understanding of the passages those verses come from.

Matthew 7:1: *"Do not judge, or you too will be judged."* This is probably the most cited verse by non-Christians and one of the most misapplied by Christians. It is routinely used to mean: never make a moral evaluation of another person's behavior.

Read the full passage. Matthew 7:1-5 is about hypocritical judgment — judging others for failures you carry unacknowledged in your own life. Verse 5: *"First take the plank out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother's eye."* After the plank is removed, you do remove the speck. The passage is not a prohibition on discernment. It is a prohibition on hypocritical judgment.

Then Matthew 7:15-16: *"Watch out for false prophets... By their fruit you will recognize them."* Recognizing fruit requires evaluation. Jesus is explicitly asking for discernment.

Context does not just add nuance. It sometimes completely reverses the apparent meaning of an isolated verse.

The correction: always read the paragraph, then the chapter, then the full discourse. Individual verses get their meaning from their context, and that meaning is often more demanding than the isolated verse suggests.

## Mistake 4: Filtering Jesus Through Paul Instead of the Reverse

Paul's letters — particularly Romans and Galatians — are the most theologically dense documents in the New Testament. Paul is essential reading. He is also secondary to Jesus.

The mistake is reading Paul first and then reading the Gospels through Pauline categories. The Sermon on the Mount is the clearest example. A heavily Pauline reading treats its ethical demands as law designed to expose inadequacy and drive us to grace: we cannot meet these demands; thank God for Christ who met them on our behalf. This is a coherent system. It is not what Jesus said the Sermon was.

Jesus never said the Sermon was a demonstration of inadequacy. He said in Matthew 7:24 that hearing his words and putting them into practice is like building on rock. The emphasis is practice — not failure that leads to grace.

Paul himself understood the subordination. 1 Corinthians 11:1: *"Follow my example, as I follow the example of Christ."* Paul directs attention back to Christ, not to himself. Read Paul through Jesus, not Jesus through Paul.

## Mistake 5: Expecting Understanding Before Obedience

The Western mind proceeds in sequence: learn — understand — evaluate — decide — act. Jesus consistently reversed this.

John 7:17: *"Anyone who chooses to do the will of God will find out whether my teaching is from God or whether I speak on my own."* Choose to do — then find out. Understanding comes on the far side of the choice, not the near side.

Matthew 4:19: *"Come, follow me, and I will make you fishers of men."* Not: I will explain my program, you will evaluate it, and if it meets your criteria you may follow. Come follow — and in the following, I will make you into something.

Luke 9:57-62 records three would-be disciples who each wanted to delay following until something else was resolved. Jesus refused all three delays. The Kingdom admits no waiting room.

The correction: when studying a passage, ask specifically — what action does this require of me, and am I doing it? If the answer is "I'm still figuring it out," examine honestly whether you are processing or deferring.

Jesus was direct about this. Matthew 7:24: *"Everyone who hears these words of mine and puts them into practice is like a wise man who built his house on the rock."*

Hear. Practice. Build on rock. The sequence is clear. The mistakes are what interrupt it.
