The culture war over masculinity has produced two bad options.

Option one: masculinity as domination — the loud, aggressive, take-what-you-want model that demands strength but has no theology of sacrifice, no framework for tenderness, and no concept of accountability to God.

Option two: masculinity as problematic — a fundamentally toxic category that needs to be dismantled, softened, and re-educated out of men before it does more damage.

Both are wrong, and both produce broken men. The first produces tyrants. The second produces passive men who have no idea what they are for.

The Bible offers a third option — not a compromise between the two, but a completely different framework altogether. It describes men who are strong enough to sacrifice everything, tender enough to weep, courageous enough to tell hard truth, and honest enough to acknowledge that their strength is not their own.

This is not a culture-war article. It is a textual one. Let's go to the actual texts.

## Genesis 2: Formed First, Given the Assignment

The sequence matters in Genesis 2. Adam is formed first (v. 7), placed in the garden (v. 15), given the assignment before Eve exists (v. 15-17), and given the task of naming every living creature (v. 19-20). Only after Adam has been formed, placed, commissioned, and named does God bring Eve.

*"The LORD God took the man and placed him in the Garden of Eden to work it and to guard it."* (Genesis 2:15, BSB)

Two Hebrew words carry the weight here: *abad* (to work, serve, cultivate) and *shamar* (to guard, keep, protect). Adam was given both a productive mandate and a protective mandate before Eve arrived. He was commissioned for responsibility before he was given companionship.

This is not about women's inferiority — nothing in the text suggests that. It is about the shape of male calling. Men are built, at a foundational level, to cultivate and to guard. To bring things to flourishing and to protect what is entrusted to them.

When Adam fails in Genesis 3, he is not absent — he is *there* (v. 6, "who was with her"). His failure is not of location but of character. He stands passive while the serpent deceives his wife. He fails in the guarding function he was built for. And when God comes looking, He does not call for Eve first. He calls for Adam: *"But the LORD God called out to the man, 'Where are you?'"* (Genesis 3:9, BSB)

Adam is held accountable because he carried the primary responsibility. That is not a curse — it is the design. The man is responsible. He will be asked where he was, what he did, whether he cultivated and guarded what was entrusted to him.

That weight has not been lifted.

## Ephesians 5:25: Love as Sacrifice, Not Control

Ephesians 5:22-33 is routinely misread in both directions. Wives are called to submit (v. 22-24), and this verse gets all the attention — either from people outraged by it or from men who use it as a trump card.

But the text gives husbands a far harder assignment: *"Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave Himself up for her."* (Ephesians 5:25, BSB)

That is not soft language. Paul is commanding men to love the way Jesus loved — and Jesus' love was a death. Voluntary, sacrificial, selfless, costly death. The man who takes Ephesians 5:22 seriously and hands it to his wife as an obligation while ignoring the death-sized demand of verse 25 has misread the text catastrophically.

The model for male leadership in marriage is not the Roman *paterfamilias* with authority over everyone beneath him. It is the crucified Christ who gave up everything for those He loved. The head of a home is not the one who makes the rules. He is the one who bleeds for the household first.

Verse 28 makes the application explicit: *"In the same way, husbands ought to love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself."* (BSB) Self-interest and love for your wife are the same thing, rightly understood. A man who dominates or controls his wife is working against his own interest. A man who sacrifices for her is building the most important human relationship he will ever have.

## 1 Timothy 3: Character Is the Qualification for Leadership

When Paul lists the requirements for church leadership in 1 Timothy 3:1-7, the list is almost entirely about character, not competency. He does not ask if the elder is gifted, talented, educated, or experienced. He asks what kind of man he is.

*"An overseer must be above reproach, the husband of one wife, temperate, self-controlled, respectable, hospitable, able to teach, not addicted to wine, not violent but gentle, not quarrelsome, not a lover of money. He must manage his own household well, keeping his children under control in a dignified manner — for if a man does not know how to manage his own household, how can he take care of the church of God?"* (1 Timothy 3:2-5, BSB)

Notice the sequence. Before anything about his public leadership capacity comes his private character and his home. Can he govern himself? Is he one woman's man in integrity? Is he free from addictive patterns? Is he gentle? Is his home in order?

The model here is integration. A man's private character and his public leadership are not separate departments. What he is at home on Thursday is what he is. Leadership is the overflow of a formed interior life — it cannot be performed sustainably in public if it is absent in private.

This is the opposite of the culture's model of leadership, which is largely about competency, charisma, and results. God's model asks: who are you when no one is watching? What kind of man are you in your own home?

The answer to those questions is the answer to the leadership question.

## Proverbs 27:17: The Man Who Sharpens You

*"Iron sharpens iron, and one man sharpens another."* (Proverbs 27:17, BSB)

The Hebrew for "sharpens" in the second clause is *chalah* — to sharpen by contact, but also to make bright, to cause to shine. The man who sharpens you is not just making you better at things. He is revealing you more fully — the way friction on iron brings out its edge and luster.

This text exposes a dangerous tendency in men: the preference for surface relationships that never get close enough to actually sharpen. Men tend to bond over shared activity or shared interest without ever going deep enough to actually challenge each other. The result is camaraderie without transformation, friendship without sharpening.

The Biblical model of brotherhood is purposefully uncomfortable. It requires a man who knows you well enough to say what is actually true, and a relationship resilient enough to sustain the friction that honesty creates. That is not common. It requires cultivation. It requires risk.

A man who has never been sharpened by a friend is a man with dull edges — capable enough at the surface, but not refined. Men need this. Not optionally.

## The Integrated Man: Strong, Tender, Courageous, Honest

What emerges from these texts is not a monolithic character type but an integrated person whose strength is expressed in multiple registers.

**Strong but not domineering.** The man of Ephesians 5 is strong enough to die for his family. That strength is not expressed as control but as sacrifice. He does not need to dominate his household because he is secure enough in his identity to serve.

**Tender but not passive.** Jesus wept (John 11:35). The shortest verse in Scripture, and one of the most important for men. The Son of God, described elsewhere as the Lion of Judah, wept at a grave. Tender emotion and fierce courage occupied the same person without contradiction. The man who cannot weep has a deficiency, not a strength.

**Courageous but not reckless.** Courage in Scripture is consistently directional — toward something that needs to be done or defended, not toward proving oneself. Joshua 1:9 commands courage as obedience to God's call, not as a personality trait to display. The brave man moves toward the hard thing because it is right, not because he enjoys danger.

**Honest but not brutal.** Ephesians 4:15 calls for "speaking the truth in love" — not softening truth until it has no content, and not deploying truth as a weapon. Honesty in a Kingdom man is governed by love. The question is not "can I say this?" but "can this person receive this truth from me in a way that actually helps them?"

## The Cost of Recovering This

Biblical masculinity is not easy or comfortable. The culture-war version of masculinity is actually easier — it requires strength but no sacrifice, dominance but no death, authority but no accountability.

The Scriptural version is harder. It asks you to be strong enough to sacrifice, courageous enough to be honest, tender enough to weep, humble enough to be led by the Spirit, disciplined enough that your private life is not a contradiction of your public one, and faithful enough that what you are at home on an ordinary Wednesday is what you are in front of everyone else.

That is a man worth becoming. It is also a man who costs the world something if he does not exist.

The families that need a man who guards and cultivates. The friendships that need a man who sharpens. The organizations that need a man whose character qualifies him to lead. The culture that needs men who are neither tyrants nor passive bystanders.

The Biblical model of manhood is not a relic. It is not a culture-war talking point. It is the design — drawn from the text, tested in history, and urgently needed in the world you occupy right now.

The question is not whether the culture approves of it. The question is whether you will build it.

Start there. Build the character. The culture can catch up.
