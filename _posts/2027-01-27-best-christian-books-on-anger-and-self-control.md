Anger is not your enemy. Anger is information. The question is what your anger is telling you about your heart, your circumstances, and the gap between what you want and what you have.

Most Christian men handle anger in one of two failing ways: explode and apologize, or suppress and stew. Neither is biblical. Neither produces growth. These five books offer a third way — a careful, biblical, heart-level approach to understanding and addressing anger that actually produces change.

---

## 1. Good and Angry — David Powlison

{% include book-card.html
  title="Good and Angry: Redeeming Anger, Irritation, Complaining, and Bitterness"
  author="David Powlison"
  asin="1942572743"
  description="The modern classic on anger from one of the most important biblical counselors of his generation. Powlison treats anger as a complex moral and spiritual response that requires careful examination — not just management techniques but heart-level renovation."
  verdict="The single best Christian book on anger in print. Read it slowly. Re-read it every few years."
%}

**What it covers:** A full biblical and pastoral treatment of anger in all its forms — the explosive anger that damages relationships, the simmering bitterness that poisons the heart, the chronic irritation that wears down a marriage, the righteous anger that responds rightly to evil. Powlison treats anger as a moral and spiritual response that reveals what we love and what we hate. The book moves from diagnosis to gospel-centered change.

**Who it's for:** Any Christian man who is honest enough to admit he has an anger problem — and any Christian man who insists he does not. Both groups need this book. Powlison's diagnostic questions will surface anger you did not know you had.

**What it adds:** A theology of anger that takes the emotion seriously without either celebrating it or pathologizing it. Powlison's central insight is that anger is a response to perceived violation of something we value. The question is whether what we value is what God values — and whether our response is proportionate, righteous, and constructive.

**One honest note:** Powlison's prose is dense, and the book rewards slow reading. Do not try to finish it in a weekend. Work through it over a month or two, with a pen, and stop frequently to apply.

## 2. Uprooting Anger — Robert Jones

{% include book-card.html
  title="Uprooting Anger: Biblical Help for a Common Problem"
  author="Robert D. Jones"
  asin="0875527035"
  description="A focused biblical counseling resource on anger. Jones examines the heart-level causes of anger — disordered desires, unmet expectations, claims of personal sovereignty — and traces them back to specific gospel-centered solutions."
  verdict="The best practical workbook on anger from a biblical counseling perspective. Pairs well with Powlison."
%}

**What it covers:** A focused study of anger as a heart issue requiring heart-level change. Jones uses the framework of "ruling desires" — the things we want so badly that not getting them produces anger. He walks through identifying the desires, examining whether they are legitimate, and bringing them under the rule of Christ. Practical exercises and case studies throughout.

**Who it's for:** Men who have done some work on anger but feel stuck — the explosions are less frequent, but the underlying patterns are still there. Jones helps you see the desires beneath the anger and address them at the level where change actually happens.

**What it adds:** Diagnostic precision. Jones gives you specific questions to ask when anger surfaces: What did I want that I didn't get? What did I expect that didn't happen? Where am I claiming authority that belongs to God? The questions get you to the actual problem faster than introspection alone usually does.

**One honest note:** Jones writes within the biblical counseling tradition (formerly nouthetic counseling) associated with CCEF and similar institutions. The framework is robust but particular. Some readers from other traditions may find the categories unfamiliar at first.

## 3. War of Words — Paul David Tripp

{% include book-card.html
  title="War of Words: Getting to the Heart of Your Communication Struggles"
  author="Paul David Tripp"
  asin="0875526047"
  description="Tripp's classic study of the words we use — and the heart conditions that produce them. Anger expresses itself in speech more than in any other channel. This book diagnoses what your words reveal and prescribes gospel-centered change."
  verdict="The book on the connection between speech, anger, and the heart. Essential for any man whose anger primarily shows up in what he says."
%}

**What it covers:** A biblical study of speech — the words we use, the heart conditions they reveal, and the gospel-centered path to changed communication. Tripp argues that speech is never neutral: every word reveals what the heart treasures, fears, or rejects. Anger, criticism, sarcasm, manipulation, and silence are all forms of communication that expose specific heart issues.

**Who it's for:** Men whose anger primarily expresses itself in what they say — sharp words to a spouse, criticism of children, sarcasm at work, contempt in conflict. Tripp's framework helps you see speech as the diagnostic window into the heart.

**What it adds:** The connection between speech and heart. Tripp shows that you cannot change your words by deciding to change your words. You change your words by addressing the heart conditions that produce them. The implication: the path to better communication runs through the gospel, not through communication techniques.

**One honest note:** Tripp's prose is repetitive in places — he wants the central points to land, and he restates them often. Readers who prefer tighter prose may find some sections slower than necessary. The substance is worth the pace.

## 4. Anger, Anxiety, and Fear — Stuart Scott

{% include book-card.html
  title="Anger, Anxiety, and Fear: A Biblical Perspective"
  author="Stuart Scott"
  asin="1633420221"
  description="A short, practical biblical counseling resource on the cluster of emotions that drive much of men's struggle — anger, anxiety, and fear. Scott treats them together because they often function together in the heart of the struggling man."
  verdict="The best short biblical counseling resource for the man dealing with multiple overlapping struggles. Practical and gospel-centered."
%}

**What it covers:** A short pastoral treatment of three related emotional struggles — anger, anxiety, and fear — that often appear together in the same man. Scott treats each emotion biblically, identifies the heart conditions that produce them, and gives practical gospel-centered direction for change. The book is part of the P&R Resources for Changing Lives series.

**Who it's for:** Men whose struggle is not just anger but the broader cluster of anger, anxiety, and fear that often appear together. The integrated treatment is more useful than separate books for each issue when the issues are connected — which they usually are.

**What it adds:** Integration of related struggles. Most books on anger treat anger in isolation. Scott shows the connections: anger often masks fear, fear often produces anxiety, anxiety often erupts as anger. Addressing the cluster rather than just one piece is more effective.

**One honest note:** This is a short pastoral resource, not a comprehensive treatment of any of the three topics. Use it as a starting point or a workbook companion to Powlison, Jones, or Tripp.

## 5. The Heart of Anger — Lou Priolo

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Heart of Anger: Practical Help for the Prevention and Cure of Anger in Children"
  author="Lou Priolo"
  asin="1879737264"
  description="Priolo's practical guide to addressing anger in children — but the diagnostic framework and the heart-level approach apply equally to adult men. The book has been used by Christian counselors and parents for over twenty-five years."
  verdict="The classic biblical counseling resource on anger. Originally written for parenting, but the framework works for any age."
%}

**What it covers:** A practical guide to identifying and addressing anger as a heart issue. Priolo's primary audience is parents of angry children, but the framework — anger as a sinful response to unmet expectations, the role of family patterns in shaping anger, the gospel-centered path to change — applies to adults as well. The book includes specific diagnostic tools and counseling exercises.

**Who it's for:** Christian fathers dealing with anger in their children. Also men working through their own anger who want to understand the family patterns that may have shaped it. Priolo is unusually clear about the inherited nature of anger and the work required to break the cycle.

**What it adds:** Family systems clarity. Priolo recognizes that anger is rarely just an individual problem — it is shaped by the family of origin, reinforced by family patterns, and passed to children unless deliberately interrupted. The implications for fathers are particularly direct.

**One honest note:** Priolo writes within the older biblical counseling tradition, and some of his language and applications feel dated. The core diagnostic framework remains useful. Pair it with Powlison for the more contemporary pastoral voice.

## How to Use This List

Anger is a long fight. None of these books fixes it in a weekend. All of them give you tools you will use for years.

**Read first:** Powlison, *Good and Angry*. The full diagnostic and pastoral framework.

**Read second:** Jones, *Uprooting Anger*. Workbook-level application of the diagnostic.

**Read third:** Tripp, *War of Words*. Address the speech that anger produces.

**For broader struggle:** Scott, *Anger, Anxiety, and Fear*. When anger is not the only issue.

**For fatherhood:** Priolo, *The Heart of Anger*. Break the cycle for your children.

The man who refuses to address his anger will pass it to his sons. Address it. The gospel has resources for this.
