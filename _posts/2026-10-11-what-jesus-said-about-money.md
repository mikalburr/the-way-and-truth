Jesus talked about money more often than almost any other subject. Roughly a third of his parables involve money, possessions, or stewardship. The Sermon on the Mount has a sustained section on it. The encounters with the rich young ruler, Zacchaeus, the widow at the temple treasury, and the disciples on their travels all turn on money.

Pulled out individually, these teachings can be turned into ascetic guilt or prosperity gospel. Held together, they form a coherent picture that is more demanding than either — and more honest.

## "You Cannot Serve God and Mammon" (Matthew 6:24)

The foundation is in the Sermon on the Mount. "No one can serve two masters, for either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve God and money."

The Greek for "money" here is *mamōnas* — the Aramaic *mammon*, meaning wealth or property. Jesus does something pointed: he personifies it. Mammon takes the grammatical position of a master, parallel to God. Two masters. Two lords. You cannot serve both.

This is not a statement about budgeting. It is a statement about ultimate allegiance. Money, in Jesus's framework, is not neutral. It is a spiritual power with a claim. The question is not whether money has gravity. The question is which gravitational field you are inside.

## "Where Your Treasure Is" (Matthew 6:19-21)

Immediately preceding the mammon verse: "Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moth and rust destroy and where thieves break in and steal, but lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven... For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also."

The order in verse 21 is important. The heart follows the treasure, not the other way around. Most modern teaching reverses this — "get your heart right, then your spending will follow." Jesus says the opposite: where you put your money is where your heart will end up. Spending is upstream of affection.

This makes financial decisions diagnostic and formative. You can read your actual loyalties off your transaction history.

## The Rich Young Ruler (Mark 10:17-22)

A wealthy man kneels before Jesus and asks what he must do to inherit eternal life. He claims to have kept the commandments from his youth. Mark 10:21 reports: "And Jesus, looking at him, loved him, and said to him, 'You lack one thing: go, sell all that you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me.'"

The young man walks away sorrowful, "for he had great possessions."

This instruction is not universal. Jesus does not tell every wealthy follower to liquidate. Zacchaeus gives half (Luke 19:8). Joseph of Arimathea retains enough wealth to own a tomb. The instruction to the rich young ruler was diagnostic — it named the specific thing he could not release, and that revealed where his actual lord was.

The principle generalizes: whatever you cannot release for the Kingdom is functioning as your lord. For most people in wealthy cultures, that thing is money.

## The Parable of the Rich Fool (Luke 12:13-21)

A man interrupts Jesus's teaching to ask him to arbitrate an inheritance dispute. Jesus refuses and instead tells a parable. A rich man's land produces abundantly. He decides to tear down his barns and build bigger ones, and says to his soul, "Soul, you have ample goods laid up for many years; relax, eat, drink, be merry."

God responds: "Fool! This night your soul is required of you, and the things you have prepared, whose will they be?"

Jesus's summary in verse 21: "So is the one who lays up treasure for himself and is not rich toward God."

The rich fool's sin is not wealth. It is the assumption that accumulation secures the soul. The Greek *aphrōn* — fool — is the same word the Septuagint uses in Psalms for the one who says in his heart there is no God. The fool's atheism is functional, not stated. He builds barns as if God will not call.

## The Widow's Mite (Mark 12:41-44)

Jesus watches people give at the temple treasury. Many rich people give large sums. A poor widow gives two small copper coins (*lepta* — the smallest denomination in circulation).

Jesus calls his disciples: "Truly, I say to you, this poor widow has put in more than all those who are contributing to the offering box. For they all contributed out of their abundance, but she out of her poverty has put in everything she had, all she had to live on."

The economic principle Jesus teaches is that the proportion of what is given relative to what is retained matters more than the absolute amount. Generosity is measured against capacity. A small gift from someone with nothing weighs more in Kingdom economics than a large gift from someone with abundance left over.

## The Parable of the Unjust Steward (Luke 16:1-13)

This is the strangest of Jesus's money parables. A manager about to be fired discounts his master's debtors to make friends who will take him in after his dismissal. The master commends the dishonest manager "for his shrewdness" (16:8).

The conclusion in verse 9: "Make friends for yourselves by means of unrighteous wealth, so that when it fails they may receive you into the eternal dwellings."

Jesus is not endorsing dishonesty. He is making a point about deployment. The unjust steward used money strategically with an eye to his future. Most people in the Kingdom fail to use money strategically with an eye to their future. The contrast is between worldly people who are shrewd about temporary security and Kingdom people who should be shrewd about eternal welcome.

Verse 13 returns to the mammon theme: "You cannot serve God and money."

## The Parable of the Talents (Matthew 25:14-30)

A master entrusts his servants with talents (a *talanton* was a massive sum — roughly 20 years of a day-laborer's wages). Two double their talents. One buries his and returns it intact. The buryer is condemned not for losing the money but for refusing to deploy it.

The Kingdom economic principle is faithfulness with risk, not protection through inaction. Resources are entrusted for deployment. Refusing to deploy out of fear is the failure. (Detailed exegesis is coming in [a dedicated post on the talents](/2027/01/31/parable-of-the-talents-explained/).)

## What Holds All of It Together

Money in Jesus's teaching is a rival lord (Matthew 6:24); a heart-direction signal (Matthew 6:21); a diagnostic of actual allegiance (the rich young ruler); a false security against death (the rich fool); measured by proportion to capacity (the widow); to be deployed strategically for the Kingdom (the steward); and entrusted for faithful investment (the talents).

What Jesus does not teach: that poverty is inherently virtuous, that wealth is inherently sinful, that the goal is austerity for its own sake, or that giving an amount guarantees blessing.

The unifying principle is mastery. Money will serve, or it will rule. There is no third option. (See [the narrow path teaching](/true-teachings/2026/05/12/narrow-path-matthew-7/) for how this connects to the broader cost of discipleship.)

The wealthy disciple gives like Zacchaeus. The poor disciple gives like the widow. Both are inside the Kingdom because in both cases the money is downstream of the lord.
