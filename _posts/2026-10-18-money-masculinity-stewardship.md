"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also." — Matthew 6:21

Jesus said this in the middle of a passage about money. He had just told his hearers not to store up treasures on earth, but in heaven. Then he gave the reason: your heart goes where your treasure goes. The order of cause and effect is not what most men assume. The heart does not direct the money. The money directs the heart.

This is one of the most underappreciated facts about money in the Christian life. Where you put your money is not just a reflection of what you care about. It is a forming of what you will care about. The man who pours his money into status, comfort, and accumulation will, over time, care more about status, comfort, and accumulation. The man who pours his money into the Kingdom, his family, and generous causes will care more about those things. The money makes the heart.

This is why money is territory. Whoever controls how it moves controls a significant portion of what the man becomes.

## Money in Scripture: A Brief Survey

The Bible is not coy about money. There are over 2,000 verses on the subject — more than on faith and prayer combined. Several themes run through them all.

**Money is a stewardship, not a possession.** Psalm 24:1 — "The earth is the Lord's and the fullness thereof." The man does not own his money. He manages it on behalf of the one who owns everything. The parable of the talents in Matthew 25 makes this concrete: each servant is given a portion proportional to his ability, and each is evaluated on what he did with what he was given. The owner is not the steward. The steward will give an account.

**The love of money is a root of evils.** 1 Timothy 6:10 is specific — not money itself, but the love of money. The man who has organized his life around acquisition is on a path that ends badly. Verse 9 names it: those who desire to be rich "fall into temptation, into a snare, into many senseless and harmful desires that plunge people into ruin and destruction."

**Generosity is commanded.** Not suggested. Commanded. Deuteronomy 15:7-11. Proverbs 11:24-25. 2 Corinthians 9:6-15. The man who is not generous with money is not yet living as a Kingdom man, regardless of his theology.

**Debt is warned against.** Proverbs 22:7 names it bluntly — the borrower is slave to the lender. Romans 13:8 — owe no one anything except love. The Bible does not call all debt sin, but it consistently warns that debt is captivity, and captivity restricts what the man can do.

## The Four Functions of a Man's Money

A man's money does four things. Each one needs to be operating, or the system is off.

### 1. Provide

Provision is the floor. 1 Timothy 5:8 — "If anyone does not provide for his relatives, and especially for members of his household, he has denied the faith and is worse than an unbeliever." Provision is not negotiable. Food, shelter, clothing, education, healthcare — the man takes responsibility for these. Not alone, necessarily, but as the one ultimately responsible.

### 2. Give

Generosity is the next layer. The Old Testament tithe was 10 percent as a baseline. The New Testament does not lower it — it raises the bar by removing the cap. Give cheerfully. Give sacrificially. Give in a way that actually costs something. A man who tips ten percent and calls it stewardship is missing the discipline. Real generosity hurts a little. That is part of how it forms the heart.

Start with 10 percent if you are not currently giving. Build toward more as your income grows. The trajectory should be that the percentage rises with income, not falls.

### 3. Save

Saving is wisdom. Proverbs 21:20 — "Precious treasure and oil are in a wise man's dwelling, but a foolish man devours it." The man who saves nothing is one bad month from crisis. The man who saves something has margin for emergency, opportunity, and generosity at scale.

The traps in saving are two: hoarding (Luke 12 — the rich fool) and trusting in the savings (1 Timothy 6:17 — "set their hopes not on the uncertainty of riches but on God"). Save with open hands. The savings are not your security. God is your security. The savings are a tool he gives you to use wisely.

A practical floor: three to six months of expenses in an emergency fund. Beyond that, save for known future needs — retirement, kids' education, generational investment — but do not stockpile beyond what you can defend as stewardship.

### 4. Spend

Spending is the leftover. What is left after providing, giving, and saving — this is what you can spend on the life you are building. Spend with intention. Buy what serves the mission. Avoid the consumer treadmill that turns income into more and more obligations.

Most men have the order wrong. They spend first, save what is left, give what is left after that, and the providing is the only category that gets the discipline. Reverse the order. Give first. Save second. Provide third. Spend fourth. The financial life works differently in this order.

## The Tell of Generosity

Generosity is the tell. It is the single most reliable indicator of a man's relationship to his money — and therefore to God.

The miser cannot give because he believes his money is his security. The status-spender cannot give because he needs the money to maintain the image. The Kingdom man gives because he knows the money is not his, the security is not in the money, and the image is not what he is building.

You can tell a man is free of money by how he handles it. He gives without flinching. He spends without anxiety. He saves without hoarding. He does not check his accounts compulsively. He does not panic over a bad month. The money does what it is supposed to do — provides, builds, blesses — without owning him.

This is what Jesus called the freedom from mammon (Matthew 6:24). The man who cannot serve God and money has to choose. The man who has chosen God is free to use money — and the use looks like generosity, prudence, and peace.

## Debt as Captivity

Most men in the West are in debt they did not have to take on. Consumer debt — credit cards, financed cars, financed vacations, financed lifestyle — is the most common form. This kind of debt is captivity. It puts the man in a position where he cannot say yes to what God might call him to, because his monthly obligations are dictating his options.

The fix is not glamorous. It is methodical.

- Stop adding to the pile. No new debt.
- List every debt by interest rate. Pay minimums on all, attack the highest interest first.
- Build the emergency fund alongside debt payoff, so a single emergency does not restart the cycle.
- Lower the lifestyle until the debt is gone. The temporary discomfort is the price of freedom.

Strategic debt — a reasonable mortgage, possibly a business loan for productive purposes — is a different category. But even here, Scripture's caution applies. The borrower is still a slave to the lender. Carry the minimum that gets the job done. Pay it down faster than required when you can.

A man without consumer debt has options. He can change jobs. He can give generously. He can take a year to focus on something different. He can say yes to the call he hears. The man buried in debt cannot. He is owned.

## What This Builds

A man who handles money this way for twenty years has built something. He has provided faithfully. He has given consistently — likely tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars to Kingdom causes over a working lifetime. He has saved with wisdom and used the savings for opportunity and emergency. He has stayed out of captivity.

More importantly, he has discipled his heart through his money. The discipline of generous, faithful stewardship has formed him over decades. He is not the same man he would have been if he had simply spent. He has been trained.

This is the [warrior mindset](/2026/05/12/christian-warrior-mindset/) applied to one of the most contested arenas of male life. Money is territory. Hold it well. Use it for the Kingdom. Refuse the captivity. Build the generosity. Let your treasure be where your heart should be.
