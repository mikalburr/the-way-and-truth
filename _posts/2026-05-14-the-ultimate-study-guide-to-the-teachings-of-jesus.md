Jesus was not a systematic theologian. He did not write a treatise organized by doctrine. He taught in the middle of life — on hillsides, in synagogues, at dinner tables, on boats, walking between towns. His teaching was responsive, contextual, and relentlessly focused on the Kingdom of God as the organizing reality of human existence.

That said, his teachings cluster around identifiable themes, and those themes can be mapped and studied systematically without losing the texture of how he taught them. This guide organizes his major teaching blocks by theme, identifies the key passages for each, and provides journal questions to drive the teaching from information into application.

This is not a casual reading guide. It is a working document for serious study.

---

## Theme 1: The Kingdom of God

### What It Is
The Kingdom of God (*basileia tou theou*) is the sovereign rule of God — active, present, and breaking into human history in a new way through Jesus. It is the organizing concept of everything Jesus taught.

### Key Passages
- **Mark 1:14-15** — The mission statement: *"The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news."*
- **Matthew 13:1-52** — The parable chapter: mustard seed, leaven, hidden treasure, pearl, dragnet. Seven parables describing the Kingdom's nature and dynamics.
- **Matthew 5:3-12** — The Beatitudes: who already inhabits the Kingdom and why.
- **Luke 17:20-21** — *"The kingdom of God is in your midst."* The Kingdom is not a future place; it is a present reality.
- **John 3:1-8** — To *see* the Kingdom requires being born again (*anothen* — from above).

### Brief Explanation
The Kingdom is not a metaphor for heaven after death or a feeling of spiritual peace. It is the active reign of God, which Jesus announces has arrived in himself. Every ethic, every parable, every demand Jesus makes derives its logic from this Kingdom reality. Understand the Kingdom and everything else organizes around it.

### Journal Questions
1. What did I assume "Kingdom of God" meant before studying this? What is different now?
2. In what area of my life does God's rule feel most absent? What would it look like for Kingdom reality to apply there?
3. Which parable about the Kingdom was most surprising to me? Why?

## Theme 2: Prayer

### What It Is
Prayer, in Jesus's teaching, is not a religious practice or a performance. It is communication between a citizen and their King — honest, dependent, and persistent.

### Key Passages
- **Matthew 6:5-13** — The Lord's Prayer as structural model: pray in secret, not for performance; use this framework, not these exact words.
- **Matthew 7:7-11** — Ask, seek, knock: persistence in prayer matched by a Father who gives good gifts.
- **John 15:7** — *"If you remain in me and my words remain in you, ask whatever you wish, and it will be done for you."* Prayer grounded in abiding.
- **Luke 18:1-8** — The persistent widow: pray and do not give up.
- **John 17** — The High Priestly Prayer: the model of intercession for others.

### Brief Explanation
Jesus's teaching on prayer is simultaneously simpler and more demanding than most practice. Simpler because it is relational — a child speaking to a Father (Matthew 6:9). More demanding because it requires genuine dependence, not performance, and requires alignment with the Kingdom (*"your will be done"* — Matthew 6:10).

### Journal Questions
1. What does my current prayer life reveal about how I actually think about God?
2. Do I pray to be heard by God or to be seen by people? Be honest.
3. What would I ask for if I genuinely believed Matthew 7:7-8?

## Theme 3: Money

### What It Is
Money, in Jesus's teaching, is the most practical test of where your actual allegiance lies — not where you claim it lies.

### Key Passages
- **Matthew 6:19-24** — Do not store up earthly treasure; where your treasure is, your heart is. You cannot serve God and money (*mammon*).
- **Luke 12:13-21** — The parable of the rich fool: *"This is how it will be with whoever stores up things for themselves but is not rich toward God."*
- **Luke 12:32-34** — Sell your possessions and give to the poor. Kingdom generosity is not aspirational; it is practical.
- **Mark 10:17-25** — The rich young ruler: Jesus loved him and told him to sell everything. He walked away grieving.
- **Luke 16:10-13** — *"Whoever can be trusted with very little can also be trusted with much."* Stewardship is a Kingdom metric.

### Brief Explanation
Jesus talked about money more than almost any other subject. His teaching is not that money is evil; it is that money competes with God for the position of ultimate allegiance. The test of where that allegiance actually sits is revealed by what you do with money — specifically, whether you hoard it or deploy it for Kingdom purposes.

### Journal Questions
1. If Jesus reviewed my spending and saving for the last 90 days, what would he conclude about where my heart is?
2. What would I do differently if I genuinely believed the Kingdom economy described in Luke 12:32-34?
3. Have I walked away from a Kingdom demand because of what it would cost financially?

## Theme 4: Relationships

### What It Is
Relationships, in Jesus's teaching, are the primary arena where Kingdom ethics are tested. Love of neighbor and love of enemy are not abstractions; they are measured in specific relationships with specific people.

### Key Passages
- **Matthew 5:43-48** — Love your enemies. Pray for those who persecute you. This is the hallmark of Kingdom citizenship.
- **Matthew 18:15-17** — Direct confrontation of a brother who has sinned against you. Kingdom community requires honest engagement.
- **Matthew 18:21-22** — Forgive seventy-seven times (or seventy times seven). Forgiveness is not a cap system.
- **Luke 10:25-37** — The Good Samaritan: your neighbor is the person in front of you, regardless of group membership.
- **John 13:34-35** — *"Love one another as I have loved you."* The new commandment that defines Kingdom community.

### Brief Explanation
The most demanding relational command Jesus gives — love of enemies — is also the most diagnostic. Your response to people who have harmed you reveals more about your actual Kingdom alignment than your response to people who are easy to love.

### Journal Questions
1. Who is the enemy I am most reluctant to pray for? What would it mean to genuinely love them?
2. Is there a relationship I have avoided honest confrontation in, using grace as a cover for conflict avoidance?
3. What does "as I have loved you" actually look like in my most difficult current relationship?

## Theme 5: Suffering

### What It Is
Suffering, in Jesus's teaching, is not an anomaly or a sign of God's absence. It is the expected experience of Kingdom citizens in a world that is not yet fully aligned with the Kingdom.

### Key Passages
- **Matthew 5:10-12** — Blessed are those persecuted for righteousness. Rejoice and be glad. This is not passive acceptance; it is active reframing.
- **John 15:18-21** — *"If the world hates you, keep in mind that it hated me first."* Suffering is relational; it comes from alignment with Jesus in a world that rejected him.
- **Matthew 16:24-25** — *"Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me."* The cross is not a metaphor for inconvenience.
- **John 16:33** — *"In this world you will have trouble. But take heart! I have overcome the world."* Certainty of suffering and certainty of ultimate victory.

### Brief Explanation
Jesus never promises that following him eliminates suffering. He promises companionship in it (Matthew 28:20), a framework for it (the cross as the path through death to life), and ultimate vindication (the resurrection). The framework is what makes suffering bearable — it is not random or meaningless, but participates in the same pattern as Jesus's own suffering.

### Journal Questions
1. What suffering am I currently interpreting as evidence that God is absent or failing me?
2. How does John 16:33 change my posture toward that suffering?
3. What does it mean, in practice, to "take up your cross" in my current circumstances?

## Theme 6: Identity

### What It Is
Identity, in Jesus's teaching, is established by the Father, not by performance, achievement, ethnicity, or religious status.

### Key Passages
- **Matthew 5:13-14** — *"You are the salt of the earth... You are the light of the world."* Present tense declarations of identity, not aspirations.
- **John 1:12** — *"To all who received him, to those who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God."*
- **John 15:15** — *"I no longer call you servants... Instead, I have called you friends."* Relationship redefined.
- **Matthew 6:26** — *"Look at the birds of the air... Are you not much more valuable than they?"* Your value is not a function of productivity.
- **Luke 15:11-32** — The prodigal son: the father restores full sonship to the one who had forfeited it by his own choices.

### Brief Explanation
Modern men construct identity through achievement, status, relationship, and performance. Jesus consistently undermined these foundations and replaced them with a single one: you are a child of the Father, and that identity is not earned or lost by performance. The prodigal son received the robe, ring, and sandals — full restoration of status — before he completed his repentance speech.

### Journal Questions
1. Where am I deriving my sense of identity right now? Is it from what Jesus says about me or from what I have achieved?
2. How would my daily behavior change if I genuinely believed I was a "friend" of Jesus (John 15:15)?
3. What would "full restoration of sonship" look like in a specific area where I feel disqualified?

## Theme 7: The Enemy

### What It Is
Jesus taught the reality of spiritual opposition — a personal adversary (the devil, Satan, the enemy of souls) who operates through deception, accusation, and temptation.

### Key Passages
- **Matthew 4:1-11** — The temptation of Jesus. Three temptations, each answered with Scripture. The model for resisting the enemy.
- **John 8:44** — *"He was a murderer from the beginning, not holding to the truth, for there is no truth in him. When he lies, he speaks his native language."* The enemy's primary weapon is deception.
- **Luke 10:18-19** — *"I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven... I have given you authority to trample on snakes and scorpions."* Authority granted to Kingdom citizens.
- **John 10:10** — *"The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy; I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full."* The contrast between Kingdom life and the enemy's agenda.

### Brief Explanation
Jesus did not teach about the enemy as a theological abstraction. He encountered spiritual opposition directly (the temptations, the demonized individuals he healed), he named it clearly, and he equipped his disciples with authority over it (Luke 10:17-19). The enemy's primary tactic, per John 8:44, is deception — which means the primary defense is truth: truth about God, truth about identity, truth about reality as Jesus defines it.

### Journal Questions
1. What are the consistent lies I believe about myself, God, or my circumstances? Where might those be coming from?
2. How does Jesus's model in Matthew 4 (responding to temptation with specific Scripture) apply to my most persistent patterns of failure?
3. What would it mean, practically, to exercise the authority Jesus gave in Luke 10:19?

## Recommended Reading Order

1. Mark (entire) — immediate, action-based, chronological
2. Matthew 5-7 (Sermon on the Mount) — core ethics
3. Matthew 13 (parables of the Kingdom) — Kingdom dynamics
4. Luke 10-19 (travel narrative) — applied teaching
5. John 14-17 (farewell discourse) — intimate, deep, essential
6. Luke 15 (three parables of the lost) — Kingdom grace

Repeat the sequence before adding any secondary reading. The Gospels reward returning.

## After Each Section: The Two Questions

Before closing your Bible or notebook after any study session, write answers to these two questions:

1. **What is one thing Jesus said that I did not fully believe today before I read it?**
2. **What is one specific action this passage is calling me to take in the next 24 hours?**

The gap between what you believe and what Jesus said is where transformation happens. The gap between what you know and what you do is where it is often blocked. These two questions close both gaps, one session at a time.
