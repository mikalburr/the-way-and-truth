The honest answer is: a lifetime.

But that answer alone is not useful without the specifics — what happens in the first month, what happens in the first year, what is different at five years, and what remains perpetually in process.

Understanding Jesus's teachings is not like understanding a textbook. A textbook has a fixed amount of content; you can measure your progress toward completing it. Jesus's teachings are not a closed system with finite content. They are a person's words — and like any relationship with a person, the depth of understanding grows indefinitely as you grow in capacity to receive it.

He said as much. John 16:12: *"I have much more to say to you, more than you can now bear."*

There is more than you can currently receive. That is not a discouragement. It is a description of the scope of what you are engaging with.

## The Process: How It Actually Unfolds

The process of genuine engagement with Jesus's teachings follows a recognizable pattern:

**Confusion → Question → Clarity → New Confusion at Deeper Level**

This is not a spiral of permanent frustration. It is the natural epistemology of encountering a teacher who is consistently ahead of your current capacity. Each cycle of confusion-to-clarity produces a deeper foundation from which the next level of confusion becomes productive.

The disciples experienced this. Mark 9:32: *"But they did not understand what he meant and were afraid to ask him."* Luke 18:34: *"The disciples did not understand any of this."* John 12:16: *"At first his disciples did not understand all this. Only after Jesus was glorified did they realize..."*

These are people who were with him in person. They cycled through confusion and clarity multiple times across three years, and their deepest clarity came only after the resurrection and the coming of the Spirit.

If you are confused, you are in good company. The question is what you do with the confusion — whether you sit with it productively or use it as a reason to step back from engagement.

## What to Expect at 30 Days

If you have genuinely read the Gospels straight through for the first time — or re-read them as a primary source rather than a filtered secondary encounter — 30 days produces primarily disorientation.

The good kind.

You have discovered that Jesus said things you did not know he said. The demands are more total than you understood. The Kingdom frame changes the meaning of passages you thought you knew. Some famous verses mean something different in context.

You have also discovered passages you cannot yet make sense of — not because they are obscure, but because they are making demands on areas of your life you have not yet brought into the inquiry.

At 30 days, you have a list of questions you did not have before. That is the most valuable output of the first month. The questions are the beginning of genuine understanding.

What you should not expect at 30 days: resolution, settled theology, confident answers to the major questions. None of that. What you should expect: a much more accurate picture of what you don't yet understand, which is a significant improvement over the false confidence of never having engaged seriously.

## What to Expect at 1 Year

A year of consistent, daily engagement with Jesus's teachings — using the minimum viable practice from the previous article (one passage, read three times, one sentence of application) — produces several recognizable changes.

**A working Kingdom framework.** You have encountered the phrase "Kingdom of God" often enough, in enough contexts, to have a functional understanding of what Jesus meant by it. This is not academic knowledge; it is a working lens that you carry into daily life and that begins to reorder how you read circumstances.

**A different relationship with confusion.** You have been confused and stayed in the text and watched clarity emerge enough times that you trust the process. You no longer treat confusion as a sign that you're doing something wrong. You recognize it as the precursor to the next level of understanding.

**The first major application results.** If you have genuinely applied specific teachings for extended periods — Matthew 5:44 for a month, Matthew 6:33 for a month, John 15:4 as a structural practice — you have seen results. The interior posture has shifted in measurable ways. The relationships have changed. The anxiety management has a different texture.

**The disorientation about your prior understanding.** At one year, you are likely in the uncomfortable position of realizing that significant portions of what you understood Christianity to be were filtered versions of something more demanding and more alive. This is productive disorientation — it is the old wineskin giving way to new wine (Mark 2:22). But it is uncomfortable.

What you should not expect at 1 year: complete coherence, settled answers to every theological question, absence of confusion. The complexity grows as the understanding grows. That is correct.

## What to Expect at 5 Years

Five years of consistent, serious engagement with Jesus's teachings produces a depth of familiarity that is qualitatively different from what the earlier stages produce.

**His voice is recognizable.** You know how he talks — the patterns of his teaching, the way he uses questions, the structure of his parables, the escalation pattern in the antitheses of the Sermon on the Mount. When you read a new commentary or encounter someone's interpretation, you have enough primary source familiarity to evaluate whether it sounds like Jesus or like someone's construction of Jesus.

**The teachings are integrated.** At the beginning, the teachings feel like discrete instructions. At five years, they are part of a coherent whole — each teaching connects to the Kingdom framework, which connects to his identity, which connects to his understanding of the Father, which connects back to the ethics. The system becomes visible.

**The demands have reorganized your life.** Not completely — there are always areas that have not yet come under the Kingdom's restructuring. But the areas that have been genuinely engaged look different than they did at the start. The prayer is different. Some relationships are different. The relationship with money is different. The response to suffering is different.

**The depth of what remains is more visible.** At five years, you see more clearly how much remains. This is not discouragement. It is the natural result of having a higher resolution picture of the territory. The beginner cannot see what they don't know. At five years, you can see the horizon more clearly — which reveals how far there is still to go.

## The Goal: Alignment, Not Comprehension

The goal of engaging Jesus's teachings is not to eventually achieve full comprehension. The goal is increasing alignment — an ever-closer correspondence between the reality he describes and the reality you are living.

John 8:31-32: *"If you hold to my teaching, you are really my disciples. Then you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free."*

The knowing and the freedom are not the preconditions for holding. They are the results. You hold — remain — and the knowing and freedom follow. And the knowing deepens as the holding continues.

This is the architecture of lifelong engagement. You do not graduate from the study of Jesus's teachings. You deepen within it. Each year of serious engagement equips you to receive more of what he said in John 16:12: *"I have much more to say to you, more than you can now bear."*

The progressive capacity to bear more — that is what five years, ten years, a lifetime of genuine engagement produces. Not completion. Increasing capacity. Increasing alignment. Increasing freedom.

The disciples had three years with Jesus in person, a dramatic resurrection encounter, the descent of the Spirit at Pentecost, and a lifetime of apostolic ministry — and Paul wrote in Philippians 3:12: *"Not that I have already obtained all this, or have already arrived at my goal, but I press on to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me."*

If Paul, at the height of his apostolic ministry, was still pressing on — you have good company in the journey.

Press on.
