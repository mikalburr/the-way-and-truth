There is a version of Christian piety that treats the body with something close to contempt. The body is the problem — the source of temptation, the location of weakness, the thing that must be endured until we get the spiritual version of ourselves. Physical training, in this framework, is vanity at worst and irrelevance at best. Real Christians focus on the spiritual things.

This is a gnostic idea, not a biblical one. And it has produced a generation of Kingdom men who are spiritually engaged and physically neglected — men who pray hard and live in bodies they have not disciplined, stewarded, or maintained with anything like the intentionality they bring to their theology.

The Bible has a different view. Let me show you.

---

## The Temple Argument: 1 Corinthians 6:19-20

Paul writes: "Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your bodies."

This is not primarily about sexual purity, though Paul uses it in that context. The principle is broader and more demanding than a single application: your body is the residence of the Holy Spirit. It is not your property to neglect. It was purchased. You are a steward of it, not an owner.

Honor God with your bodies. That is an active, positive command — not merely "avoid dishonoring." The word honor implies investment, care, intentional maintenance, and active stewardship.

What does it mean to honor God with a body that is chronically undertrained, overfed, under-rested, and governed by appetite rather than discipline? The honest answer: it does not honor God. It mismanages what he entrusted to you.

This is not an argument for aesthetic perfection or competitive athletic achievement. It is an argument for treating the body as what it is — the temple of the Spirit, the physical infrastructure through which your Kingdom assignment is executed — with the same intentionality you would bring to maintaining any structure of genuine importance.

---

## The Athletic Metaphor: 1 Corinthians 9:24-27

Paul does not merely imply physical discipline. He explicitly uses athletic training as the model for Kingdom development:

"Do you not know that in a race all the runners run, but only one gets the prize? Run in such a way as to get the prize. Everyone who competes in the games goes into strict training. They do it to get a crown that will not last, but we do it to get a crown that will last forever. Therefore I do not run like someone running aimlessly; I do not fight like a boxer beating the air. No, I strike a blow to my body and make it my slave so that after I have preached to others, I myself will not be disqualified for the prize."

Several things are happening in this passage that deserve careful attention.

**"Run in such a way as to get the prize."** Not to participate. Not to finish. To win. Paul is not arguing for the spiritual participation trophy. He is arguing for the full-effort posture of a man competing to win — with all the preparation, intensity, and discipline that entails.

**"Everyone who competes in the games goes into strict training."** Strict. That word (*egkrateuomai* — to exercise self-control, to be disciplined) is not moderate. It describes the total governance of diet, sleep, training, and lifestyle that ancient athletes employed in preparation for competition. Paul holds this up as the model for Kingdom development.

**"I strike a blow to my body and make it my slave."** This is Paul describing the governance of physical appetite through deliberate discipline. The body is not the enemy — but it is not the master either. The Kingdom man governs his body; his body does not govern him. Physical training is one of the primary mechanisms through which that governance is built and maintained.

---

## The Spiritual Benefit of Physical Discipline

The connection between physical discipline and spiritual formation is not merely metaphorical. It is structural.

**Physical discipline builds tolerance for discomfort.** The spiritual life requires the capacity to stay in things that are uncomfortable: sustained prayer when nothing seems to be happening, fasting when the appetite protests, sitting with grief or difficulty without reaching for numbing comfort. These require a tolerance for discomfort that must be trained — and physical training is one of the most direct ways to train it.

The man who has learned to push through the last set, to complete the run when his body demands he stop, to rise early when his body demands he stay down — that man has built a concrete, physical foundation for the same capacity in the spiritual domain.

**Physical discipline provides evidence that the will can override the flesh.** This is enormously important for the man who has a history of being governed by his appetites. Every time you train when you do not feel like it, you are not just building physical capacity. You are building proof — evidence that the will is capable of overriding what the flesh demands. That proof becomes the internal testimony you draw from when the flesh makes demands in spiritual, relational, and moral domains.

**Governing one appetite trains governance of all appetites.** The man who governs his food and his physical training is building the same capacity that governs his sexual appetite, his anger, his tendency toward passive comfort, and his capacity for sustained Kingdom mission. These are not separate skills. They are expressions of the same underlying discipline of the will.

---

## What Kingdom Training Actually Looks Like

Kingdom training is not the same as bodybuilder training, competitive athletics, or the pursuit of aesthetic perfection. It has a different purpose, which gives it a different shape.

**The purpose is stewardship, not vanity.** You are not training to look impressive. You are training to maintain the temple and build the capacity for mission. That reorientation changes what you optimize for: energy, endurance, functional strength, and the governance of appetite — not aesthetic metrics.

**Consistency over intensity.** The Kingdom man's training is built on consistency — showing up reliably, maintaining the discipline over years, not on peak performances that collapse under the pressure of real life. Three days a week, done consistently for five years, produces more functional capacity than one intense cycle per year with long gaps between.

**The discipline of the training matters more than the specific training.** Running, lifting weights, martial arts, swimming, manual labor — the specific modality matters less than whether you are doing it consistently and governing your body in the process. Find what you will actually sustain. Do that. Maintain it for years.

**Appetite governance is part of the training.** What you eat, how you eat, and whether you fast are not separate from physical training — they are the same discipline applied to a different domain. The man who trains his body but does not govern his eating has maintained one part of the temple while neglecting another.

---

## A Direct Word to Men Who Have Let This Go

If you have been using "the body is not the focus of the faith" as permission to neglect what God entrusted to you — that ends today.

Not with guilt. Not with an unsustainable overhaul. With one honest commitment: I am going to steward this body as the temple it is.

One week, three sessions, thirty minutes each. Walk, run, lift — whatever your current condition supports. Do it before you talk yourself out of it. Do it as an act of stewardship, not performance. Do it because the King who lives in you deserves a maintained temple.

You will be surprised how quickly physical discipline affects everything else: your prayer life, your clarity, your capacity for sustained focus, and your sense of yourself as a man who governs his life rather than being governed by it.

The body matters. Paul knew it. The Spirit lives in yours. Take care of it.
