The most dangerous theological errors are not made by people who reject Jesus. They are made by people who claim to follow him while systematically misreading what he said. Jesus himself warned about this with precision in Matthew 7:21-23.

These are the mistakes that most consistently undermine genuine discipleship in people who are already committed Christians.

## Mistake 1: Using Jesus's Words to Justify Existing Preferences

The human capacity for self-deception is extraordinary. We are capable of reading any text and finding, with effort, support for what we already wanted to do. The Gospels are not immune to this.

The most common form of this mistake: finding the passages that affirm your existing way of life and ignoring the passages that challenge it, while presenting the selected passages as your theology.

A person who wants to justify wealth accumulation gravitates toward the passages where Jesus dines with wealthy people (Luke 19:1-10 — Zacchaeus) and downplays the passages where he commands divestiture (Mark 10:17-25, Luke 12:33-34). A person who wants to justify withdrawal from difficult relationships gravitates toward Matthew 10:14 (*"shake the dust off your feet"*) and ignores Matthew 18:15-17's requirements for persistent, structured engagement with a sinning brother.

The correction is not comfortable: read the passages that make you most uncomfortable first. Those are the ones most likely to represent what Jesus was actually calling you to, precisely because your resistance to them reveals where your actual allegiance is.

Luke 6:46 cuts to the core: *"Why do you call me, 'Lord, Lord,' and do not do what I say?"* The question assumes that most people who call him Lord are not doing what he says. He was right about his own audience. He is right about the contemporary one.

## Mistake 2: Applying the Easy Parts Systematically and Skipping the Hard Parts

This mistake is subtler than outright rejection. It looks like genuine discipleship from the outside — and sometimes from the inside — because you are applying some of the teachings.

A common pattern: deep engagement with the comforting aspects of Jesus's teaching (John 3:16, the Beatitudes as comfort, his promises about prayer) combined with consistent avoidance of the demanding aspects (love of enemies, sell your possessions, take up your cross, forgive as you have been forgiven).

The selective application produces a distorted portrait of Jesus — one who is primarily a source of comfort, reassurance, and grace, with the demanding elements present but perpetually deferred. I'll work on loving enemies when I've matured more. I'll address the possessions question when my financial situation is more stable. I'll take up my cross when I understand better what that means.

These deferrals are indefinite. The maturity never arrives, the financial situation never stabilizes enough, the understanding never becomes sufficient to require action.

Jesus anticipated this. Luke 9:59-62 records three would-be disciples who each wanted to delay following until something was resolved first. Jesus refused every delay without exception. The Kingdom admitsno preparation period.

The hard parts of the teaching are not electives for advanced disciples. They are the content of what it means to follow Jesus. The easy parts are real but incomplete. A discipleship that only ever engages the easy parts is not following Jesus — it is following a construction of Jesus assembled from the passages you prefer.

## Mistake 3: Treating the Beatitudes as Comfort Rather Than Challenge

Matthew 5:3-12, the Beatitudes, are regularly preached as words of comfort for the struggling. You're going through a hard time? Blessed are those who mourn. You feel spiritually inadequate? Blessed are the poor in spirit.

This reading is not entirely wrong — the Beatitudes do offer perspective to people in difficult circumstances. But it is seriously incomplete, and it misses the primary function of the passage.

The Beatitudes are descriptions of Kingdom citizenship, not consolations for the struggling. They describe the character of people who are actually living in Kingdom reality — and the character is not what most people would choose as a description of the successful, the godly, or the spiritually mature.

*Blessed are the poor in spirit* — those who have nothing to offer spiritually, who are at the bottom of every religious hierarchy.

*Blessed are the meek* — the Greek *praus* means power under control, not weakness, but it is the opposite of the assertive, self-promoting personality that achieves success in most human systems.

*Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness* — not those who are successful, celebrated, or comfortable.

Read as a challenge, the Beatitudes demand self-examination: do I resemble this? Do I carry the character of someone who has been shaped by Kingdom reality? Or am I pursuing the opposite qualities — spiritual confidence, assertive self-promotion, social acceptance — and calling it blessed?

Read as comfort only, the Beatitudes produce passive reassurance. Read as challenge, they produce active transformation. Jesus intended the latter.

## Mistake 4: Confusing Jesus's Teaching on Forgiveness With Approval of Behavior

Matthew 18:21-22: Peter asks how many times he should forgive a brother who sins against him — up to seven times? Jesus's response: *"Not seven times, but seventy-seven times"* (or "seventy times seven" — the Greek *hebdomekontakis hepta* can be read either way, but the point is unlimited forgiveness).

This teaching is frequently applied to mean: you must continue to accept harmful behavior from people who have wronged you without consequence or adjustment, because forgiveness requires it.

This is not what Jesus taught.

Forgiveness is the release of the debt of offense — choosing not to hold it against the person, not to nurse resentment, not to seek revenge. It is an internal posture and a relational choice that can be made regardless of the other person's repentance or change of behavior.

Forgiveness does not require trust. Trust is earned by changed behavior. Forgiveness precedes behavioral change and does not require it. You can forgive someone for what they did to you and simultaneously choose not to place yourself in a position where they can do it again.

Matthew 18:15-17 demonstrates this. The same passage that contextualizes the seventy-seven times forgiveness teaching begins with a process for addressing a brother who sins against you: go to him personally, then take witnesses, then bring it to the community. This is a structured process for addressing wrong behavior — not suppressing it in the name of grace.

Forgiveness and accountability are not opposites. Both are present in Jesus's teaching. Confusing them leads either to enabling harmful behavior (if forgiveness is misread as acceptance) or to bitterness (if the demand for forgiveness seems impossible without behavioral change from the offender).

## Mistake 5: Using the Cross to Make the Sermon on the Mount Optional

This is the most theologically sophisticated mistake on this list, and the one most likely to be made by people with serious theological training.

The argument runs: Jesus obeyed the law perfectly on our behalf. His righteousness is imputed to us through faith. Therefore the ethical demands of the Sermon on the Mount have been met by him, and we receive the benefit of that obedience by faith, not by attempting to meet those demands ourselves. Attempting to meet the demands of the Sermon on the Mount through effort is works-righteousness.

This argument is internally coherent. It also systematically displaces the Sermon on the Mount from its role in discipleship — the demands of the Sermon become, in practice, optional.

There are two problems with this reading.

First, it is not what Jesus said the Sermon was. He introduced it with declarations about the character of Kingdom citizens (the Beatitudes) and closed it with a direct command: *"everyone who hears these words of mine and puts them into practice"* (Matthew 7:24). The emphasis is on practice. Jesus did not say: "everyone who trusts that I have practiced these things on their behalf." He said: hear and practice.

Second, Paul himself, who is the primary source for the imputed righteousness doctrine, did not use it to make ethical demands optional. Galatians 5:13-14: *"You, my brothers and sisters, were called to be free. But do not use your freedom to indulge the flesh; rather, serve one another humbly in love. For the entire law is fulfilled in keeping this one command: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.'"* The freedom of grace does not eliminate ethical demand; it changes the energy source from obligation to love.

The cross does not make the Sermon on the Mount optional. The cross makes the Sermon on the Mount possible. The Spirit who is given because of the cross is the one who produces Kingdom character from the inside out — which is exactly what the Sermon describes.

---

Each of these mistakes has in common the same underlying problem: reading Jesus through the lens of what is comfortable, manageable, or consistent with existing preferences, rather than letting Jesus speak plainly and then responding to what he actually said.

The correction is always the same: return to the text. Read it without commentary. Ask: what is Jesus actually saying here? What would I do if this were literally true? Then decide honestly whether you are willing to do that.

That honest confrontation with the actual teaching of Jesus — not the managed, softened, tradition-filtered version — is where genuine discipleship begins.
