Most Christians can define grace in four words: getting what you don't deserve. This is accurate as far as it goes. It does not go far enough. The biblical word is richer, the concept is more demanding, and the misunderstanding of grace has produced as much damage in the church as the outright rejection of it.

A man who understands grace correctly will not use it as license or lean on it as excuse. He will be undone by it — and remade. That is what it is for.

## The Greek Word

Grace in the New Testament is *charis*. The word appears over 150 times in the New Testament and carries a semantic range that the English word "grace" barely captures.

*Charis* in the Greek world meant:
- **Favor** — the goodwill of one who has the power to benefit
- **Gift** — something given freely, without obligation or repayment
- **Gratitude** — the appropriate response to favor received (the verb *eucharisteo* — "to give thanks" — comes from the same root)
- **Empowerment** — the capacity given from above to do what could not otherwise be done

This is not a sentimental word. It is a royal word. *Charis* is the favor that flows from a king — not earned, not owed, but freely given and transformatively powerful. When Paul opens every letter with "grace to you" (*charis hymin*), he is not offering a pleasantry. He is pronouncing an active benefit from the highest authority in the universe.

## Grace in John 1

The opening of John's Gospel situates grace theologically before Paul develops it doctrinally:

> "And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth... For from his fullness we have all received, grace upon grace. For the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ." — John 1:14, 16-17

Three observations:

**Full of grace *and* truth.** Not grace instead of truth. Not truth that overrides grace. Both, together, fully. The grace of Jesus is never divorced from truth, and the truth of Jesus is always carried by grace. The man who wants grace without truth wants permission without reality. The man who wants truth without grace wants law without life. Jesus offers both simultaneously.

**Grace upon grace.** The Greek *charin anti charitos* — grace in exchange for grace, grace upon grace, wave after wave of grace. This is not a one-time favor but a continuous supply. You do not exhaust the available grace. There is no floor beneath which the supply runs out.

**The contrast with Moses.** Law was given through Moses — the standard was revealed. Grace and truth *came* through Jesus Christ — the standard was fulfilled and the power to live it was made available. The law tells you what you must do. Grace does in you what you cannot do for yourself. These are not in competition. They are sequential revelations of the same God.

## Grace in Romans 5-6

Paul's most complete treatment of grace is in Romans, and it is precisely here that he has to guard against misreading:

> "But where sin increased, grace abounded all the more, so that, as sin reigned in death, grace also might reign through righteousness leading to eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord." — Romans 5:20-21

Grace *abounded* — *huperperisseusen* — super-abounded, overflowed. For every sin, there is more grace. This is astonishing. Paul knows exactly what question it will generate, because he asks it himself in the next verse:

> "What shall we say then? Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means! How can we who died to sin still live in it?" — Romans 6:1-2

The misreading of grace as license — "since grace covers it, it doesn't matter what I do" — is not a modern innovation. It is an ancient misunderstanding that Paul addresses directly. His answer is not "you should try harder not to sin." His answer is ontological: "How can we who *died* to sin still live in it?" The man who is truly united with Christ has died to the power of sin. The question of license misunderstands what has already happened.

Romans 6:14 is decisive: "For sin will have no dominion over you, since you are not under law but under grace." Grace is not the absence of standard. Grace is the power that breaks sin's *dominion*. Law commands and leaves you to fail. Grace commands and provides the power to obey. The man under grace is not free from the standard — he has been given what he needs to meet it.

## Grace and Effort: 1 Corinthians 15:10

Paul's most personal statement on grace is in his account of his own apostleship:

> "But by the grace of God I am what I am, and his grace toward me was not in vain. On the contrary, I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I, but the grace of God that is with me." — 1 Corinthians 15:10

Three movements in this verse:
1. "By the grace of God I am what I am" — his identity, his position, his calling: entirely grace.
2. "His grace toward me was not in vain" — he did not waste what he was given. Grace received produced fruit.
3. "I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I, but the grace of God that is with me" — extraordinary effort and complete attribution to grace in the same sentence.

This is the mature understanding of grace: it does not eliminate effort — it produces and empowers effort. The man who uses grace as a reason not to try has not understood what was given. The man who works as if everything depends on him has not understood who is working through him. Paul holds both: maximum effort, total dependence.

## The Error of Cheap Grace

The 20th century German pastor and theologian Dietrich Bonhoeffer, who was eventually executed by the Nazis for his resistance to their regime, wrote with devastating clarity about the misuse of grace in his work *The Cost of Discipleship*:

Bonhoeffer distinguished between "cheap grace" and "costly grace." Cheap grace, as he described it, is grace as doctrine, grace as system — "the preaching of forgiveness without requiring repentance, baptism without church discipline, Communion without confession... Grace without the cross, grace without Jesus Christ, living and incarnate."

Costly grace is the opposite: "It is costly because it costs a man his life, and it is grace because it gives a man the only true life." It is the grace Jesus preached — which always came with a command: "Follow me."

The cheap grace error is common and comfortable: affirm that grace covers your sin, continue living as before, use grace as the ceiling rather than the floor. Bonhoeffer argued that this is not grace at all — it is the abuse of the vocabulary of grace in service of self-indulgence.

Costly grace does not add to grace — it recognizes what grace actually is. A king's favor that transforms the life of the recipient. Not a blanket approval of whatever you were already doing. An active, life-reshaping power.

## Grace as the Ground of Everything Else

The mature understanding of grace is not just "God forgives me when I fail." It is the foundation of the entire Christian life:

- You pray because you have access through grace (Hebrews 4:16 — "let us then with confidence draw near to the throne of grace").
- You serve because grace has made service possible and given you capacity for it (1 Corinthians 15:10).
- You endure hardship because grace is sufficient for every circumstance (2 Corinthians 12:9 — "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness").
- You give generously because you have received generously (2 Corinthians 8:7 — "excel in this act of grace also").

Grace is not the occasional safety net beneath a life lived by your own effort. It is the operating power of every good thing you do, have, and are. Receiving it means acknowledging this constantly — and living from it rather than in spite of it.

"You then, my child, be strengthened by the grace that is in Christ Jesus." — 2 Timothy 2:1

Be strengthened by it. Draw from it. Work from it. That is what grace is for.
