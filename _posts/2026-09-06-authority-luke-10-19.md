Most Christians are unsure whether they have spiritual authority. They have read the verses. They have heard sermons. But when push comes to shove — when something dark actually appears, when prayer hits genuine resistance — they default to either panic or passivity.

Luke 10:19 is the sentence that should settle this. The grant is explicit, the source is named, and the operation is described. The question is whether you actually believe it.

## The Text

> "Behold, I have given you authority to tread on serpents and scorpions, and over all the power of the enemy, and nothing shall hurt you." — Luke 10:19

Jesus says this to the seventy-two disciples who have returned from a mission trip during which they cast out demons in his name. They are excited about it. He is teaching them what just happened — and what is now permanently available.

The grammar is past tense and complete: I *have given*. The grant is not pending. The authority is in their possession.

## Exousia, Not Just Dunamis

The Greek word for "authority" here is *exousia*. This is one of the most important words in the New Testament's vocabulary of Kingdom operation, and it is distinct from the word usually translated "power" — *dunamis*.

*Dunamis* is raw capability, force, power. It is the root of our word "dynamite." It refers to the ability to do something.

*Exousia* is the right to do something. It is delegated authority — the authorization that comes from being sent by someone with the right to send.

A police officer with a badge has *exousia* in his jurisdiction. A much stronger man without a badge has more *dunamis* but no authority. The badge gives the officer the legal right to act on behalf of the state. He does not generate the authority. He carries it because of who deputized him.

Luke 10:19 is *exousia*. Jesus is not saying the disciples have become more spiritually powerful in themselves. He is saying they have been authorized to act on his behalf in the spiritual realm. The *dunamis* belongs to him. The *exousia* — the delegated right — is now theirs.

## Serpents, Scorpions, and the Enemy

The imagery is layered. Serpents and scorpions are literal threats in the ancient Near Eastern landscape, but they are also longstanding symbols of demonic opposition. Psalm 91:13, which Jesus is echoing, uses the same imagery to describe protection from spiritual evil. Genesis 3 frames the serpent as the original adversary.

When Jesus says "over all the power of the enemy," he is removing any ambiguity. The authority extends to every form of demonic opposition. There is no class of darkness exempt from the delegation.

The promise — "nothing shall hurt you" — is not a promise of immunity from all suffering. The same Jesus tells his disciples elsewhere that they will be hated, persecuted, and killed. The promise here is specifically about the enemy's capacity to harm them in the exercise of their commission. While they are doing what he sent them to do, the enemy cannot break through the authority they carry.

## Authority Is Delegated, Not Earned

This is the part most modern Christians miss. They treat spiritual authority as something they earn by spiritual experience, accumulated prayer hours, or proven track record. There is some truth in the experience part — confidence grows with use — but the authority itself is not earned. It is delegated.

The disciples on the mission in Luke 10 were not spiritual giants. They were ordinary disciples sent on a specific mission with specific authority for that mission. The authority worked not because of who they were but because of who sent them.

This is the structure throughout the New Testament. The authority of the believer is exercised "in the name of Jesus" — that is, on the authority of his person and commission — not in the name of the believer's spiritual maturity.

Compare Acts 19:13-16. A group of itinerant exorcists tried to use the name of Jesus as a formula without actually being sent by him. They said, "I adjure you by the Jesus whom Paul proclaims, come out." The demon answered, "Jesus I know, and Paul I recognize, but who are you?" Then the possessed man overpowered them.

The name is not a magic word. It is the operative seal of an actual delegation. When you are genuinely sent and you operate in the name, the authority functions. When you try to use the name without the relationship and commission underneath it, the spiritual realm recognizes the imposture.

## Authority vs. Experience

Most Christians who struggle with this confuse two distinct questions.

Question one: Do I have the authority? That question is answered by the delegation, not by experience. If you are united with Christ, the authority is genuinely yours to exercise.

Question two: Have I learned to use the authority? That question is answered by experience, formation, and practice. Just as a new police officer technically has the legal authority of the badge from day one but has not yet learned how to act effectively, a new believer has real authority but is still learning to exercise it.

The first question is settled. The second is a lifelong practice.

This is also why the believer who is [seated with Christ](/kingdom-mindset/2026/07/12/seated-with-christ-ephesians-2-6/) can exercise authority over the powers and principalities described in Ephesians 6. The seat and the authority are aspects of the same union with Christ.

## How This Actually Operates

In practice, exercising delegated authority looks like this:

**You speak rather than beg.** Jesus did not ask demons to leave; he commanded them. The disciples in Luke 10 did the same. The vocabulary of authority is declarative, not petitionary. When you are confronting darkness in something you have authority over — your own life, the territory God has assigned you, the people you are responsible for — the prayer is often a command rather than a request. "In the name of Jesus, leave."

**You operate from position, not toward it.** You are not trying to climb to a place where you can speak. You are already in the place. The work is to act consistently with where you already are. ([What Is Kingdom Mindset?](/kingdom-mindset/2026/05/12/what-is-kingdom-mindset/) builds out this frame further.)

**You stay within the commission.** The authority is not unlimited. It is specifically the authority of someone sent on the King's business. When you wander outside the commission — pursuing your own agenda, settling personal scores, performing for an audience — the authority does not follow. The badge works on duty, not for personal use.

**You learn through use.** The first time you exercise authority in a real situation, you may feel ridiculous. That is fine. The authority does not depend on your feelings. Over time, the inner conviction catches up to the legal reality.

## The Final Word

Luke 10:19 is not a promise reserved for missionaries or pastors or experts. It is the operating description of how delegated authority works for anyone sent by Christ. The grant is in the past tense. The authority is in your possession. The enemy's power has limits the authority can enforce.

Stop asking whether you have authority. Start learning to exercise the authority you have been given.
