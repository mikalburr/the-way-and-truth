The Sermon on the Mount is the single most important piece of teaching Jesus ever gave. Three chapters in Matthew 5-7. The Beatitudes. The call to love enemies. The Lord's Prayer. The warning about the broad and narrow ways. The parable of the two foundations. If you can only memorize one extended section of Scripture, this is it.

Most Christian men have read it. Few have studied it. Fewer still have built their lives on it. The gap between reading the sermon and obeying it is where most Christian formation either happens or doesn't.

These five books take the sermon seriously as the actual curriculum of Kingdom life. I have read four of them in full and worked with the fifth in segments. Here they are, in the order I recommend them.

---

## 1. The Message of the Sermon on the Mount — John Stott

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Message of the Sermon on the Mount (The Bible Speaks Today)"
  author="John Stott"
  asin="083083360X"
  description="John Stott's careful expository treatment of Matthew 5-7, part of the Bible Speaks Today series. The most balanced combination of exegesis and application available in a single volume."
  verdict="Start here. Stott is the model of pastoral expository preaching, and this volume on the Sermon on the Mount is one of the finest in the BST series."
%}

**What it covers:** Stott works through the entire Sermon on the Mount section by section — the Beatitudes, the salt and light imagery, Jesus's relationship to the law, the six antitheses, the practice of righteousness (giving, prayer, fasting), worldly anxiety, judging, the two ways, the two trees, the two builders. Each section receives careful exegetical treatment and substantive pastoral application.

**Who it's for:** Men who want one balanced introduction to the sermon — exegetically substantive without being academically heavy, pastorally applied without losing scholarly rigor. Stott's voice throughout is measured, careful, and grounded in the actual text.

**What it adds:** Balance. The other treatments on this list go deep in particular directions. Stott covers everything competently. After reading Stott, you have a solid working framework for what the entire sermon is teaching and how to live it.

**One honest note:** Stott is a measured writer. Some readers will want more fire (read Lloyd-Jones) or more philosophical weight (read McKnight). Stott gives you the careful pastoral foundation that everything else builds on.

## 2. Studies in the Sermon on the Mount — D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones

{% include book-card.html
  title="Studies in the Sermon on the Mount (Two Volumes in One)"
  author="D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones"
  asin="0802807313"
  description="The compiled 30+ sermons Lloyd-Jones preached on Matthew 5-7 at Westminster Chapel in London from 1950-1952. The most spiritually penetrating extended treatment of the sermon in print."
  verdict="The deepest pastoral treatment on this list. Read one chapter per week, not per day. This is not a book to power-read. It is a book to be searched by."
%}

**What it covers:** Lloyd-Jones preached through the entire Sermon on the Mount over the course of two years at Westminster Chapel. The published volumes are the compiled sermons — over 600 pages of careful exposition, soul-searching application, and theological depth. The treatment of the Beatitudes alone runs to over 100 pages.

**Who it's for:** Men who want the most spiritually substantive treatment of the sermon available in English. Lloyd-Jones combines theological precision with pastoral piercing in a way almost no other expositor matches. He preaches in a way that makes you re-examine your own life as you read.

**What it adds:** Spiritual depth. The other books on this list explain the sermon. Lloyd-Jones applies it relentlessly to the reader's soul. The chapters on poverty of spirit, mourning, and meekness will reshape how you understand the Christian life.

**One honest note:** This is sermon material — preached, transcribed, lightly edited. The repetition and rhetorical structure that work well in spoken form sometimes feel redundant in print. Read slowly. The repetition is doing pastoral work even when it feels excessive.

## 3. The Cost of Discipleship — Dietrich Bonhoeffer

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Cost of Discipleship"
  author="Dietrich Bonhoeffer"
  asin="0684815001"
  description="Bonhoeffer's classic 1937 work on Christian discipleship, the central section of which is a piercing reading of the Sermon on the Mount as the actual life Jesus calls his disciples to live — not impossible idealism."
  verdict="The most spiritually demanding book on this list. Read it knowing that Bonhoeffer proved the cost of discipleship with his own life. The reading of the sermon is unmatched."
%}

**What it covers:** *The Cost of Discipleship* is not exclusively about the Sermon on the Mount — the book opens with Bonhoeffer's famous distinction between cheap grace and costly grace, and develops a broader theology of discipleship — but the central section is an extended reading of the Sermon on the Mount as the curriculum of Christian obedience. Bonhoeffer addresses the Beatitudes, the love of enemies, the questions of judgment and prayer, and the parable of the two builders with unusual intensity.

**Who it's for:** Men who suspect the Sermon on the Mount is being treated as inspirational rather than commanded and want a treatment that takes Jesus's words at face value as the actual life he calls his disciples to live. Bonhoeffer refuses to let the sermon be reduced to impossible idealism.

**What it adds:** Moral seriousness. Bonhoeffer wrote *The Cost of Discipleship* in 1937 in Nazi Germany, while leading an underground seminary for the Confessing Church. He was executed by the Nazis in April 1945. The reading of the sermon is informed by a man who knew what it cost to actually obey it. That credibility cannot be replicated by anyone who has not paid a comparable price.

**One honest note:** Bonhoeffer's reading of the sermon is intentionally maximalist. He pushes against any interpretation that softens Jesus's demands. Some readers will find this clarifying; others will find it discouraging. Either way, the work itself is essential reading.

## 4. Sermon on the Mount — Scot McKnight

{% include book-card.html
  title="Sermon on the Mount (The Story of God Bible Commentary)"
  author="Scot McKnight"
  asin="0310327130"
  description="McKnight's contemporary scholarly commentary on Matthew 5-7, part of the Story of God series. Combines careful exegesis with attention to the sermon's place in the larger biblical story and its application to contemporary life."
  verdict="The best contemporary scholarly commentary on the sermon. Read it after Stott and Lloyd-Jones for fresh exegetical insights and contemporary application."
%}

**What it covers:** McKnight develops each section of the Sermon on the Mount with the three-part structure of the Story of God series: Listen to the Story (the text in its biblical-theological context), Explain the Story (exegesis), Live the Story (application). McKnight brings strong New Testament scholarship to the exegesis and unusually thoughtful contemporary application.

**Who it's for:** Men who want a contemporary scholarly treatment that engages recent biblical scholarship while still serving the church. McKnight is an established New Testament scholar (Northern Seminary, formerly North Park) writing for thoughtful Christians rather than for academic peers.

**What it adds:** Contemporary scholarship and biblical-theological framing. Stott and Lloyd-Jones are older treatments. McKnight engages recent scholarship on the sermon's first-century context, its place in Matthew's larger argument, and its relationship to Jewish ethical teaching. The result is fresh insights that supplement what the older works provide.

**One honest note:** McKnight occasionally takes positions on contested questions (the relationship of the sermon to Mosaic law, the meaning of certain antitheses, the role of Anabaptist readings in interpretation) that not every reader will share. Engage the arguments on their merits. The commentary is substantive regardless of where you land on particular questions.

## 5. The Beatitudes for Today — John MacArthur

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Beatitudes: The Only Way to Happiness"
  author="John MacArthur"
  asin="0802453562"
  description="MacArthur's focused treatment of the eight Beatitudes from Matthew 5:3-12 — the opening section of the Sermon on the Mount and its theological gateway. Direct, expository, and doctrinally pointed."
  verdict="The most focused treatment of the Beatitudes on this list. Use it as a deep dive into the sermon's opening section before tackling the whole work."
%}

**What it covers:** MacArthur develops the eight Beatitudes one at a time, with each chapter focused on a single beatitude — poor in spirit, mourning, meekness, hunger for righteousness, mercy, purity of heart, peacemaking, persecution for righteousness. Each chapter combines exegetical analysis of the Greek text, doctrinal explanation of what the beatitude is teaching, and direct application to Christian life.

**Who it's for:** Men who want to go deep into the opening section of the sermon — the Beatitudes — before working through the whole. The Beatitudes are the theological gateway to everything else in the sermon, and an unhurried treatment of them sets up the rest.

**What it adds:** Focused depth. The other books on this list cover all of Matthew 5-7. MacArthur stays in the opening twelve verses. The result is a deeper treatment of those verses than any of the full-sermon commentaries can offer.

**One honest note:** MacArthur is uncompromising in his expository style and his Reformed doctrinal commitments. Readers from other traditions will note the theological framing. The exegetical substance is sound regardless of where you stand on secondary issues.

## How to Study the Sermon on the Mount

The order:

1. **Stott (The Message of the Sermon on the Mount)** — careful balanced overview of the whole
2. **MacArthur (The Beatitudes)** — focused depth on the gateway section
3. **Lloyd-Jones (Studies in the Sermon on the Mount)** — extended pastoral treatment that searches the soul
4. **Bonhoeffer (The Cost of Discipleship)** — moral seriousness, the maximalist reading
5. **McKnight (Sermon on the Mount)** — contemporary scholarship and biblical-theological framing

You do not have to read all five. But the man who has worked through Stott and Lloyd-Jones has spent serious time inside the most important teaching Jesus ever gave. Add Bonhoeffer if you want to be confronted by the cost. Add MacArthur for depth on the Beatitudes. Add McKnight for fresh scholarship.

The Sermon on the Mount is not a section of the Bible. It is the constitution of the Kingdom. Build your life on it. Read it, study it, obey it. The man who hears these words and does them is the one Jesus calls wise — the one who builds his house on the rock.
