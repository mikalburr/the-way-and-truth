Matthew 28:18-20 is one of the most quoted passages in the New Testament and one of the most grammatically mishandled. Most English Bibles translate the verbs in a way that obscures the actual structure of the Greek. The result is that the Great Commission is often preached as "Go!" — a command to evangelistic departure — when the actual command in Greek is something different.

Recovering the grammar does not make the commission less demanding. It makes it more so.

## The Text

"And Jesus came and said to them, 'All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.'" (Matthew 28:18-20)

In Greek, the verse contains one imperative and three participles. Most English translations flatten this into four imperatives. The Greek does not.

## The One Imperative — Mathēteusate

The single imperative verb in verse 19 is *mathēteusate* — "make disciples" (or more woodenly, "disciple"). It is an aorist active imperative, second person plural. This is the command. Everything else in the sentence is grammatically subordinate to this verb.

*Mathēteusate* comes from *mathētēs* — disciple, apprentice, learner — and the related verb *manthanō*, to learn. A *mathētēs* in the first-century Hellenistic world was not a student in a modern classroom. They were an apprentice — someone who learned a craft, philosophy, or way of life by imitating a master and traveling with him. The relationship was long-term, formational, and embodied.

To make disciples is to do that work — to form people who follow Jesus by imitation and instruction over time.

## The Three Participles

The three other action-words in the Greek are participles:

1. **Poreuthentes** — "having gone" or "as you go" (aorist passive participle)
2. **Baptizontes** — "baptizing" (present active participle)
3. **Didaskontes** — "teaching" (present active participle)

In Greek grammar, participles attached to an imperative typically describe the manner, means, or attendant circumstances of the imperative. They are not parallel commands. They modify the main command.

So the structure of the commission is:

- **Command:** Make disciples of all nations
- **Means:** (1) going / as you go, (2) baptizing, (3) teaching

This does not mean "going" is optional. The participle assumes movement. But the central command is not the departure. The central command is the disciple-making, which the going serves.

## Why This Matters

The flattened English reading produces a particular kind of Christianity: short-term mission trips, evangelistic events, conversion-counting. The going is treated as the work. Once you have "gone" and someone has "decided," the box is checked.

The Greek reading produces a different kind: ongoing apprenticeship of believers, baptism into the Triune identity, sustained teaching of everything Jesus commanded. The going is real but it is preliminary. The work is what happens *after* the going.

This also recalibrates "all nations" — *panta ta ethnē*. *Ethnē* means peoples or ethnic groups, not just geopolitical nations. The scope is every people-group on earth. But the goal in each is not converts. It is disciples — apprentices of Jesus, formed over time, baptized and taught.

## "All Authority Has Been Given to Me" (v. 18)

Verse 19 begins with *oun* — "therefore." The commission is grounded in verse 18: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me."

The Greek *pasa exousia* — all authority — is exhaustive. Not some authority. Not religious authority. Not soul authority. All. In heaven and on earth. The risen Christ claims cosmic jurisdiction.

This is the foundation of the commission. The disciples are not asked to convert the nations on their own resources. They are sent out under the authority of the one who already holds the nations. The work is to make disciples of a Lord who already has cosmic rights to them.

This eliminates two failure modes:
- **Arrogance:** The disciples are not the source of the authority. They go under Christ's authority, not their own.
- **Despair:** They are not asked to acquire what Christ does not already hold. The nations already belong to him by right of authority.

## "Baptizing Them in the Name" (v. 19b)

*Baptizontes autous eis to onoma* — baptizing them into the name. The preposition *eis* indicates motion into, transfer of identity. Baptism is not a public ritual added to a private decision. It is the transfer of the new disciple into the identity of the Triune God.

The single name (*onoma*, singular) of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit is significant. Jesus does not say "the names" of the three persons. He says "the name" — one name shared by three. This is the early grammatical foundation for Trinitarian theology, given by Jesus himself in his final commission.

To baptize someone in the Triune name is to mark them as belonging to that name. Their primary identity transfers.

## "Teaching Them to Observe All That I Have Commanded You" (v. 20)

*Didaskontes autous tērein panta hosa eneteilamēn humin* — teaching them to observe (or guard, keep, obey) all that I have commanded you.

Two emphases:

1. **All that I have commanded.** Not a selection of palatable teachings. The full red-letter corpus — including the Beatitudes (see [the Beatitudes exegesis](/true-teachings/2026/05/24/the-beatitudes-verse-by-verse/)), the narrow path (see [the narrow path](/true-teachings/2026/05/12/narrow-path-matthew-7/)), the cross-call (see [take up your cross](/true-teachings/2026/12/06/take-up-your-cross-meaning/)), the warnings about money (see [what Jesus said about money](/true-teachings/2026/10/11/what-jesus-said-about-money/)), and everything else. Selective teaching produces partial disciples, which is to say not disciples.

2. **Teaching them to observe.** The Greek *tērein* is stronger than "consider" or "know." It means to keep, to guard, to do. The teaching is for obedience, not for information. A disciple is one who does what was commanded, not just one who can recite it.

## "I Am With You Always, to the End of the Age" (v. 20b)

The commission closes with a promise: *idou egō meth' humōn eimi pasas tas hēmeras*. "Behold, I — I myself — am with you all the days."

The pronoun *egō* is emphatic in Greek. It would be redundant grammatically — the verb *eimi* already encodes the first person. Including *egō* draws attention to *who* is with them. The Egō eimi — the I AM — is with them (see [the I AM statements](/true-teachings/2026/11/08/the-seven-i-am-statements/)).

"All the days, to the end of the age" — *heōs tēs synteleias tou aiōnos*. Until the consummation. The presence does not run out before the mission does.

## What the Commission Actually Commands

Make disciples of all peoples — long-term apprentices of Jesus — by going where they are, baptizing them into the Triune identity, and teaching them to obey everything Jesus commanded. Do it under the authority of the risen Christ, who has all jurisdiction in heaven and earth, and in the presence of his promised companionship until the age ends.

This is more demanding than the flattened reading. It cannot be completed by short-term evangelistic events. It requires the slow work of formation over years, of teaching the full teaching, of baptizing people into a name they will then have to live under.

It is also less anxious than the flattened reading. The authority is not the disciple's. The presence is not contingent. The end of the age is the horizon, not a deadline you might miss.

Make disciples. Go in the going. Baptize. Teach everything. He is with you.
