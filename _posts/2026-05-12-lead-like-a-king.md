"You know that the rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their great ones exercise authority over them. It shall not be so among you. But whoever would be great among you must be your servant, and whoever would be first among you must be your slave." — Matthew 20:25-27

Jesus said this to men who were arguing about which of them would sit at his right and left hand in the Kingdom. They were negotiating rank. He reordered the entire framework.

This is one of the most misread passages in the New Testament — and one of the most consequential for men. It gets used to argue that Kingdom men should be passive, deferential, quietly accommodating. That leadership is suspect and authority is pride. This reading is wrong, and dangerously so.

What Jesus is describing is not the absence of authority. He is describing a completely different kind of authority — one that is more demanding, more powerful, and more rare than anything the Roman world or the modern world produces.

## The Man Who Washed Feet

John 13. The night before the crucifixion. Jesus knows what is coming — verse 3 says he knew "that the Father had given all things into his hands, and that he had come from God and was going back to God." He knows who he is. He knows what he has. And then he gets up, takes a towel, and washes his disciples' feet.

The foot-washing is not the act of a man who is uncertain about his authority. It is the act of a man who is so secure in his authority that he doesn't need it to be performed for him. He doesn't need the disciples' deference to know that he leads them. He doesn't need the symbols of status to know his position. He is completely free from the anxiety that drives most human leadership — the anxiety of having to be seen.

This is what makes Kingdom authority different. It doesn't require external validation because it's rooted in something that external validation cannot give or take away.

## What Authority Is Actually For

Paul's instructions to men in Ephesians 5:25-28 are well known and consistently misused: "Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her."

What is typically extracted from this verse is a permission structure — a hierarchy that places husbands in authority over wives. What is typically ignored is the model: Christ's relationship to the church is one of sacrificial giving. Christ gave himself up. He died for her. He did not leverage his authority for his own benefit or comfort. He used it for hers.

The authority that Paul is describing is not authority for the leader. It is authority for the people being led. The husband who leads as Christ loved the church is not extracting benefit from the family — he is providing protection, direction, provision, and spiritual covering. He is the one who absorbs cost. He is the one who stands in the gap. The authority is real, and it comes with weight proportional to its scope.

This is why most men don't actually want Kingdom authority once they understand it. It's not power over. It's power for. And it costs.

## The Weakness Problem

There is a failure mode in Christian teaching on manhood that produces passive, conflict-avoidant, spiritually inert men who use the language of humility to avoid the risk of leadership. This is not Kingdom manhood. It is cowardice in religious dress.

Jesus was not passive. He drove the money-changers from the temple with a whip he made himself (John 2:15). He called the Pharisees "whitewashed tombs" and "a brood of vipers" directly to their faces (Matthew 23). He told Peter "Get behind me, Satan" without softening it (Matthew 16:23). He healed on the Sabbath in full view of those who were watching to accuse him, and when they challenged him, he challenged back (Luke 6:6-11).

Gentle was one of his characteristics. It was not the only one, and it did not preclude directness, confrontation, or decisive action. A man who is never direct, never confrontational, and never decisive is not exhibiting Christian gentleness — he is exhibiting failure of nerve.

The Sermon on the Mount's Beatitudes describe the "meek" as those who will inherit the earth (Matthew 5:5). The Greek word is *praus* — it describes a horse that has been broken to the bit. The horse still has all its power. The power is now under control, directed, responsive to the rider's intention. Meekness is not weakness. It is power submitted to higher purpose.

## What Kings Do

Kingdom authority in practice looks like specific behaviors.

**Kings make decisions.** Avoiding decisions — deferring, hedging, waiting for someone else to carry the weight — is not wisdom. It is abdication. A man who will not decide forces the people around him to live in uncertainty. The leader's job is to get clear, decide, and move. You will be wrong sometimes. Lead anyway.

**Kings take responsibility.** When something under your authority fails, the failure is yours. Not primarily — not exclusively — but fundamentally. The man who leads his family, his team, his community takes responsibility for the outcomes in proportion to his authority over the conditions. He does not explain why it wasn't his fault. He asks what he's going to do about it.

**Kings provide covering.** There is something that happens in a family or a team when the man in authority is genuinely present — not physically present, but spiritually and relationally present. When he is paying attention. When he knows what's happening. When he's engaged with the real conditions of the people in his care. This presence is a form of protection. Its absence leaves a gap.

**Kings face threats.** 1 Peter 5:8 — "Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion, seeking someone to devour." The lion looks for weak points, unguarded moments, isolated individuals. A man who understands his role as protector and leader stands between the threat and the people in his care. This is not metaphorical. In spiritual terms, in relational terms, in physical terms — he faces what is coming.

**Kings build.** Nehemiah rebuilt the walls of Jerusalem while half his workers held spears (Nehemiah 4:17). He faced opposition, discouragement, threats, and betrayal — and he kept building. Kingdom men are builders. They invest in what will outlast them. They build families, communities, character, and faith with the same sustained energy that combat requires.

## The Source of Kingdom Authority

The authority Jesus had was not positional. It was not Roman. It was not institutional. The crowds "were astonished at his teaching, for he was teaching them as one who had authority, and not as their scribes" (Matthew 7:28-29). The scribes had the institutional position. Jesus had something else.

What he had was alignment with the Father's will. His authority came from his source — from the one who sent him, from living fully within his calling, from doing what he was made to do. "The Son can do nothing of his own accord, but only what he sees the Father doing" (John 5:19). Complete dependence on the Father produced absolute authority in the world.

This pattern is available to Kingdom men. The authority you carry is not self-generated. It flows from alignment — with God's purposes, with your calling, with the integrity of your character. A man who is aligned with God, who has disciplined himself, who knows who he is and whose he is, carries authority that others recognize without being able to explain it. It is not loudness. It is not dominance. It is the settled weight of a man who is exactly where he's supposed to be, doing exactly what he's supposed to do.

## Where to Start

Start with the people already in your care. Not with authority over strangers. Not with position or title. With your immediate circle — your family, your closest relationships, the people who already look to you.

Ask what you are building there. Ask whether you are present. Ask whether you are making decisions or deferring to everyone and everything. Ask whether you are absorbing cost or distributing it. Ask whether you know what the people in your care actually need.

The man who can answer those questions honestly, and act on what the answers reveal, is developing Kingdom authority. The throne, for most men, is not in the boardroom or the church. It's in the home, at the table, on his knees before the Father, in the daily work of becoming the kind of man that the people in his care can actually count on.

That is what Kingdom leadership looks like. It is harder than any other kind. It is also the only kind that lasts.
