This is a fair question, and it deserves a direct answer rather than a theological dodge.

Do the teachings of Jesus work? For everyone? In the real world, with real consequences?

The honest answer is: they are true for everyone, but not everyone will receive them. And those two things are not in contradiction.

## The Distinction Between True and Received

Mathematics is true for everyone. The law of gravity is true for everyone. These truths operate regardless of whether a person believes them, understands them, or chooses to cooperate with them. A man who denies gravity will still fall.

The teachings of Jesus operate similarly in the domain of ultimate reality — what Jesus called the Kingdom of God. They describe how reality actually works under the sovereignty of God. They are not suggestions for people who prefer that style of living. They are the architecture of the real.

Matthew 7:24-27 is his own statement on this: *"Therefore everyone who hears these words of mine and puts them into practice is like a wise man who built his house on the rock. The rain came down, the streams rose, and the winds blew and beat against that house; yet it did not fall, because it had its foundation on the rock. But everyone who hears these words of mine and does not put them into practice is like a foolish man who built his house on sand. The rain came down, the streams rose, and the winds blew and beat against that house, and it fell with a great crash."*

Note what Jesus does not say here. He does not say: "Everyone who believes the right things about me has a house on rock." He does not say: "Everyone who attends church regularly has a house on rock." He does not even say: "Everyone who has prayed the sinner's prayer has a house on rock."

He says: everyone who hears his words *and puts them into practice*.

The rock and sand are not theological positions. They are structural conditions — the difference between a foundation built on ultimate reality and a foundation built on something that cannot bear the load when the storms come.

The storms come for everyone. The question is whether the foundation holds.

## Why Some People Find the Teachings Unworkable

There is a specific and identifiable reason why some people encounter Jesus's teachings and conclude they don't work: they are applying them as a behavior upgrade rather than as a Kingdom framework.

The distinction matters. A behavior upgrade assumes you are the same person with modified external actions. You don't commit adultery — but you are still the person whose inner life is organized around self-gratification, personal security, and status management; you've just added a rule about sexual behavior on top of that operating system.

Jesus's teachings are not a behavior upgrade. They are a description of what it looks like when the operating system itself has been changed — when the fundamental orientation of the inner life has shifted from self-centered autonomy to Kingdom-centered allegiance.

Matthew 12:33-35: *"Make a tree good and its fruit will be good, or make a tree bad and its fruit will be bad, for a tree is recognized by its fruit... For out of the overflow of the heart the mouth speaks. A good man brings good things out of the good stored up in him, and an evil man brings evil things out of the evil stored up in him."*

The fruit comes from the tree. You cannot reliably produce good fruit from a bad tree by disciplining the fruit. The tree has to change.

This is why Jesus's conversation with Nicodemus in John 3 is so central. A Pharisee — a man of extraordinary behavioral discipline and religious knowledge — comes to Jesus impressed by his signs. Jesus responds not by affirming his religious achievement but by saying: *"You must be born again"* (John 3:7). Born from above. A new origin, not an improved version of the existing one.

People who apply the teachings of Jesus as behavior modification discover that they don't reliably hold. You can white-knuckle your way through love of enemies for a while. But when sufficient pressure is applied, and the tree hasn't changed, the fruit reverts.

People who are genuinely born from above — who have experienced the *metanoia* (complete reorientation) Jesus called for — discover that the teachings describe how their new inner life actually wants to operate. They are not a cage. They are a description of Kingdom life that the new nature recognizes as home.

## The Teachings Are Not Therapy

This is an important clarification for the contemporary context.

Therapy is a legitimate and valuable set of practices for addressing psychological pain, processing trauma, and building relational skills. The teachings of Jesus are not therapy, and they should not be evaluated by therapeutic criteria.

Therapy asks: does this make you feel better? Does it reduce anxiety? Does it improve your relationships and your functional capacity?

The teachings of Jesus ask: are they true? Are they in alignment with ultimate reality as defined by the Kingdom of God?

Jesus did not promise that following him would maximize subjective wellbeing in the short term. He promised the opposite of that in several places:

Matthew 5:10-12: *"Blessed are those who are persecuted because of righteousness, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven... Blessed are you when people insult you, persecute you and falsely say all kinds of evil against you because of me. Rejoice and be glad, because great is your reward in heaven."*

John 15:20: *"If they persecuted me, they will persecute you also."*

Matthew 10:34-36: *"Do not suppose that I have come to bring peace to the earth. I did not come to bring peace, but a sword... a man's enemies will be the members of his own household."*

These are not promises of immediate wellbeing. They are promises of ultimate vindication and present Kingdom reality, in a context that may be objectively difficult.

The teachings work — but "working" means they accurately describe reality and produce the fruit Jesus promised: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness (Galatians 5:22-23), the capacity to withstand storms (Matthew 7:24-25), and the deep relational knowledge of God that Jesus defined as eternal life (John 17:3).

If your benchmark for "working" is comfort, the teachings will regularly seem to fail. If your benchmark is truth and Kingdom reality, they are unfailing.

## Are They Universal?

Yes. Jesus was explicit about the universality of his claim.

John 18:37: *"Everyone on the side of truth listens to me."* Not: everyone in this ethnic group, everyone in this religious tradition, everyone in this cultural moment. Everyone on the side of truth.

John 14:6: *"I am the way and the truth and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."* The exclusivity is total. This does not mean the invitation is narrow — Jesus elsewhere compares the Kingdom to a banquet with an open invitation (Luke 14:16-23). The exclusivity is that there is one access point, not that few are welcome.

Matthew 28:19-20: *"Go and make disciples of all nations."* Not some nations. All. The teachings are for every human being in every culture in every age.

But universal truth is not universally received. Jesus was clear about this too. Matthew 7:13-14: *"Enter through the narrow gate. For wide is the gate and broad is the road that leads to destruction, and many enter through it. But small is the gate and narrow the road that leads to life, and only a few find it."*

The narrowness is not the narrowness of invitation. It is the narrowness of the demand. The road to life requires the *metanoia* — the full reorientation — that many people are unwilling to make. Not because the demand is arbitrary or cruel, but because it requires giving up the illusion of autonomous self-governance that is the deepest human resistance to Kingdom reality.

The teachings of Jesus work for everyone who genuinely receives and practices them. They are true for everyone whether they are received or not. And the ultimate test of whether they work is not how you feel this week, but whether your house is standing when the storm hits.

The storms are coming. The question is the foundation.
