Most devotionals are inspirational placebos. A verse, a feel-good paragraph, a prayer that asks God for nothing serious. They produce nothing because they require nothing.

A real devotional does something different. It places substantive theological content next to your soul every morning for the rest of your life. It is short enough that you will actually do it. It is heavy enough that doing it for a decade changes you.

These five devotionals meet that standard. I have used four of them in extended daily practice — Spurgeon and Tripp for years, Chambers for two cycles, Valley of Vision as ongoing reference. The fifth, Catt, I have read in segments and recommend with confidence. Here they are.

---

## 1. New Morning Mercies — Paul David Tripp

{% include book-card.html
  title="New Morning Mercies: A Daily Gospel Devotional"
  author="Paul David Tripp"
  asin="1433541386"
  description="365 daily readings, each built around a single tweet-length theological gospel statement that opens into a 1-2 page meditation. Concentrated, pastoral, and structurally consistent."
  verdict="The best modern devotional for men. Start here. The combination of brevity, theological weight, and pastoral honesty is unmatched in contemporary devotional writing."
%}

**What it covers:** Each day opens with a single concentrated gospel statement, then develops it across one to two pages of meditation. Tripp is one of the most theologically careful pastoral writers working today, and the daily readings consistently move from doctrine to application without losing either dimension.

**Who it's for:** Men who want a daily devotional that fits in 5-10 minutes but does not feel like a 5-minute devotional. Tripp's writing is dense enough to be substantive and clear enough to be accessible at 6 AM with one cup of coffee.

**What it adds:** Gospel-centered repetition. The same fundamental truths about Christ, sin, grace, and daily dependence are turned and re-turned across the year. By December you have heard them in 365 different angles and they have become the air you breathe.

**One honest note:** Tripp writes from a Reformed evangelical position with strong emphasis on heart-level sin and gospel dependence. If you want a devotional with more emphasis on action and obedience, pair this with Spurgeon. Together they balance.

## 2. Morning and Evening — Charles Spurgeon

{% include book-card.html
  title="Morning and Evening: A New Edition of the Classic Devotional"
  author="Charles Spurgeon"
  asin="1433509415"
  description="Two daily readings — morning and evening — across the full year. Spurgeon was the most influential preacher of the 19th century and these meditations carry that homiletical weight."
  verdict="The classic devotional for men who want substantive daily readings with historical weight. Use morning and evening for sustained rhythm, or just morning if that is what you can hold."
%}

**What it covers:** 730 short devotional readings — one for the morning and one for the evening of each day — drawn from across the Bible and built around a single verse per reading. Spurgeon's writing is rich, image-dense, and pastorally tender in ways that modern devotionals rarely match.

**Who it's for:** Men who want their daily devotional connected to the broader tradition of serious Christian reading. Spurgeon preached to thousands at the Metropolitan Tabernacle in London and was widely regarded as the most gifted English-language preacher of the 19th century. The same gifts come through in the devotional readings.

**What it adds:** Affective depth. Spurgeon writes with a warmth toward Christ that many modern devotionals lack. He helps you love Christ, not just think about him. That is harder to produce than it sounds, and Spurgeon does it in nearly every reading.

**One honest note:** The original Spurgeon prose is 19th-century English and can feel dense to modern readers. Several editions update the language modestly without losing the substance — the Alistair Begg-introduced edition is one of the most accessible. Or buy the original. Both are excellent.

## 3. My Utmost for His Highest — Oswald Chambers

{% include book-card.html
  title="My Utmost for His Highest"
  author="Oswald Chambers"
  asin="1572934700"
  description="365 daily readings compiled posthumously by Chambers's widow from his Bible Training College lectures. Confrontational, demanding, and unconcerned with comforting you."
  verdict="The most spiritually demanding devotional on this list. Chambers will not flatter you. Read him when you are ready to be examined."
%}

**What it covers:** Each day's reading is built around a single biblical text and develops a single piercing application — usually about the difference between religious nominalism and actual abandonment to Christ. Chambers is unrelenting in his refusal to soften the cost of discipleship.

**Who it's for:** Men who suspect their Christianity is too comfortable and want to be confronted by a writer who refuses to coddle. Chambers asks the questions most devotionals avoid: Are you actually following Jesus? Or are you running a religious project under his name?

**What it adds:** Confrontation. The other devotionals on this list comfort, instruct, and warm the heart. Chambers does some of that — but his primary mode is to put you on the spot. The men I know who have worked through him slowly report that it changes them.

**One honest note:** Chambers wrote in early 20th-century English and his prose is more demanding than most readers expect. The substance rewards slow reading. Do not try to power-read this one — sit with each entry for ten minutes if necessary.

## 4. The Valley of Vision — Arthur Bennett, editor

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Valley of Vision: A Collection of Puritan Prayers and Devotions"
  author="Arthur Bennett"
  asin="085151228X"
  description="A compiled collection of Puritan prayers organized thematically — confession, communion with God, gospel adoration, Christian warfare, suffering, grace. The single most spiritually formative prayer book in print."
  verdict="Not a daily devotional in the standard format. A prayer companion to use alongside your devotional. The Puritan voice on prayer is unmatched and will reshape how you talk to God."
%}

**What it covers:** Roughly 200 prayers compiled and lightly edited from the writings of John Bunyan, John Owen, Isaac Watts, Richard Baxter, and other 17th and 18th century Puritan and Reformed writers. Organized thematically rather than by date. Each prayer is approximately a page long.

**Who it's for:** Men whose own prayer vocabulary has become repetitive and shallow. The Puritan writers prayed at a depth that most modern Christians have lost the language for. Reading and praying through Valley of Vision restores some of that vocabulary.

**What it adds:** Theological richness in prayer. The Puritan prayers move fluently between confession of specific sin, adoration of specific divine attributes, plea for specific holiness, and rest in specific gospel realities. They model what serious prayer can sound like.

**One honest note:** This is not a 365-day devotional. Use it differently — pray through one prayer per day as your morning prayer, or read it alongside another devotional as a prayer-formation companion. Either pattern works.

## 5. Strong and Courageous: Daily Promises for Men — Michael Catt

{% include book-card.html
  title="Strong and Courageous: Daily Promises for Men of Courage"
  author="Michael Catt"
  asin="1462762344"
  description="A men-specific daily devotional built around courage, leadership, and biblical manhood. Each reading pairs a Scripture passage with a short meditation aimed at men in active roles — husband, father, worker, leader."
  verdict="The most directly applied devotional on this list for men in family and leadership roles. Less theologically dense than Tripp or Chambers, but more situationally targeted to the daily life of a working man."
%}

**What it covers:** Daily readings built around the biblical theme of courage as a defining virtue of biblical manhood. Catt — the longtime pastor at Sherwood Baptist Church and executive producer of the Sherwood Pictures films — writes with directness aimed at men in active family and vocational roles.

**Who it's for:** Men in the season of life where leadership, fatherhood, and vocational pressure are the operative realities. Catt speaks into those specific domains with practical biblical application that most general devotionals do not address.

**What it adds:** Situational focus. The other devotionals on this list are for any reader. This one is for men. The questions it raises and the encouragements it gives are calibrated to the actual conditions of a Christian man's weekday life.

**One honest note:** This is a more popular-register devotional than Spurgeon, Chambers, or Tripp. Use it as a supplement, not as your primary devotional. Pair it with one of the heavier options above for a balanced daily rhythm.

## How to Build Your Devotional Practice

The mistake most men make is trying to read three devotionals at once and finishing none of them. Pick one. Commit to it for a year.

The order I recommend:

**Year 1:** New Morning Mercies. Gospel-centered, modern, and the most likely to actually get read every day.

**Year 2:** Morning and Evening (Spurgeon). The historical and pastoral weight of the 19th century alongside the gospel saturation of Tripp.

**Year 3:** My Utmost for His Highest. By now you have built the daily habit. Chambers will use it to dismantle whatever comfort has crept in.

**Always:** Valley of Vision as a prayer companion alongside whichever devotional you are currently reading.

A single substantive devotional read daily for a decade will do more for your spiritual formation than every men's conference you will ever attend. Start tomorrow morning. One reading. Five minutes. Do not skip.
