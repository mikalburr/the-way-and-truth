The first year of following Jesus is the most formational year of a believer's life. What you read in that year — and equally important, what you do not read — shapes the theological frame that everything else will sit inside for decades.

Most new Christians end up with whatever was on the shelf at the church bookstore, recommended by a well-meaning friend, or going viral on Instagram. That is a problem. The first year deserves five books chosen carefully. These are mine. I have read all five and recommend each one for specific reasons.

---

## 1. Mere Christianity — C.S. Lewis

{% include book-card.html
  title="Mere Christianity"
  author="C.S. Lewis"
  asin="0060652926"
  description="The most thorough and accessible introduction to the basics of the Christian faith in print, originally delivered as wartime radio talks by a former atheist who thought his way carefully into faith."
  verdict="The single best first book for any new Christian. Read it slowly, with a pen, and revisit it in three years."
%}

**What it covers:** Lewis covers the moral argument for God's existence (the universal sense of right and wrong), the basics of Christian belief (the Trinity, the incarnation, the atonement), Christian behavior (cardinal virtues, sexual morality, forgiveness, hope), and the deeper theological topics (the nature of God, the new humanity in Christ). The book originated as BBC radio talks during World War II and retains the directness and clarity of that format.

**Who it's for:** Any new believer, regardless of background. Lewis is especially valuable for thoughtful adults who came to faith with questions still on the table. He was a former atheist who thought his way in carefully, and the book reflects that journey.

**What it adds:** Intellectual confidence. Many new Christians arrive at faith emotionally and never develop the framework to articulate what they believe to themselves or others. Lewis gives you that framework in 200 pages of unusually clear prose.

**One honest note:** Lewis is an Anglican writing in 1940s Britain. A few of the cultural illustrations feel dated, and a handful of theological points have been engaged differently by other traditions. None of this diminishes the value of the book as a first read. Lewis remains unmatched.

## 2. Basic Christianity — John Stott

{% include book-card.html
  title="Basic Christianity"
  author="John Stott"
  asin="083083403X"
  description="Stott's classic introduction to the Christian faith — structured around the person of Christ, the need of man, the response of faith, and the results of conversion. Direct, evangelistically clear, and theologically careful."
  verdict="The best entry-level book on what Christianity actually claims. Pair with Lewis for the most thorough first-year foundation."
%}

**What it covers:** Stott structures the book in four parts: who Christ is (his claims, character, resurrection), what man needs (the nature and consequences of sin), what Christ has done (the cross and resurrection in their saving meaning), and how to respond (repentance, faith, and the results in a transformed life). The book is short, tight, and unusually clear.

**Who it's for:** New believers who want a more directly evangelistic and theologically focused introduction than Lewis provides. Stott is especially useful for those who came to faith without much teaching beforehand and need the basic content of Christian belief presented in order.

**What it adds:** Theological focus. Where Lewis ranges across morals, virtues, and apologetics, Stott concentrates on the central claims of Christianity itself — Christ, sin, atonement, faith, transformation. The focus is exactly right for a foundational read.

**One honest note:** Stott writes in a measured British evangelical register that some American readers find understated. The substance is anything but understated. Adjust your expectations of tone; the content is worth it.

## 3. The Holiness of God — R.C. Sproul

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Holiness of God"
  author="R.C. Sproul"
  asin="1414336195"
  description="Sproul builds the case that holiness is the central attribute of God, the one that organizes every other attribute and explains the structure of the gospel itself. The book that changed countless serious Christians' understanding of God."
  verdict="Essential for any new believer who senses that their picture of God has become too small or too sentimental. Read it carefully."
%}

**What it covers:** Sproul begins with Isaiah 6 — the prophet's vision of God in the temple, the seraphim crying "Holy, holy, holy" — and works outward through the biblical text and church history to develop holiness as the foundational attribute of God. He covers the trauma of holiness in encounter (Isaiah, Peter, Job), the misunderstanding of holiness in popular religion, and the relationship between holiness and the cross.

**Who it's for:** New believers whose picture of God is dominated by love or grace without holiness, and who need the corrective. Most modern American Christians come to faith with an underdeveloped doctrine of God's holiness. This is the book that fills the gap.

**What it adds:** Theological weight. Sproul does not let God be small. He insists on the actual biblical portrait — terrifying, glorious, just, holy, gracious — and refuses to soften it. The result is a portrait of God that finally feels worth worshipping.

**One honest note:** Sproul writes from a Reformed framework. New believers from other traditions will still benefit from the book — the doctrine of God's holiness is shared across orthodox Christianity. Engage the framework honestly; the substance is essential.

## 4. What Is the Gospel? — Greg Gilbert

{% include book-card.html
  title="What Is the Gospel?"
  author="Greg Gilbert"
  asin="1433515008"
  description="A short, careful answer to the most basic Christian question — what is the gospel? — built around four themes: God, man, Christ, response. The most useful brief gospel summary in print."
  verdict="Every new believer should read this once and then keep it on the shelf as a reference. Short, focused, faithful to the New Testament."
%}

**What it covers:** Gilbert organizes the book around four questions: Who made us and what does he expect? What is our problem? What is God's solution? How do we receive it? The answers are biblical, focused, and refuse to inflate or deflate the gospel. The result is the clearest brief statement of the Christian message in contemporary print.

**Who it's for:** Every new believer should read this once early. It functions as a clarifying reset whenever your understanding of the gospel gets cluttered by additions or substitutions — which it will, more often than you expect, in your first decade of faith.

**What it adds:** Clarity. The American evangelical world has produced a hundred competing versions of "the gospel" — therapeutic, prosperity, social, moralistic. Gilbert returns to the New Testament's actual content and presents it without inflation. The reset is needed.

**One honest note:** Gilbert is short. Some readers want more biblical depth or pastoral application. Use the book as the clarifying core; supplement with the larger books on this list for the surrounding theology.

## 5. King's Cross — Tim Keller

{% include book-card.html
  title="King's Cross: The Story of the World in the Life of Jesus"
  author="Timothy Keller"
  asin="1444702165"
  description="Keller's walk through the Gospel of Mark — chapter by chapter, scene by scene — showing how the life, ministry, death, and resurrection of Jesus is the central story of the world. Devotionally powerful and theologically rich."
  verdict="The best book for a new believer learning to read the Gospels carefully. Use it alongside your own reading of Mark."
%}

**What it covers:** Keller takes the Gospel of Mark and works through it in two parts — the King (Mark 1-8) and the Cross (Mark 9-16) — chapter by chapter, drawing out the historical context, the literary structure, the theological weight, and the personal application of each section. The result is a guided tour through the most direct of the four Gospels.

**Who it's for:** New believers who have started reading the Gospels and want a careful guide alongside them. Especially valuable for readers who are encountering the actual narrative of Jesus's life for the first time and need help seeing the depth.

**What it adds:** Engagement with the actual text of a Gospel. Most introductory Christian books talk about Jesus in summary. Keller walks you through what the Gospel writer actually wrote, in order, with care for the scenes themselves. The shift from summary to text is significant.

**One honest note:** This is the longest book on the list and the most narrative-driven. Read a chapter of Mark, then read the corresponding section of Keller, then sit with it. The book is most useful taken slowly.

## How to Use This List in Your First Year

A reading order designed for a deliberate first year of formation:

1. **Months 1-2: Mere Christianity** — the foundational framework
2. **Months 3-4: Basic Christianity** — the focused theological core
3. **Months 5-6: What Is the Gospel?** — the clarifying short read
4. **Months 7-9: The Holiness of God** — the doctrine of God you actually need
5. **Months 10-12: King's Cross** — the Gospel of Mark guided carefully

You will not master Christianity in one year. No one does. But you can build a working theological foundation that everything else will sit on for decades — and that foundation is unusually durable when these five books are the materials.

A new Christian needs less than they think. A few good books, read slowly, with prayer and a more mature Christian to talk it through with — that is enough for the first year.

Start tonight. One book. The first chapter.
