A checklist is only useful if it tells you the truth. The goal of this assessment is not to make you feel good about where you are. It is to give you an accurate picture of where you are operating from — Kingdom identity or something else — so you know where the work needs to happen.

Read each question. Answer it honestly — not how you think you should answer it, but how your actual behavior and interior life answer it. Then compare against the Kingdom-aligned response.

---

## Category 1: Identity

**1. When you fail significantly, what is your first internal response?**
Kingdom response: "I have failed, and there is no condemnation for me in Christ. What does the King ask of me now?" The failure is acknowledged without becoming a verdict on identity. Recovery is initiated without extended shame.

**2. When someone criticizes you publicly or professionally, what drives your response?**
Kingdom response: Self-possession. The ability to consider the criticism honestly without defensive collapse or defensive aggression, because your identity is not built on others' evaluations.

**3. Do you know who you are in Christ beyond a list of biblical facts?**
Kingdom response: The truths of your citizenship (Romans 8:17, 2 Corinthians 5:17, Ephesians 2:10) are not just propositions you affirm. They are operating beliefs that shape how you move through the world on ordinary days.

**4. Is your sense of worth stable across good and bad seasons?**
Kingdom response: Kingdom identity is not circumstance-dependent. It does not inflate in good seasons or collapse in bad ones. The foundation (who you are in the King's eyes) does not change with your performance or your circumstances.

---

## Category 2: Prayer Life

**5. What percentage of your prayer is requests versus alignment?**
Kingdom response: A Kingdom prayer life is primarily about alignment — your kingdom come, your will be done — with requests in proper proportion. If the majority of your prayer is asking God to help your plans, you are praying from a consumer position, not a citizen one.

**6. Do you pray as if God is reluctant or as if the Father runs toward you?**
Kingdom response: Luke 15:20 establishes the Father's posture. A Kingdom citizen prays with confidence, not because he has earned an audience but because access is established by the King. "For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses... Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence" (Hebrews 4:15-16, BSB).

**7. Does your prayer change you, or only your circumstances?**
Kingdom response: The primary function of prayer is not to change God's actions but to align the citizen with the King's perspective. If you leave prayer the same person with different requests, something is missing.

**8. Can you pray honestly — including complaint, confusion, and lament?**
Kingdom response: Kingdom citizens have access to the full emotional range in prayer. The Psalms — which Jesus quoted from the cross — model prayer that includes desolation, accusation of absence, and sustained honest engagement with difficulty. Sanitized prayer is not Kingdom prayer.

---

## Category 3: Response to Failure

**9. How long does it take you to recover from significant failure?**
Kingdom response: Recovery time in a Kingdom mindset shortens over years — not because failure matters less but because the path back is clearer. Return, acknowledge, receive, move forward. The spiral of self-recrimination is broken by Kingdom identity, not perpetuated by it.

**10. Do you hide your failures or bring them into the light?**
Kingdom response: Kingdom community is the safest place for honest disclosure. Men operating in Kingdom mindset can name their failures to trusted brothers without collapse, because the community's response is not judgment but solidarity. If you are hiding your significant failures from everyone, you are operating from shame, not Kingdom.

**11. Do you tend to blame others or accept responsibility?**
Kingdom response: Kingdom identity produces honest ownership of failure without catastrophizing. Men who know who they are do not need external targets for their shame. They can say "I was wrong" with directness and without drama.

**12. After failure, does your engagement with God increase or decrease?**
Kingdom response: The prodigal son turned toward the father, not away. Men operating in Kingdom mindset respond to failure by returning — more prayer, more Scripture, more honest community — not by withdrawal from the relationship.

---

## Category 4: View of Others

**13. When another man succeeds — especially in an area you care about — what is your interior response?**
Kingdom response: Genuine pleasure. Men operating in Kingdom identity experience others' success as evidence that the Kingdom is advancing, not as evidence of their own inadequacy. The ability to celebrate another man's success without the shadow of comparison is one of the clearest markers of genuine Kingdom identity.

**14. Do you rank people — spiritually, morally, socially?**
Kingdom response: Jesus's Kingdom is relentlessly anti-hierarchical in social terms. The first will be last. The servant is the greatest. Men who are constantly ranking themselves against others — above or below — are operating from a religious performance system, not Kingdom identity.

**15. Can you love people who actively work against you?**
Kingdom response: "But I say to you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you" (Matthew 5:44, BSB). This is not conditional on the enemy improving their behavior. Kingdom love extends beyond the boundary of merit. This is not natural — it is the specific product of Kingdom identity formed over time.

**16. Do you invest in other people's growth, or does their development feel like a threat?**
Kingdom response: A Kingdom citizen invests in others because the Kingdom's advancement matters more than their own status. Men who hoard knowledge, access, or opportunity because others' development threatens their position are operating from scarcity, not Kingdom abundance.

---

## Category 5: Daily Priorities

**17. On a normal day, what actually governs your decisions?**
Kingdom response: The question "what does the Kingdom ask of me today?" should be at least a regular input into daily decision-making. If your decisions are governed entirely by productivity, financial return, social approval, or comfort, the Kingdom is not functioning as first priority (Matthew 6:33).

**18. Is your work connected to Kingdom purpose, or is it just income?**
Kingdom response: Work, in Kingdom theology, is participation in the dominion mandate (Genesis 1:28) — exercising stewardship over the domain the King has entrusted to you. Even ordinary work, properly understood, is Kingdom activity. The man who shovels with Kingdom intent is not just moving dirt. He is participating in the ordering of creation on behalf of the King.

**19. What does your actual schedule reveal about your priorities?**
Kingdom response: Time is the most honest indicator of priority. Not what you say matters — what you spend hours on. If you say the Kingdom is first priority but your schedule shows zero engagement with Scripture, prayer, or Kingdom community in a given week, the priority is stated, not real.

**20. When you imagine your life in ten years, what are you hoping for?**
Kingdom response: Kingdom vision is not primarily about personal achievement, security, or comfort. It is about advancement of the King's purposes in your specific domain. A Kingdom citizen imagines the future in terms of what the King could do through him — not just what he could acquire for himself.

---

## How to Use These Results

Do not try to fix everything at once. Identify the one or two questions where the gap between your actual response and the Kingdom response is largest. That gap is your growth edge. Work there first.

Kingdom mindset is developed through honest diagnosis followed by targeted engagement. The checklist is only the first step. The work is what happens after you put the list down.

If you answered honestly and found that most of your responses are already Kingdom-aligned, be careful. Spiritual self-assessment has a well-documented bias toward flattery. Ask the man who knows you best to review your answers. His assessment will be more accurate than yours.
