The obstacle is not motivation. Most people who want to study Jesus's words have enough motivation to begin. The obstacle is design — having a practice that is simple enough to do consistently, substantial enough to produce real engagement with the text, and robust enough to work on the days when motivation is low.

This article is about the design of that practice.

## The Minimum Viable Practice

The minimum viable practice is this: one passage, read three times, one sentence of application.

One passage means a bounded unit of text — a parable, a teaching block, a conversation. Not a verse in isolation; the context matters. Not a whole chapter if it contains multiple distinct units. Something that functions as a complete thought — the Sermon on the Mount's section on prayer (Matthew 6:5-13), the parable of the prodigal son (Luke 15:11-32), the vine and branches teaching (John 15:1-17).

Read it three times, each with a different question:

**First reading:** What is Jesus saying? Understand the plain content before interpretation.

**Second reading:** What is Jesus doing? Look at the structure of the argument — how is he building his point, what sequence is he following, who is he addressing and why?

**Third reading:** What is Jesus requiring of me? This passage, this specific day, this specific season of your life — what does it demand?

One sentence of application: write one specific sentence that names what you will do in the next 24 hours in response to what you read. Not "I will try to be more loving." One specific action. *"I will text [name] today and ask how they are."* *"I will not check my investment account balance today as a test of where my trust actually is."* Specific. Actionable. Time-bounded.

This practice takes 10-15 minutes. It can be done anywhere. It has no prerequisites. It produces, over time, the cumulative engagement with Jesus's teaching that transforms the inner life.

## Why Quantity of Reading Matters Less Than Depth of Engagement

There is a common Protestant instinct toward quantity of Bible reading — read the whole Bible in a year, cover-to-cover plans, chapters-per-day goals. The instinct is not wrong. Breadth of exposure to Scripture has real value.

But with the Gospels specifically, and Jesus's teachings in particular, depth of engagement with fewer passages produces more transformation than breadth of exposure to many passages without depth.

James 1:22-25 makes the distinction: *"Do not merely listen to the word, and so deceive yourselves. Do what it says. Anyone who listens to the word but does not do what it says is like someone who looks at his face in a mirror and, after looking at himself, goes away and immediately forgets what he looks like. But whoever looks intently into the perfect law that gives freedom and continues in it — not forgetting what they have heard but doing it — they will be blessed in what they do."*

The mirror metaphor is precise: brief exposure, no retention, no change. Intently looking — with continuation, with action — produces blessing.

One passage applied deeply is worth more than ten passages skimmed. You are not trying to complete the text. You are trying to let the text complete you — to shape your thinking, your reflexes, your priorities, your relationships.

A person who has deeply engaged with Matthew 5:43-48 (love your enemies) for thirty days and genuinely applied it will be more transformed than a person who has read through the entire Sermon on the Mount three times without applying any of it.

The goal is not coverage. The goal is formation.

## The Lectio Divina Approach, Adapted for Modern Readers

Lectio Divina (Latin: sacred reading) is a practice of meditative engagement with Scripture that originated in the monastic tradition, formalized by Benedict of Nursia in the sixth century. It has four movements: *lectio* (reading), *meditatio* (meditation), *oratio* (prayer), *contemplatio* (contemplation).

The four movements are not steps in a sequence so much as a spiraling engagement with the same text. Adapted for a modern reader who is not a monk and has 15 minutes:

**Lectio (3-4 minutes):** Read the passage slowly. When a word or phrase catches your attention — creates a point of friction, resonates unexpectedly, confuses you, or challenges you — stop there. Don't rush past it. That word or phrase is the focal point for the rest of the session.

**Meditatio (4-5 minutes):** Turn that word or phrase over in your mind. What does it actually mean? Read it in context. If it's a Greek word with more range than the English translation captures (and many are), that is worth looking up. Allow the word to interact with your current circumstances, your current failures, your current questions. Let it speak to your situation rather than treating it as abstract.

**Oratio (3-4 minutes):** Talk to God about what you have found. Not a formal prayer — a conversation. What does this mean for you? What are you resisting? What would change if you took this word seriously? What do you need from him to do that? Be specific and honest.

**Contemplatio (2-3 minutes):** Silence. Not filling the silence with more words or thoughts — receiving. This is the hardest part for modern readers, who are trained to fill every silence with content. The contemplation is about presence rather than production. Simply being before the Father, in the light of what you have just read.

This practice with one passage, done consistently, is more spiritually formative than an hour of rapid reading done occasionally.

## What to Do When a Passage Makes No Sense

Every serious reader of the Gospels will hit passages that are genuinely unclear — either because the cultural background is unfamiliar, the grammar is complex, or Jesus is making a point that requires more context than you have.

The instinct is to immediately look it up. Resist this instinct for at least one session. Sit with the confusion first.

The reason: many passages that appear unclear will resolve with re-reading and reflection. The second and third reading often reveals a coherence that the first reading missed. If you jump to commentary immediately, you skip the productive struggle that often produces the deepest understanding.

*"Sit with it, not around it"* is the principle. Around the difficult passage means avoiding it — skipping ahead, finding a more manageable verse, moving on to something that makes sense. Into it means staying at the point of confusion and working it from multiple angles before seeking outside help.

After one session of honest engagement with the confusing passage, practical steps:

**Check the context.** Read the ten verses before and after. Most of the time, context resolves apparent confusion.

**Read multiple translations.** The BSB, ESV, and The Message will give you three registers of the same text — formal equivalence, literal, and felt meaning. Sometimes the confusion is about how a specific translator chose a specific English word, and a different translation resolves it immediately.

**Use BibleHub.com's interlinear.** Look at the Greek word behind the confusing English word. See its full range of meaning. Sometimes the translation has chosen a narrow English word for a Greek word with a much wider range, and expanding the range resolves the confusion.

**Then consult commentary.** Matthew Henry for application, John Gill for original language depth, N.T. Wright for Jewish context. Check multiple commentaries rather than trusting any single one.

One important caution: some passages appear confusing because they are demanding something you don't want to do, and your mind is generating complexity to avoid the plain meaning. Be honest with yourself about whether the confusion is intellectual or motivational. The test: if the passage said something easy or comfortable, would you still find it confusing? If not, the confusion is likely resistance.

## The Long Game

Building a daily habit of studying Jesus's words is a decades-long project. The payoff is not immediate. The first month produces some disorientation and some insight. The first year produces measurable shifts in the priority structure, the interior life, the relational patterns. The first five years produce a depth of familiarity with Jesus's voice and a coherence of Kingdom framework that no other practice produces.

John 8:31-32: *"If you hold to my teaching, you are really my disciples. Then you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free."*

The Greek word for "hold" is *meno* — remain, abide, dwell. The knowing and the freedom are the result of remaining over time. Not visiting. Remaining.

The minimum viable practice, done consistently over years, builds the remaining. One passage, read three times, one sentence of application. Day after day. That is enough to begin and enough to sustain the entire project.

Start today. Start simply. Stay in it.
