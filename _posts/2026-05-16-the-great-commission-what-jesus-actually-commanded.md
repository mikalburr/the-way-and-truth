Matthew 28:18-20 — the Great Commission — is one of the most quoted passages in Christianity and one of the most misunderstood. It has been used to justify overseas missions programs, altar calls, street evangelism campaigns, church growth strategies, and colonial enterprise. Some of these uses are better than others. Most of them share a common problem: they miss the main verb.

Read it carefully.

## The Full Text

> "And Jesus came and said to them, 'All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.'" — Matthew 28:18-20

## The Authority Statement Is Not Optional

The commission begins not with a command but with a declaration: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me." This is the foundation. Everything that follows — "go therefore" — rests on this statement.

*Exousia pasa* — all authority. Not some authority. Not spiritual authority with certain limitations. All authority in both domains: heaven (the spiritual realm) and earth (the physical, historical, political realm). This is an absolute claim.

Why does this matter? Because it establishes who is sending and what backing the commission carries. The man who goes to make disciples is not operating on his own initiative or on the authority of his church or denomination. He is operating on the authority of the risen King, who holds all authority everywhere. The commission is not a suggestion from a well-meaning teacher. It is an order from the One who actually runs everything.

The resurrection is the basis. Jesus does not make this claim before the cross. The authority is "given" — *edothe*, a divine passive, something granted by the Father to the Son through the resurrection and ascension. Death could not hold him. The authority of the Kingdom is now concentrated in the resurrected Christ and extended through his commissioned people.

## The Main Verb: Make Disciples

In Greek, the Great Commission contains one main imperative verb: *matheteusate* — make disciples. The other three verbs — "go," "baptizing," and "teaching" — are participles in Greek. They describe the manner in which the main command is carried out, not separate equal commands.

The commission is not: go AND make disciples AND baptize AND teach — four separate commands of equal weight.

The commission is: **make disciples** — as you go, through baptizing, by teaching.

This distinction changes the entire shape of what is being commanded. The primary assignment is *matheteusate* — making disciples. Everything else serves that end.

### What a Disciple Is

*Mathetes* — disciple, learner, follower. In the first-century world, a disciple was someone who attached himself to a rabbi, traveled with him, studied his teaching, and over years became shaped by his teacher's way of life. The goal was not information transfer. The goal was formation — becoming someone who thought, acted, and lived as the teacher did.

Jesus's disciples did not attend weekly lectures and go home unchanged. They ate with him, traveled with him, watched him in every situation, heard him respond to critics and suffering and need, and were expected to eventually do what he did. Formation, not information.

When Jesus commands "make disciples," he is commanding the reproduction of that process — not in its every external detail (few will leave their fishing nets and follow Jesus physically) but in its essential character: people being formed into the image of Christ over time, in relationship, through teaching and practice.

## What "All Nations" Means

*Panta ta ethne* — all nations, all peoples. The scope is universal. The Greek *ethne* refers to ethnic and cultural groups — peoples, not just political nation-states. The commission is to every tribe, every language, every culture on earth.

This is the fulfillment of the promise to Abraham: "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:3). The Great Commission is not a new agenda. It is the arrival of the agenda God announced at the beginning of the covenant history. The people of God are the instrument through which God's blessing reaches every human group.

## Baptizing: The Entry Rite

Baptism is the rite of entry into the community — the visible, public identification with the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. It marks the beginning of the disciple's life in the new community, not the end of the process. The person who is baptized has begun — they have not arrived.

Baptism in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit is Trinitarian — and this is one of the fullest Trinitarian formulas in the Gospels. The new disciple is brought into relationship with God in his fullness, not a single dimension of the divine character.

## Teaching: The Formation Process

"Teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you." This is the ongoing work of discipleship. And the specific content is precise: *all that I have commanded you*. Not a selection of the comfortable parts. Not a version modified for cultural palatability. Everything Jesus commanded.

What did Jesus command? The Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7). The love of enemies (Matthew 5:44). The forgiveness of wrongs without limit (Matthew 18:22). The service of others as the definition of greatness (Matthew 20:26-28). The love of God with everything you are and the love of neighbor as yourself (Matthew 22:37-40). The care of the poor, the hungry, the stranger, the sick, the imprisoned (Matthew 25:35-36).

"Teaching them to *observe*" — *terein*, to keep, to guard, to practice. This is not teaching them to know about these things. It is teaching them to do them. The goal of Christian teaching is not informed Christians — it is obedient Christians. The measure of success is not whether someone can articulate the teaching but whether they are practicing it.

This is the rebuke of the church that produces theologically informed people who live like everyone else. Knowledge without formation is not discipleship.

## What the Commission Is Not

**It is not primarily about evangelism events.** Evangelism — the initial proclamation of the gospel — is a necessary part of the disciple-making process. But the commission does not end when someone responds to an altar call. It begins there. The decision to follow is the entry point. The commission calls for the full formation of a person into a Kingdom citizen.

**It is not about building churches as institutions.** Building churches is one of the means by which disciples are gathered, formed, and deployed. But the commission is not "build church attendance." It is "make disciples." These are not the same thing. A church full of regular attenders who are not being formed into disciples of Jesus has not fulfilled the commission regardless of its size.

**It is not optional.** "Go therefore" — *poreuthentes oun* — the "therefore" connects back to the authority statement. Because Jesus has all authority, you go. This is not an invitation to those who feel called to missions. It is the commission of every person in the community: reproduce the life of discipleship in others.

## The Promise That Closes the Commission

"And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."

*Idou* — behold, look, pay attention. This is not a footnote. It is the basis on which the commission is possible. You are not making disciples in your own strength, with your own wisdom, using your own resources. The One who sent you is with you — always, without interruption, to the end of the age.

The commission is enormous in its scope. It requires a lifetime. It demands formation, not just proclamation. It is aimed at all peoples on earth. And it is accompanied by the permanent, uninterrupted presence of the One who holds all authority in both heaven and earth.

You are not in this alone. The King is with you. Do the work.
