Few topics generate more heat and less light in Christian conversation than hell. Traditionalists insist on a literal eternal torment that brooks no nuance. Progressive revisers sometimes dismiss the concept entirely, treating it as a theological embarrassment inherited from a less enlightened era. Both approaches share a common problem: they argue more from tradition and reaction than from the actual text.

An honest treatment requires something neither camp typically offers: a careful reading of what Jesus actually said, using the actual words he used.

## Three Words, Not One

Most readers do not know that the English word "hell" translates three different Greek words in the New Testament — and those words refer to quite different realities. Conflating them produces enormous confusion.

### Hades

*Hades* is the Greek equivalent of the Hebrew *Sheol* — the realm of the dead, the place where the dead exist. It appears 10 times in the New Testament. It is not a place of punishment specifically. It is simply the abode of the dead — all the dead.

Jesus uses *Hades* in Matthew 16:18: "I will build my church, and the gates of Hades shall not prevail against it." The gates of Hades represent death's power — and Jesus is saying that death cannot overcome the community he is building. In Luke 16:23, the rich man in the parable is in *Hades* — and there it does include a place of torment for this particular figure. But *Hades* as a category is broader than punishment.

In Revelation 20:13-14, death and *Hades* are themselves thrown into the "lake of fire" — which suggests *Hades* is itself a temporary state, not the final destination. Whatever the final state is, *Hades* gives way to something else at the final judgment.

### Tartarus

*Tartaros* appears exactly once in the New Testament — 2 Peter 2:4: "For if God did not spare angels when they sinned, but cast them into hell (*tartaros*) and committed them to chains of gloomy darkness to be kept until the judgment..."

In Greek cosmology, Tartarus was the deepest abyss — the prison beneath even Hades, where the worst offenders were confined. Peter uses it specifically for the fallen angels — the spiritual beings who rebelled before humanity's fall. This is not a term Jesus uses, and it applies to a specific category of beings, not to humans.

### Gehenna

*Gehenna* is the word Jesus uses almost exclusively when he warns about hell-like judgment. It appears 12 times in the New Testament. Jesus uses it 11 of those 12 times. This is the word that deserves the most careful attention.

## Gehenna: The Historical Reality

*Gehenna* is a Greek transliteration of the Hebrew *Ge Hinnom* — the Valley of Hinnom. This was a real, specific, geographically identifiable place: a valley on the south side of Jerusalem.

Its history is dark. The Valley of Hinnom was the site of child sacrifice to the Canaanite god Molech — a practice the Hebrew prophets condemned in the sharpest terms (2 Kings 23:10, Jeremiah 7:31-32). King Josiah defiled it to prevent further use. By the time of Jesus, it had become, according to several scholars and historians, a perpetually burning waste dump for the city — a place where garbage, refuse, and the bodies of criminals were burned. The fire was constant. The worms were constant. The smoke was constant.

When Jesus used *Gehenna* as an image of judgment, his first-century Jewish audience did not hear a theological abstraction. They heard a reference to a real, visible, locally known place — a site of historic desecration, ongoing fire, and continuous decay. The image was immediately visceral and intelligible.

Jeremiah 7:32-33 prophesied: "The days are coming, declares the Lord, when this place will no longer be called Topheth or the Valley of Ben-Hinnom, but the Valley of Slaughter, for they will bury in Topheth until there is no more room." The valley had a history of judgment and death in the Jewish imagination. Jesus builds on that history.

## What Jesus Said: The Passages

Jesus uses *Gehenna* in ethical contexts — almost always as the consequence of moral choices, particularly choices that damage other people. Let us read the actual passages:

**Matthew 5:22:** "But I say to you that everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment; whoever insults his brother will be liable to the council; and whoever says, 'You fool!' will be liable to the hell (*Gehenna*) of fire."

**Matthew 5:29-30:** "If your right eye causes you to sin, tear it out and throw it away. For it is better that you lose one of your members than that your whole body be thrown into hell (*Gehenna*)."

**Matthew 10:28:** "And do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. Rather fear him who can destroy both soul and body in hell (*Gehenna*)."

**Matthew 23:15:** Jesus to the Pharisees: "You travel across sea and land to make a single proselyte, and when he becomes a proselyte, you make him twice as much a child of hell (*Gehenna*) as yourselves."

**Matthew 23:33:** "You serpents, you brood of vipers, how are you to escape being sentenced to hell (*Gehenna*)?"

**Mark 9:43, 45, 47:** Three uses in close succession about cutting off the offending hand, foot, or eye — "better to enter life crippled than to be thrown into hell (*Gehenna*), into the unquenchable fire."

## What Jesus Emphasizes

Several patterns emerge from reading the *Gehenna* passages together:

**The warnings are primarily ethical, not dogmatic.** Jesus does not say "whoever fails to say the right prayer goes to Gehenna." He says whoever harbors destructive anger, leads others into sin, exercises religious authority without integrity, or makes disciples into worse people than themselves. The Gehenna warnings are addressed primarily to the religious establishment and to people making choices that cause harm to others. This is not the same as a generic warning about unbelief.

**The language is relentlessly physical and vivid.** "Where their worm does not die and the fire is not quenched" (Mark 9:48, quoting Isaiah 66:24). Unquenchable fire. Outer darkness. Weeping and gnashing of teeth. Jesus does not apologize for the visceral imagery. He uses it to communicate that the consequences of moral choices are real, serious, and not recoverable.

**The urgency is practical.** The images of cutting off a hand or plucking out an eye (hyperbole, not instruction for self-mutilation) emphasize that present moral choices have ultimate consequences. The seriousness of the consequences justifies the seriousness of the present response.

**The audience matters.** Most of the sharpest Gehenna warnings are directed at the Pharisees and religious leaders. The people who have accumulated moral and spiritual authority and have used it to burden and mislead others receive the harshest language about judgment. This is not incidental.

## What the Text Does Not Say

An honest reading also requires noting what Jesus does not provide in these passages:

- **A detailed map of the afterlife.** Jesus does not give a systematic geography of post-mortem existence. He uses imagery to communicate moral seriousness.
- **A clear statement on the duration of Gehenna for individuals.** The word *aionios* (often translated "eternal") when applied to punishment in Matthew 25:46 is the same word used for eternal life — and its primary meaning is qualitative, relating to the age to come, not simply "lasts forever."
- **An explanation of the mechanics of judgment.** What happens to whom, when, and on what basis is described in general terms, not as a systematic theological manual.

## Why the Distinction Matters

This matters for several reasons:

A man who preaches Gehenna as if it is a simple concept — "bad people burn forever" — will produce fear-based faith that does not hold up under examination or hardship. The text is more nuanced than that.

A man who dismisses the concept entirely — because it makes modern people uncomfortable — has decided that Jesus's ethical seriousness was either mistaken or exaggerated. He has not actually read what Jesus said.

The honest position is: Jesus used the imagery of Gehenna seriously and repeatedly to communicate that moral choices have ultimate consequences, that human behavior toward others matters enormously to God, and that the man who has been entrusted with religious authority and uses it to harm rather than serve faces a particularly severe reckoning. Jesus took judgment seriously. His followers should too — without the sensationalism that turns doctrine into theater, and without the revisionism that turns it into nothing.

## The Right Fear

"Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. Rather fear him who can destroy both soul and body in hell." (Matthew 10:28)

The fear Jesus calls for is not the fearful paralysis of a man terrified of a capricious God. It is the appropriate reverence of a man who understands that what he does with his life, his authority, his influence, and his moral choices has weight that exceeds the present moment. The One who assigns ultimate value is the One whose assessment matters.

That sober awareness — held without sensationalism, without evasion, and with the full weight Jesus placed on it — is the honest response to what Jesus actually said.
