"Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her... In the same way husbands should love their wives as their own bodies." — Ephesians 5:25, 28

Most men reading this verse hear the leadership clause and miss the model. The model is Christ on the way to the cross. Christ gave himself up. He did not extract from the church — he poured himself into her at his own cost. Ephesians 5 leadership is not the leadership of demand. It is the leadership of sacrifice.

This matters specifically for the man who feels like a failure. He hears "lead your wife" and thinks of a competent, successful, confident version of himself that does not exist. He looks at where he actually is — financially strained, spiritually dry, marriage strained, fathering inconsistently — and concludes he cannot lead. So he abdicates. He goes quiet. He withdraws. He stops trying because he no longer believes he is the kind of man who can.

This is the wrong response. Failure does not disqualify a man from leadership. Refusal to repent disqualifies. Withdrawal disqualifies. Hiding disqualifies. The man who has failed and is honestly engaging the failure is more qualified to lead than the man who appears successful and is hiding the failures he has not faced.

## The David Model

King David is the most useful Old Testament example because he failed publicly and catastrophically, and he continued to lead.

2 Samuel 11. He sleeps with Bathsheba, the wife of one of his most loyal men. She becomes pregnant. He tries to cover it. When the cover-up fails, he arranges for Uriah to be killed in battle. He marries Bathsheba. He thinks it is closed.

The prophet Nathan confronts him in chapter 12. David's response in verse 13 is the model: "I have sinned against the Lord."

No defense. No qualifying it. No "but I was under pressure." Direct acknowledgment.

Then Psalm 51, written in the aftermath: "Have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love... wash me thoroughly from my iniquity, and cleanse me from my sin. For I know my transgressions, and my sin is ever before me."

This is what biblical repentance sounds like. Specific. Named. Honest. No performance. No minimizing.

David did not stop being king. He did not stop being a father (though his fathering took serious hits as a consequence of his sin). He did not abdicate the throne. He continued to lead — from a position of acknowledged failure that he had honestly repented of.

This is the pattern. Failure plus repentance does not disqualify. Failure plus denial does.

## The Specific Failures Most Men Carry

The man who feels like a failure to his wife usually has a specific list. The list often includes some combination of these.

- He has not been present — physically there but mentally elsewhere for years.
- He has been critical or distant when she needed warmth.
- He has prioritized work or hobbies over her consistently.
- He has been emotionally avoidant — not engaging hard conversations.
- He has been spiritually passive — not leading prayer, Scripture, or worship in the home.
- He has been harsh — words spoken in anger that have not been retracted.
- He has been unfaithful — at the level of imagination, or further.
- He has been irresponsible with money, time, or commitments.

Most husbands can identify themselves in at least two of these. Some can identify themselves in most. The shame the list produces is often what keeps the man stuck. He cannot see how to come back from where he has gone, so he stays where he is and the marriage continues to decline.

The path back is not complicated. It is hard, but not complicated.

## The Path Back

### Step 1: Get Honest Before God

Before you say anything to her, get clear before God. Sit with the list. Name it specifically. 1 John 1:9 — confess and receive forgiveness. This is the foundation. You cannot repent to your wife from a posture of shame and self-loathing. You repent from a posture of forgiven brokenness.

Pray Psalm 51 in your own words. Let the Father deal with you first.

### Step 2: Repent Specifically to Her

This is the hard step. Most men try to skip it. They want to "do better going forward" without saying the words.

The words have to be said. Schedule a conversation. Not on the way out the door. Not during a fight. A specific time, a private place, your full attention.

Then say specific things. Not "I've been a bad husband." That is too general to land. Say "For the last three years I have not been spiritually leading our home, and that has cost you. I have been emotionally distant when you have tried to talk to me about hard things. I have prioritized [specific thing] over you, and I have seen you feel less important. I am sorry. I was wrong. I am turning."

Whatever the specifics are, name them. The specificity is what makes the repentance real. Generic apology lets you off the hook. Specific repentance puts the actual failures on the table.

### Step 3: Receive Her Response

Her response will not be what you imagine. She may receive it gratefully. She may be skeptical because she has heard apologies before that did not stick. She may be angry — anger that has been buried for years often surfaces when the door is opened. She may need time.

Do not defend yourself. Do not argue with her response. Receive it. Whatever she says, let her say it. You are not in a position to manage her reaction. You are in a position to absorb it.

This is what Ephesians 5 sacrificial love looks like in practice. You are bearing the cost of your own failures by letting her express what they have cost her.

### Step 4: Change the Conditions

Words without changed action are not repentance. They are noise.

What needs to change structurally? Identify the one or two things that, if you actually did them consistently, would address the specific failures you have named.

- If you have not been spiritually leading, start a weekly time of prayer and Scripture with her, or open the day with a brief shared prayer.
- If you have not been present, set hard limits on the work or the hobby and put her on the calendar.
- If you have been harsh, learn to pause before responding, get into accountability around the temper, take it to a counselor if needed.
- If money has been mismanaged, build a budget together and bring her into the decisions.

The structural changes are what make the repentance credible. Without them, the conversation was just a verbal pass.

### Step 5: Pursue Her Spiritually

Ephesians 5:26 — Christ loved the church "that he might sanctify her." The husband's role includes the spiritual flourishing of his wife. This is not patronizing. It is practical.

Pray for her by name daily. Pray with her at some point in the week. Read Scripture together — even ten minutes once a week is a start. Bring her into the spiritual life of the home, do not assume she will manage it without you.

This is the long game. Years of consistent spiritual pursuit of your wife will change the marriage in ways no other intervention can.

## The Frame That Sets Men Free

The man who is leading his wife from a posture of acknowledged weakness, ongoing repentance, and consistent pursuit is doing what Ephesians 5 actually requires. The polished, never-failed leader is a fiction. The real Kingdom husband is a forgiven man who keeps showing up, keeps owning his failures, keeps changing, keeps loving.

This is the [kingdom authority](/2026/05/12/lead-like-a-king/) Christ modeled in John 13. Authority secure enough to wash feet. Authority secure enough to acknowledge failure without losing the throne. Authority that flows from the cross, not from competence.

Your wife does not need a perfect husband. She needs a real one — one who tells the truth, owns the failures, fights for her, and does not stop fighting because he had a bad year or a bad decade.

Start today. Get honest with God. Schedule the conversation. Say the specific things. Change the conditions. Pursue her spiritually for the rest of your life.

This is what leading like a king looks like when the king has failed. Repent, get up, and keep building.
