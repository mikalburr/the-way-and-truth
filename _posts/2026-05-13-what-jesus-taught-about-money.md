Jesus talked about money more than he talked about heaven and hell combined. More than prayer. More than faith. Roughly one in ten verses in the Synoptic Gospels touches on money, wealth, or possessions. If you have not wrestled seriously with what Jesus actually taught on this subject, you have a significant blind spot in your understanding of his message.

This is not a prosperity gospel article. It is also not an argument for Christian poverty. It is an attempt to read what Jesus actually said, in context, without the filter of either tradition.

## The Central Problem: Two Masters

"No one can serve two masters. Either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money." (Matthew 6:24, BSB)

The word translated "money" here is *mamōnas* — Mammon. It is an Aramaic loanword that appears in Greek New Testament texts, which strongly suggests it comes from Jesus's own Aramaic speech. It refers not simply to money as a medium of exchange but to wealth as a system, as a competing loyalty, as a force that commands allegiance.

Notice what Jesus does not say. He does not say money is evil. He does not say you should not have money. He says you cannot *serve* two masters — *douleuein* is the verb, the same word used for slavery. The issue is not possession but ownership: who owns you?

The logic is binary because divided loyalty in this domain is functionally impossible. A slave cannot serve two masters simultaneously. At the moment of conflict — when God's demands and financial interests diverge — you will serve one and betray the other. You can try to keep both happy while the interests align, but the moment of truth will come.

The question Jesus forces is not "How much money do you have?" but "Who serves whom?"

## Faithful in the Unrighteous Mammon (Luke 16:10-11)

"Whoever is faithful in very little is also faithful in much, and whoever is dishonest in very little is also dishonest in much. If then you have not been faithful with the unrighteous wealth, who will entrust to you the true riches?" (Luke 16:10-11, BSB)

The phrase "unrighteous wealth" (*adiko mamōna*) is striking. Jesus calls money unrighteous — not because money itself is sinful, but because it exists in a fallen, unjust system. All money has been touched by broken human systems. It is tainted by association with a world not yet redeemed.

But — and this is the point often missed — Jesus does not say to avoid it. He says to be *faithful* with it. The word *pistos* — faithful, trustworthy, reliable — is the criterion. How you handle money in a fallen economy is a test of whether you can be trusted with the genuine riches of the Kingdom.

This is a stewardship theology, not a poverty theology. Money is given to you in trust. What you do with it — whether you use it for Kingdom purposes or hoard it, exploit it, or let it own you — reveals your actual priorities and your actual fitness for Kingdom responsibility.

## Life Is Not in Abundance of Possessions (Luke 12:15)

"Watch out! Guard yourself against all covetousness, for one's life does not consist in the abundance of one's possessions." (Luke 12:15, BSB)

The Greek word *pleonexia* — covetousness, greed — literally means "wanting more." Jesus issues a rare double imperative here: *horate* and *phulassesthe* — "watch" and "guard yourselves." The intensity of the warning signals that this is a particularly subtle and dangerous temptation.

The following parable (Luke 12:16-21) makes the point concrete: a rich farmer has such an abundant harvest that he tears down his barns to build bigger ones, planning to retire on his wealth. God calls him a fool: "This very night your soul is required of you, and the things you have prepared, whose will they be?" (v. 20). The man had planned an entire future around wealth that, at the moment of death, was simply abandoned.

Jesus adds the interpretation: "So is the one who lays up treasure for himself and is not rich toward God" (v. 21). The contrast is *laying up for yourself* versus being *rich toward God*. The first is hoarding in self-interest. The second is a posture of generosity and Kingdom investment.

Note what Jesus does not say: he does not say the man was wrong to be rich. The harvest was legitimate. The abundance was real. The foolishness was in treating it as the foundation of life and making no investment toward what outlasts death.

## The Rich Young Ruler: The Point Is Not Poverty

Mark 10:17-22 records the encounter with the rich young man who kept all the commandments. Jesus "looked at him and loved him" (*emblepō*, a searching, direct gaze — and *ēgapēsen*, the same word used for God's love) and then told him to sell everything, give to the poor, and follow him.

This has been used to argue that Jesus demands literal poverty of all his followers. That reading does not hold.

When the man leaves grieving (v. 22), the disciples are astonished. Jesus says it is "easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person to enter the kingdom of God" (v. 25). They ask: "Then who can be saved?" Jesus answers: "With man it is impossible, but not with God. For all things are possible with God" (v. 27).

The issue here is not a universal command to divest. The issue is that this particular man's wealth was the thing holding him back from total allegiance. Jesus, who loved him, identified the specific idol and named it. For some people, that idol is money. For others it is something else. Jesus does not give every person this instruction. He gives it to the man whose soul required it.

Zacchaeus (Luke 19:1-10) gives away half his goods and repays fourfold what he stole — and Jesus declares salvation has come to his house. Not all. Half, plus restitution. Matthew (Levi) follows Jesus but there is no record of him liquidating his wealth. The demand fits the person.

What is consistent across all cases is the transfer of ownership. Whether you retain financial assets or give them away, they cannot own you.

## The Sermon's Economic Teaching (Matthew 6:19-34)

Matthew 6 contains the most sustained economic teaching in the Gospels. In sequence:

- **vv. 19-21**: Don't store up earthly treasure. Store up heavenly treasure. Where your treasure is, your heart will be. The heart follows the investment.
- **vv. 22-23**: The eye is the lamp of the body — if your eye is *haplous* (single, generous, focused) you are full of light; if it is *ponēros* (evil, envious, grudging) you are in darkness. The metaphor of the eye connects to how you *see* money and possessions.
- **v. 24**: Two masters — you cannot serve both.
- **vv. 25-34**: Do not be anxious about food, clothing, or provision. God feeds the birds. God clothes the grass. You are worth more than these. "Seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you" (v. 33).

The economic teaching of the Sermon is not asceticism. It is reordering. Put the Kingdom first — genuinely first, not rhetorically first — and the anxiety about provision dissolves because you are trusting the provision of a Father who knows what you need (v. 32).

## What This Actually Demands

Jesus's teaching on money is not primarily about how much you have. It is about who is in charge.

The questions his teaching generates:

1. Does your financial decision-making reflect trust in God's provision, or does it reflect the same anxious self-protection as people who have no Father watching over them?
2. Are you using money to advance the Kingdom, or accumulating it primarily for personal security?
3. If God asked you to give away a specific, painful amount — would you? Or has wealth quietly become an untouchable area of your life?
4. Does your giving reflect what is genuinely costly, or what is comfortable?

Jesus does not promise that following him will make you rich. He does promise that those who seek the Kingdom first will have what they need (Matthew 6:33), and that those who give generously will receive generously (Luke 6:38). These are not financial formulas. They are descriptions of a life lived under the economy of the Kingdom rather than the economy of the age.

The difference between the two economies is not the dollar amount. It is the master.

You cannot serve two of them. This is not a metaphor.
