If you have never read the Gospels straight through — or if you have read them but always through someone else's filter — this article is for you. No assumed theology. No prerequisites. Just the most direct path into the words of Jesus for someone starting from zero.

The question is not complicated: where do you start? The answer is specific: you start with Mark, then you move to Matthew 5-7, then you read John 14-17. Then you repeat. That reading sequence, done slowly and honestly, will give you more genuine understanding of Jesus's teachings than most people acquire in years of church attendance.

Here is why, and how.

## Start with Mark

The Gospel of Mark is the shortest of the four Gospels — sixteen chapters, around eleven thousand words in Greek. A motivated reader can finish it in one sitting of two to three hours.

Mark is the most immediate of the Gospels. Where Matthew is organized by theme and Luke is comprehensive and literary, Mark is urgent. The Greek word *euthys* — translated "immediately," "at once," or "straightway" — appears approximately forty times in Mark alone. The narrative moves fast. Jesus heals, Jesus teaches, Jesus confronts, Jesus moves on.

This velocity serves the beginner well. You don't get bogged down in extended discourses before you've developed a feel for who Jesus is in action. Mark shows you Jesus in motion first. You see him silence a demon (Mark 1:25), heal a leper by touch (Mark 1:41), calm a storm with a verbal command (Mark 4:39), raise a dead girl (Mark 5:41-42), and walk on water (Mark 6:48-50) — all before you're halfway through the book.

By the time you reach his teaching on the cost of discipleship in Mark 8:34-38, you already know who is speaking. The authority of the teaching is grounded in the authority you have witnessed in action.

Mark 1:14-15 is the mission statement: *"The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news."* Everything in Mark is an unpacking of that announcement.

**How to read Mark:** Read it in one sitting if possible. If not, in two sittings at most. Don't pause to analyze. Read it the way you'd read a story — let the narrative carry you. After you finish, sit for five minutes and write down three things Jesus did or said that surprised you. Those surprises are your entry points for deeper study.

## Then Matthew 5-7: The Sermon on the Mount

Once you have a feel for Jesus's character and action in Mark, you are ready for the densest teaching block in the Gospels: Matthew 5-7, the Sermon on the Mount.

This is approximately 111 verses. Careful, unhurried reading takes about 20-30 minutes. Plan to read it three times before moving on. The first reading is for orientation — getting the shape of the whole. The second reading is for detail — noticing what Jesus actually says in each section. The third reading is for application — asking what this demands of you specifically.

The Sermon opens with the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3-12). The word "blessed" is the Greek *makarios* — a declaration of genuine flourishing, of being in a state of Kingdom favor. Jesus is not saying "try to be like this." He is describing people who already are in Kingdom reality.

*"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."*
*"Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted."*
*"Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth."*

These are not comfort statements. They are claims about who genuinely has the Kingdom — and the list is striking. The poor in spirit, the mourning, the meek, the persecuted. Not the successful, the comfortable, the powerful, the religiously celebrated.

The Sermon then moves through a series of escalations: *"You have heard that it was said... but I tell you..."* (Matthew 5:21-48). Each time, Jesus takes a command from the Torah and pushes it past the behavior level to the heart level. Don't murder — but also don't nurse contempt. Don't commit adultery — but also don't cultivate lust. This is not legalism made stricter; it is the difference between managing symptoms and treating the disease.

Matthew 6 covers the three pillars of Jewish piety — giving, prayer, and fasting — and in each case Jesus's concern is the same: are you doing this before God or before men? Then comes the teaching on money and anxiety: *"No one can serve two masters"* (Matthew 6:24). *"Seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well"* (Matthew 6:33).

Matthew 7 closes with warnings against judgment, assurance about prayer, and the famous narrow gate (Matthew 7:13-14). The Sermon ends with the parable of the wise and foolish builders (Matthew 7:24-27) — the entire Sermon distilled: hearing these words *and putting them into practice* is the difference between a house that stands and a house that collapses.

**How to read the Sermon on the Mount:** Read it slowly. When a verse stops you — because it confuses you, or challenges you, or you don't believe it — don't skip over it. Write it down. That friction is where the real learning happens.

## Then John 14-17: The Farewell Discourse

After Mark and the Sermon on the Mount, you are ready for the most intimate teaching in the Gospels: John 14-17, the farewell discourse.

Jesus is at the Last Supper, hours from his arrest. He knows what is coming. His disciples do not. He spends four chapters — John 14 through 17 — preparing them for life in his absence and in the presence of the Holy Spirit.

John 14 opens with one of the most quoted and least understood verses in the Gospels: *"Do not let your hearts be troubled. You believe in God; believe also in me. My Father's house has many rooms; if that were not so, would I have told you that I am going there to prepare a place for you?"* (John 14:1-2).

The Greek word translated "rooms" or "mansions" (KJV) is *monai* — dwelling places, abiding places. The same root as *meno* — to abide, to remain. Jesus is not describing luxury real estate in the afterlife. He is describing permanent dwelling with the Father. That dwelling is what he is going to make possible.

John 14:6 is the most exclusive claim in the Gospels: *"I am the way and the truth and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."* Read it plainly. He is not offering a path to religion. He is claiming to be the exclusive access point to the Father.

John 15 gives the architectural metaphor for Christian life: the vine and the branches. *"I am the vine; you are the branches. If you remain in me and I in you, you will bear much fruit; apart from me you can do nothing"* (John 15:5). The word *meno* — abide, remain — appears eleven times in John 15:1-17. Remaining in Jesus is not a spiritual feeling; it is the structural reality of Kingdom life.

John 16 introduces the Holy Spirit as *Paraclete* — the Greek *parakletos* means one called alongside to help, advocate, counselor. He will guide believers into all truth (John 16:13).

John 17 is the High Priestly Prayer — Jesus praying for his disciples and for all who will believe through them. John 17:3 is the definition of eternal life: *"Now this is eternal life: that they know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom you have sent."* Not duration. Relationship. Present-tense relationship.

**How to read John 14-17:** Slowly. One chapter per sitting. These are intimate chapters — they reward meditation more than speed. When Jesus makes a promise (*"I will do whatever you ask in my name"* — John 14:13), stop and take it seriously. When he makes a demand (*"If you love me, keep my commands"* — John 14:15), stop and take it seriously.

## What to Do When You Don't Understand Something

You will not understand everything. This is certain. The disciples who were physically present, hearing him in person, repeatedly didn't understand him (Mark 9:32, Luke 18:34, John 12:16). Jesus was patient with that.

The most important thing to do with confusion is to sit with it. Not around it — through it. Write the confusing verse down. Read it again in two or three different translations (the BSB, the ESV, and The Message will give you three different registers of the same text). Ask: What is the simplest possible reading of this? What is the most demanding possible reading?

Do not rush to commentary. Do not Google the verse and take the first answer. Let the confusion stay uncomfortable for a few days. Most confusion resolves with re-reading — the context that was missing becomes visible, or a parallel passage in another Gospel clarifies the one that confused you.

When the confusion persists, then consult a resource like Matthew Henry's Commentary (verse-by-verse, accessible, application-focused) or BibleHub.com (where you can see multiple commentaries on a single verse side by side).

## The Goal Is Encounter, Not Information

The goal of reading the Gospels is not to acquire information about Jesus. It is to encounter him — to hear his voice, in his words, making claims on your life.

He said in John 10:27: *"My sheep listen to my voice; I know them, and they follow me."*

The hearing is the beginning of the following. This reading sequence — Mark, Matthew 5-7, John 14-17 — gives you the clearest path to that hearing. Start here. Read slowly. Come back and read again. The teaching will deepen with every return.
