The Psalms are the prayer book of the Bible. For three thousand years they have shaped how God's people speak to him — in joy, in lament, in confusion, in confidence, in rage at injustice and in repentance over sin. No Christian man is fully formed who has not learned to pray the Psalms.

These five books cover the Psalms seriously. Some are commentaries. Some are theological studies. One is a daily devotional. All of them will deepen your reading and your prayer.

---

## 1. Kidner's Two-Volume Psalms Commentary — Derek Kidner

{% include book-card.html

  author="Derek Kidner"
  asin="0830842608"
  description="The classic concise commentary on the Psalms — two slim volumes that pack more theological insight per page than many works ten times their length. Kidner's prose is precise, his exegesis careful, and his pastoral instincts unusually sound."
  verdict="The best concise commentary on the Psalms in print. If you can only own one Psalms commentary, make it Kidner's two volumes."
%}

**What it covers:** A psalm-by-psalm commentary across two volumes in the Tyndale Old Testament Commentaries series. Volume 1 covers Psalms 1–72; Volume 2 covers Psalms 73–150 (ASIN 0830842616). Each psalm gets an introduction noting structure and themes, followed by verse-by-verse exposition with attention to the Hebrew text and the major interpretive options.

**Who it's for:** Anyone who reads the Psalms seriously. Kidner is accessible enough for the layman and substantial enough for the pastor. Many serious students of Scripture have used these two volumes as their primary Psalms reference for decades.

**What it adds:** Density without obscurity. Kidner has the rare gift of saying more in a paragraph than other commentators say in a chapter. His introductions to each psalm orient you instantly to its structure and message. His individual verse comments reward slow reading.

**One honest note:** The Tyndale commentaries are intentionally short — Kidner had a strict word limit and could not develop every theme as far as he might have liked. For longer treatments, supplement with Wenham, Goldingay, or one of the major scholarly commentaries (Goldingay's three-volume BECOT, for instance). Kidner is the foundation, not the ceiling.

## 2. Psalms as Torah — Gordon Wenham

{% include book-card.html
  title="Psalms as Torah: Reading Biblical Song Ethically"
  author="Gordon J. Wenham"
  asin="0801031680"
  description="Wenham's landmark theological study of the Psalms as a book of ethical formation. The Psalms do not just express prayer — they shape the moral and spiritual imagination of those who pray them. A genuinely original work of biblical theology."
  verdict="Essential for understanding what the Psalms are doing — not just describing prayer but training the soul. Read this after Kidner."
%}

**What it covers:** Wenham's central argument is that the Psalter functions as torah — instruction — for God's people. The Psalms are not random songs gathered into a book. They are arranged to shape the praying community ethically and theologically. Wenham examines the structure of the Psalter, the role of the imprecatory psalms, the relationship between Psalms and Wisdom literature, and the way the Psalms instruct as well as express.

**Who it's for:** Men who have read the Psalms devotionally for years and want a deeper account of what is actually happening when they pray them. Also pastors and teachers who want to understand why the Psalms have the power they have.

**What it adds:** The ethical and formational dimension. Most books on the Psalms focus on expression — what the psalmist felt, what we feel, how the psalms give voice to our emotions. Wenham shifts the focus to formation: what the psalms do to us when we pray them. That shift changes everything.

**One honest note:** Wenham writes as a scholar engaging other scholars. The book is more technical than the others on this list, with extensive footnotes and engagement with academic debates. The argument is worth the work, but be ready to read slowly.

## 3. Psalms for Everyone — John Goldingay

{% include book-card.html
  title="Psalms for Everyone, Part 1: Psalms 1-72"
  author="John Goldingay"
  asin="0664233767"
  description="Goldingay's accessible two-part commentary on the Psalms in the For Everyone series. A leading Old Testament scholar writing for ordinary readers — careful translation, pastoral application, and an unusual gift for showing the relevance of ancient prayer to modern life."
  verdict="The best accessible commentary for a daily Psalms reading practice. Goldingay reads the Psalms with you, not at you."
%}

**What it covers:** A psalm-by-psalm devotional commentary in the For Everyone series. Goldingay gives his own translation of each psalm, followed by an introductory anecdote or reflection and a careful but accessible exposition. Part 1 covers Psalms 1–72; a parallel volume covers 73–150.

**Who it's for:** Men who want to read through the Psalms with a daily guide that is more substantial than a devotional and more accessible than a technical commentary. Goldingay is a leading scholar writing intentionally for non-specialists. The accessibility is not at the expense of theological depth.

**What it adds:** A daily voice. Goldingay does not try to settle every interpretive question. He focuses on what the psalm is doing — the shape of its prayer, the movement of its emotion, the theology it embodies — and helps the reader pray with it. The opening anecdotes from his own life and pastoral experience are unusually well-judged.

**One honest note:** Goldingay's translations are intentionally fresh and sometimes idiosyncratic. They are designed to make you hear the Hebrew differently. Treat them as supplements to a standard translation (ESV, NIV, NRSV), not replacements.

## 4. Answering God — Eugene Peterson

{% include book-card.html
  title="Answering God: The Psalms as Tools for Prayer"
  author="Eugene H. Peterson"
  asin="0060665122"
  description="Peterson's classic short treatment of how the Psalms teach Christians to pray. Not a commentary but a theological and pastoral guide to using the Psalms as the primary school of prayer for the church."
  verdict="The book that has shaped a generation of pastors' use of the Psalms. Read it before you build your own Psalms practice."
%}

**What it covers:** A short, focused book on how to use the Psalms as the foundation of a prayer life. Peterson examines the language of the Psalms, the structure of the Psalter, the role of memorization, and the way the Psalms train the praying community in honesty before God. The book is built on Peterson's conviction that the Psalms are the church's basic textbook on prayer.

**Who it's for:** Men who pray inconsistently, vaguely, or only when they need something — and suspect there is more to prayer than their current practice. Peterson does not lecture; he models. By the end of the book, you understand why the Psalms have been the prayer book of the church for two thousand years.

**What it adds:** The case for the Psalms as the primary school of prayer. Peterson argues that we do not learn to pray well by inventing our own words. We learn by praying the Psalms — letting them give us the language, the categories, and the emotional range that we lack. That argument has changed the prayer life of many serious readers.

**One honest note:** Peterson's prose is impressionistic and pastoral, not systematic. If you want exegetical commentary, this is not it. Read it as the spiritual and theological framing — then go to Kidner, Wenham, or Goldingay for the work on individual psalms.

## 5. The Songs of Jesus — Tim Keller

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Songs of Jesus: A Year of Daily Devotions in the Psalms"
  author="Timothy Keller and Kathy Keller"
  asin="0735222088"
  description="A year-long daily devotional through all 150 psalms by Tim and Kathy Keller. Each day covers a short section of a psalm with brief exposition and a prayer based on the text. The best daily Psalms practice in print."
  verdict="The daily companion. Buy a copy, work through it for a year, then start over. The format works."
%}

**What it covers:** A 365-day devotional that walks through the entire Psalter. Each day's reading covers a short section of a psalm — sometimes a few verses, sometimes a single image — with a brief reflection and a prayer prompted by the text. The Kellers cover every psalm; longer psalms take multiple days.

**Who it's for:** Men who want a sustainable daily practice in the Psalms. The format is short enough to read consistently and substantive enough to actually feed you. Many men have made this their primary daily devotional for years.

**What it adds:** Consistency. The structure forces you through all 150 psalms in a year — including the ones you would otherwise skip (the imprecatory psalms, the long historical recitals, the ones that feel obscure). By the end of the year, you have prayed the whole Psalter. Then you start over.

**One honest note:** The Kellers' reflections are short by design. This is not a commentary — it is a guide to a daily practice. Pair it with Kidner or Goldingay if you want deeper exposition on the days when the psalm catches you.

## How to Use This List

The Psalms are the one part of the Bible explicitly designed for repeated, lifelong, daily use. Build a practice.

**Read first:** Peterson, *Answering God*. Set the frame before you build the habit.

**Read second:** Keller, *The Songs of Jesus*. Use it daily for a year. Start over.

**Read for depth:** Kidner's two volumes. Consult them whenever a psalm needs more than a devotional reading.

**Read for theology:** Wenham, *Psalms as Torah*. Understand what the Psalms are doing.

**Read for breadth:** Goldingay's For Everyone volumes. Use them as a second daily voice alongside Keller.

The Psalms are not a part of the Bible you finish. They are a part of the Bible you live in.
