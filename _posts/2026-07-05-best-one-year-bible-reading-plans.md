Most men who set out to read the Bible in a year quit by February. Not because they lack discipline. Because they picked the wrong plan.

The plan you choose determines whether you finish. A reading plan that fits your life, your reading speed, and your current biblical literacy is the difference between completing the year and adding one more failed attempt to the pile.

These five plans are the best ones in print. I have used four of them and consulted with men who have used the fifth. Here they are, with honest assessments of who each one is actually for.

---

## 1. ESV One Year Bible

{% include book-card.html
  title="ESV One Year Bible"
  author="Crossway"
  asin="1433520540"
  description="The entire Bible divided into 365 daily readings, each containing an Old Testament passage, a New Testament passage, a Psalm, and a Proverbs reading. The most balanced single-volume format on the market."
  verdict="The best one-year Bible for first-time completers. Balanced, readable, and structured for finishing."
%}

**What it covers:** The full ESV Bible distributed across 365 daily readings. Each day includes roughly 3-4 chapters of Old Testament, 1 chapter of New Testament, a portion of Psalms, and a portion of Proverbs. The whole Bible. Every word. In twelve months.

**Who it's for:** Men who have never finished a Bible-in-a-year plan and want a format that helps them succeed. The four-stream structure means you never spend two months in Leviticus or Chronicles — there is always New Testament and Psalms on the same page.

**What it adds:** Format that reduces friction. The dated page format means there is no decision-making each morning. Open to today's date. Read the four sections. Done. Roughly 15-20 minutes per day at average reading speed.

**One honest note:** The four-stream design means you are reading four storylines in parallel rather than one continuous story. For first-time readers this is an advantage. For experienced readers wanting narrative immersion, a chronological plan may serve better.

## 2. NIV One Year Bible

{% include book-card.html
  title="NIV One Year Bible"
  author="Tyndale House Publishers"
  asin="1414302207"
  description="The same four-stream daily format as the ESV, in the more dynamic NIV translation. Reads faster and more conversationally — useful for men who find the ESV slightly stiff."
  verdict="The most readable one-year Bible for men who want a translation that flows. The format is identical to the ESV — choose based on translation preference."
%}

**What it covers:** Identical daily structure to the ESV One Year Bible — Old Testament, New Testament, Psalms, and Proverbs in each day's reading. The translation is the 2011 NIV, which prioritizes natural English readability over formal word-for-word correspondence with the original languages.

**Who it's for:** Men who find the ESV slightly stiff and want a translation that reads more like contemporary English. Also excellent for men reading the Bible aloud — to children, in family devotions, or as a daily spoken practice.

**What it adds:** Pace. The NIV reads roughly 15-20% faster than the ESV for most readers because the sentence structures are more familiar to modern English speakers. That margin matters when daily consistency is your primary goal.

**One honest note:** The 2011 NIV makes some gender-inclusive translation choices ("brothers and sisters" where the Greek has *adelphoi*) that more literal translations render simply as "brothers." Whether that is a feature or a bug depends on your translation philosophy. Be aware of it.

## 3. The One Year Chronological Bible (NLT)

{% include book-card.html
  title="The One Year Chronological Bible (NLT)"
  author="Tyndale House Publishers"
  asin="1414309414"
  description="The entire Bible reordered into the historical sequence of events. Job appears inside Genesis. Psalms appear alongside the events that produced them. The prophets are inserted into the historical books they addressed."
  verdict="The single best reading plan for understanding the Bible as one unfolding historical story. The chronological reordering is the value."
%}

**What it covers:** The full Bible in chronological order rather than canonical order. The events of Old Testament history are presented in the sequence they occurred, with Psalms and prophetic books inserted at the historical moments they relate to. The four Gospels are interleaved into a unified narrative of Jesus's life.

**Who it's for:** Men who have already read the Bible at least once cover-to-cover and want a new perspective. The chronological reordering surfaces connections that the canonical order obscures — David's Psalms read differently when you read them next to the events in 1 Samuel that produced them.

**What it adds:** Narrative coherence. The Bible is fundamentally one story, not 66 books. Reading it chronologically lets the story unify. You see the prophets speaking *into* the political situations recorded in Kings and Chronicles. You see Paul's letters in the context of the missionary journeys in Acts. The whole thing becomes one tapestry.

**One honest note:** The NLT is a thought-for-thought translation, more paraphrastic than the NIV. For close study, use an ESV or NIV chronological as well — the chronological reordering is the value here, not the translation itself.

## 4. The Daily Bible (NIV) — F. LaGard Smith

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Daily Bible In Chronological Order (NIV)"
  author="F. LaGard Smith"
  asin="0736902031"
  description="Chronological one-year plan with extensive narrative commentary linking sections, transitions, and historical context. The most heavily edited and contextualized chronological Bible on this list."
  verdict="The best chronological Bible for men who want narrative guidance alongside the text. Smith's commentary genuinely helps the story cohere."
%}

**What it covers:** The full NIV Bible arranged chronologically, with F. LaGard Smith's narrative commentary providing context, transitions, and historical framing between sections. Smith is a careful biblical scholar and his framing is consistently sound. The volume reads more like a narrated biblical history than a standard reading Bible.

**Who it's for:** Men who have struggled with chronological reading plans because the section transitions felt jarring or unexplained. Smith's commentary smooths those transitions and gives you the bigger-picture orientation that helps the year hold together.

**What it adds:** Editorial scaffolding. The chronological structure of Scripture is not always self-evident from the text alone — the prophets in particular require historical context to place. Smith does that placement work for you.

**One honest note:** Smith's commentary, while sound, is one scholar's framing. If you want the chronological reordering without the editorial overlay, the Tyndale *One Year Chronological Bible* gives you the same structure with less commentary. Choose based on whether you want a guide or just the text.

## 5. M'Cheyne Reading Plan (via the Two-Year ESV Daily Bible or printed plan)

{% include book-card.html
  title="ESV Daily Reading Bible (M'Cheyne Plan)"
  author="Robert Murray M'Cheyne (plan), Crossway (edition)"
  asin="1581349319"
  description="Robert Murray M'Cheyne's classic 1842 reading plan, which takes you through the entire Old Testament once and the New Testament and Psalms twice in a single year, across four daily readings."
  verdict="The most demanding and most spiritually formative plan on this list. Save it for your second or third year — but get there."
%}

**What it covers:** M'Cheyne's plan, originally written for his Dundee congregation in 1842, divides each day into four readings: two from the Old Testament, one from the New Testament, and one from the Psalms or Gospels. Over the course of a year, you read the Old Testament once and the New Testament and Psalms twice. The total daily volume is roughly 4 chapters.

**Who it's for:** Men who have completed at least one previous Bible-in-a-year plan and want a deeper, more demanding rhythm. M'Cheyne is the plan that Don Carson works with in his *For the Love of God* devotional, and it has been the structural backbone of Reformed devotional practice for nearly two centuries.

**What it adds:** Saturation. Reading four different parts of the Bible each day creates constant cross-textual connection. The Old Testament passages illuminate the New Testament passages and vice versa. You finish the year having read the New Testament and Psalms twice — which means by December, you are reading the New Testament with the entire Old Testament fresh in your mind.

**One honest note:** Four readings per day is roughly 25-30 minutes minimum, more if you read attentively. Do not start here if you have never finished a one-year plan. Build to it. The ESV Daily Reading Bible above is the simplest way to use M'Cheyne's plan in printed form — alternatively, free PDFs of the plan are widely available, and you can use it with any Bible you already own.

## How to Choose Your Plan

For most men, the order is straightforward:

**Year 1:** ESV One Year Bible or NIV One Year Bible. The four-stream format is the most likely to get you to December 31.

**Year 2:** One Year Chronological Bible or The Daily Bible. Now that you have read the canonical order once, reorder it. The chronological view will surface things you missed.

**Year 3+:** M'Cheyne Reading Plan. By now, you have read the Bible at least twice and you are ready for the discipline of four daily readings and the cross-textual saturation that produces.

Pick a plan, set a fixed time of day to read, and do not negotiate with yourself about it. The plan does not matter if you do not open it. Open it.
