The man who does not know church history believes that his version of Christianity is the original — or at least the most faithful one. He is almost always wrong. Two thousand years of the church's life sit between the New Testament and the present, and to be ignorant of that history is to be a child theologically.

These five books give you the history. Start with the accessible ones. Work toward the comprehensive ones. By the time you have read all five, you will understand the long argument the church has been having with itself about what it means to follow Christ.

---

## 1. Church History in Plain Language — Bruce Shelley

{% include book-card.html
  title="Church History in Plain Language"
  author="Bruce L. Shelley"
  asin="1418511617"
  description="The most readable comprehensive church history in print. Shelley covers two thousand years from the apostles to the present in clear, engaging prose. The standard introduction for evangelical readers."
  verdict="The best entry point to church history. Read this first — it will give you the framework everything else builds on."
%}

**What it covers:** Two thousand years of church history in a single accessible volume. Shelley walks through the early church, the rise of Christendom, medieval Christianity, the Reformation, the Enlightenment, the modern missions movement, and the global expansion of Christianity in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. Each chapter focuses on a few key figures and events rather than trying to cover everything.

**Who it's for:** Any Christian who has never read church history. Shelley is intentionally accessible — short chapters, clear prose, vivid storytelling — without being shallow. He covers the essential figures and movements without burying you in detail.

**What it adds:** Narrative. Most church history textbooks read like encyclopedias. Shelley reads like a story — because that is how he treats it. The men and women who shaped the church appear as actual people with actual lives, not as names on a chronological chart.

**One honest note:** Shelley writes from an evangelical perspective, which shows particularly in his treatment of the Reformation and the modern era. He is fair to other traditions but unambiguously rooted in his own. Catholic and Orthodox readers will want to supplement with González or MacCulloch for additional perspective.

## 2. Turning Points — Mark Noll

{% include book-card.html
  title="Turning Points: Decisive Moments in the History of Christianity"
  author="Mark A. Noll"
  asin="0801038472"
  description="Noll's accessible study of fourteen pivotal events in church history — from the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70 through the missionary movement of the modern era. The episodic structure makes the book usable as a teaching text or a personal study."
  verdict="The best supplement to a comprehensive church history. Read this after Shelley to deepen the key moments."
%}

**What it covers:** Fourteen chapters, each focused on a "turning point" in church history — a moment that decisively shaped the church's direction. Topics include the fall of Jerusalem, the Council of Nicaea, the conversion of Constantine, the Great Schism, the Reformation, the Wesleyan revival, the Edinburgh Missionary Conference, and the rise of global Christianity.

**Who it's for:** Men who have read a survey like Shelley and want to go deeper on specific pivotal events. Also excellent as a teaching text — each chapter stands alone and can be read in an evening.

**What it adds:** Focus and depth on the moments that mattered most. Most church histories cover everything in broad strokes. Noll spends a whole chapter on each turning point and develops the historical context, the key figures, and the long-term significance with care.

**One honest note:** The episodic structure means Noll does not cover the connecting tissue between his fourteen turning points. Read it as a supplement to a comprehensive narrative history, not a replacement.

## 3. The Story of Christianity — Justo González

{% include book-card.html
  title="The Story of Christianity: Volume 1, The Early Church to the Reformation"
  author="Justo L. González"
  asin="0061855884"
  description="The most accessible scholarly church history in two volumes. González is a Latin American Methodist with a global perspective — he treats the non-Western and non-Protestant streams of church history with seriousness that evangelical surveys often miss."
  verdict="The best two-volume comprehensive church history for serious readers. Volume 2 (ASIN 0061855898) covers the Reformation to the present."
%}

**What it covers:** Two volumes covering the full history of Christianity. Volume 1 takes the story from the apostolic era through the Reformation. Volume 2 (ASIN 0061855898) covers the Reformation, the modern era, and the global church through the twentieth century. González treats each major tradition — Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant, and the emerging non-Western churches — with care.

**Who it's for:** Men who want a comprehensive history that is more thorough than Shelley but more accessible than MacCulloch. González is the standard for many seminary courses for good reason.

**What it adds:** A global perspective. González was one of the first major church historians to take seriously the non-Western dimensions of Christian history — the Eastern churches, the African and Asian Christian traditions, the Latin American church. The result is a history that does not treat European Christianity as the whole story.

**One honest note:** González is a Methodist and writes from a broadly mainline Protestant perspective. Evangelicals will sometimes wish for more attention to revival traditions and confessional theology. The trade-off is a fuller global picture than most evangelical histories provide.

## 4. Christianity: The First Three Thousand Years — Diarmaid MacCulloch

{% include book-card.html
  title="Christianity: The First Three Thousand Years"
  author="Diarmaid MacCulloch"
  asin="0143118692"
  description="The monumental modern history of Christianity. MacCulloch begins a thousand years before Christ with the religious world that shaped Israel and the early church, then traces the faith across three millennia to the present. 1,200 pages of magisterial historical writing."
  verdict="The most ambitious church history of the modern era. Heavy, comprehensive, and the volume to consult when you want the fullest picture."
%}

**What it covers:** A comprehensive history of Christianity from its roots in Greco-Roman and Jewish antiquity through the present. MacCulloch begins with the religious and cultural world that produced Israel and Christianity, then traces the church's development across three millennia. The volume is unusually strong on the Eastern churches, the medieval period, and the early modern era.

**Who it's for:** Mature readers who want the fullest single-volume history available. MacCulloch is also a superb stylist — the prose is engaging across the whole 1,200 pages — which makes the length more manageable than the page count suggests.

**What it adds:** Scope and depth. No other single volume covers as much of Christian history with as much attention to non-Western and pre-modern developments. MacCulloch is particularly strong on the Eastern Orthodox traditions, on the African and Asian early churches, and on the medieval period that evangelicals often neglect.

**One honest note:** MacCulloch writes from a secular-historical perspective. He is sympathetic to Christianity but does not write as an insider. Evangelical readers will sometimes find his judgments on theological matters foreign or surprising. The historical scholarship is first-rate; the theological framework is broadly mainline-secular.

## 5. Introduction to the History of Christianity — Tim Dowley, ed.

{% include book-card.html
  title="Introduction to the History of Christianity"
  author="Tim Dowley, editor"
  asin="1506445942"
  description="A multi-author, heavily illustrated introduction to church history. Dowley assembled a team of scholars to write accessible chapters across the full span of Christian history. The illustrations, maps, and timelines make this the most visually rich church history in print."
  verdict="The best illustrated church history. Excellent for visual learners and for families teaching church history at home."
%}

**What it covers:** A comprehensive but accessible church history covering the apostolic era through the twenty-first century. Each section is written by a specialist scholar, and the whole is heavily illustrated with maps, photographs, timelines, and sidebars. The fourth edition has been thoroughly updated to include the global church and the most recent scholarship.

**Who it's for:** Visual learners, families teaching church history at home, and men who find traditional histories dry. The illustrations and visual aids significantly increase retention — and they make the book usable as a reference long after you have read it through.

**What it adds:** The visual dimension. Maps of the spread of Christianity, photographs of the major sites, timelines of the key events, portraits of the figures — the visual material is genuinely useful, not decorative. The multi-author approach also brings specialist expertise to each section.

**One honest note:** The multi-author format produces some unevenness in tone and depth across sections. The illustrations also make the book physically larger and heavier than a single-author work. Use it alongside Shelley or González for a more uniform narrative voice.

## How to Use This List

Church history is not a topic you cover. It is a tradition you join.

**Read first:** Shelley, *Church History in Plain Language*. Build the framework.

**Read second:** Noll, *Turning Points*. Deepen the pivotal moments.

**Read third:** González, *The Story of Christianity* (both volumes). Move from survey to comprehensive history.

**Read for depth:** MacCulloch, *Christianity: The First Three Thousand Years*. The magisterial single-volume.

**Use as reference:** Dowley, *Introduction to the History of Christianity*. The illustrated companion.

The church is older than your tradition, larger than your country, and more diverse than your assumptions. These books will show you that — and root you more deeply in the faith because of it.
