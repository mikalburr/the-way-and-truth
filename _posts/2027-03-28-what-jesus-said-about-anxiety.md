Jesus's most extended teaching on anxiety is Matthew 6:25-34. It is often reduced to "don't worry, be happy" — which is neither what he said nor what the Greek allows. The actual teaching is more pointed and more useful. It diagnoses anxiety as a divided posture toward provision, exposes its irrationality, and prescribes a specific reorientation.

This is not denial of reality. It is reorientation of trust.

## The Greek Verb — Merimnaō

The word translated "be anxious" or "worry" in Matthew 6:25-34 is *merimnaō*, which appears six times in this short passage. The root is the same as *merizō* — to divide. *Merimnaō* describes a divided mind, pulled in competing directions, attention fragmented across concerns.

This matters. Jesus is not addressing the emotion of feeling worried in a generic sense. He is addressing the cognitive and existential state of being torn between competing priorities — specifically, between the Kingdom and survival-management. The anxious person is a divided person, and division is the diagnostic.

The same verb appears in Paul's "be anxious about nothing" (Philippians 4:6) and in Martha's distraction (Luke 10:41 — "Martha, Martha, you are anxious and troubled about many things"). Each usage carries the divided-attention sense.

## The Context — Verse 24

Matthew 6:25 begins with "Therefore" (*dia touto*). The reasoning depends on what comes immediately before — verse 24: "No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve God and money."

The anxiety teaching is the practical follow-on to the mammon teaching. The person who has tried to serve both God and money will discover that the attempt produces anxiety. Divided lordship produces divided mind. *Merimnaō* is the psychological signature of a soul trying to operate under two masters.

This connects the anxiety passage to [what Jesus said about money](/true-teachings/2026/10/11/what-jesus-said-about-money/). The two teachings are one teaching, separated by a paragraph break that does not exist in the Greek.

## "Is Not Life More Than Food?" (v. 25)

"Therefore I tell you, do not be anxious about your life, what you will eat or what you will drink, nor about your body, what you will put on. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothing?"

Jesus's first move is to argue from greater to lesser. If the Father has given you life and body, will he not give the food and clothing necessary to sustain them? The gift of the larger thing makes provision of the smaller thing entailed.

This is not naïveté about the reality of need. People in Jesus's audience were poor. Some had probably gone hungry. He is not pretending need does not exist. He is challenging the assumption that meeting the need is the disciple's project rather than the Father's.

## "Look at the Birds" (v. 26)

"Look at the birds of the air: they neither sow nor reap nor gather into barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not of more value than they?"

The argument is from creation. The birds, lacking the agricultural infrastructure humans build to manage food, are nevertheless fed. The Father provides for them within the system he established.

Jesus is not saying not to sow and reap. He is pointing out that the system of provision is the Father's, even when human work participates in it. The work is real; the trust behind it is what matters.

"Are you not of more value than they?" — *ouch hymeis mallon diapherete autōn*. The verb *diapherō* means to be of more weight, to surpass in significance. The argument is from the lesser care given to birds to the greater care due to those made in God's image.

## "Which of You by Being Anxious Can Add a Single Hour?" (v. 27)

"And which of you by being anxious can add a single hour to his span of life?"

The Greek *hēlikia* can mean stature (height) or lifespan. The point is the same either way: anxiety produces nothing. It is functionally useless as a tool for accomplishing what it claims to be useful for.

This is the empirical argument. If anxiety actually worked — if it added time, height, or anything else — there would be at least a rational case for it. It does not. So even from a pragmatic standpoint, anxiety fails.

Modern psychology has confirmed this. Chronic anxiety damages cognition, immune function, sleep, and decision-making. It reliably subtracts capacity rather than adding it. Jesus's first-century observation holds.

## "Consider the Lilies" (vv. 28-30)

"Consider the lilies of the field, how they grow: they neither toil nor spin, yet I tell you, even Solomon in all his glory was not arrayed like one of these. But if God so clothes the grass of the field, which today is alive and tomorrow is thrown into the oven, will he not much more clothe you, O you of little faith?"

The argument extends from birds (food) to lilies (clothing). The lilies, which exist briefly and are then burned as fuel for ovens, are clothed by God with beauty surpassing Solomon's wealth. If God spends provision on temporary plants, he will spend more provision on his eternal children.

"O you of little faith" — *oligopistoi* — is Jesus's recurring rebuke to disciples who let circumstances override their trust (Matthew 8:26, 14:31, 16:8). The connection between anxiety and lack of faith is direct. *Merimnaō* is the symptom; *oligopistia* is the underlying condition.

## "The Gentiles Seek After All These Things" (vv. 31-32)

"Therefore do not be anxious, saying, 'What shall we eat?' or 'What shall we drink?' or 'What shall we wear?' For the Gentiles seek after all these things, and your heavenly Father knows that you need them all."

Jesus categorizes anxiety as a Gentile orientation. The Greek *ta ethnē* — the nations, the pagans — refers to people without the knowledge of the covenant God. Pagans operate under a worldview in which provision is uncertain and must be secured by personal effort or magical appeal. The covenant person operates under a different worldview: there is a Father who knows what is needed.

"Your heavenly Father knows" — *oiden* — is the knowledge of a parent who pays attention. The need is not hidden from him.

## "Seek First the Kingdom" (v. 33)

"But seek first the Kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you."

This is the prescriptive center. Anxiety is not removed by being told to relax. It is removed by reorientation. The verb *zēteite* — seek — is a present imperative: ongoing, sustained, active seeking.

"First" — *prōton* — is priority, not chronology. The Kingdom and his righteousness are the primary objects of pursuit. Other concerns are secondary.

The promise: "all these things will be added" — *prostethēsetai*. They will be added, supplied, included. Not as the reward for seeking the Kingdom, but as the by-product of correctly ordered priority.

This is the practical undoing of *merimnaō*. The divided mind is healed not by suppressing one concern but by giving the right priority to the right concern. When the Kingdom is first, the second-tier concerns find their proper place. They are still real; they are just no longer competing for the lordship of your attention.

## "Sufficient for the Day Is Its Own Trouble" (v. 34)

"Therefore do not be anxious about tomorrow, for tomorrow will be anxious for itself. Sufficient for the day is its own trouble."

The final verse adds a temporal dimension. Anxiety usually projects forward — it borrows trouble from a future that has not yet arrived. Jesus instructs his disciples to stay inside the day they are in.

The Greek *arketon tē hēmera hē kakia autēs* is almost proverbial: each day has enough of its own evil. Stay there. Address what is in front of you. Trust the Father with what is not yet.

This is not denial of planning. It is denial of the spiritual practice of pre-suffering imagined futures. The future is the Father's. The present is the disciple's.

## What the Teaching Refuses to Be

Matthew 6:25-34 is not:
- A claim that disciples will never feel anxious. (They will. Jesus does not claim otherwise.)
- A prohibition on planning, working, or preparing.
- A guarantee that need will never be felt.
- A spiritual bypass that ignores the reality of trauma or mental illness.

What it is: a reorientation of trust. Anxiety is the divided mind's signature. The reorientation is toward the Father who is undivided in his care. The seeking is first of the Kingdom, which restructures every other concern.

The result is not the absence of difficulty. It is the presence of trust inside difficulty. (Compare [the high priestly prayer](/true-teachings/2026/09/13/high-priestly-prayer-john-17/) for the relational structure that makes this trust possible.)
