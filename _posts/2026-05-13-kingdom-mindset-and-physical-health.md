There is a persistent false divide in much of Christian culture between the spiritual and the physical — as if the body is a temporary container for the soul that does not particularly matter in Kingdom terms. That divide is not biblical. It is Gnostic. And it has produced generations of Christian men who take their discipleship seriously and their physical stewardship not at all.

The Kingdom framework eliminates this divide. Your body is not a spiritual inconvenience. It is a domain of stewardship that the King has explicitly addressed.

## The Body as Temple

"Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore glorify God with your body." (1 Corinthians 6:19-20, BSB)

This is not a metaphor about spiritual cleanliness. It is a statement about the physical body as the dwelling place of the Holy Spirit. The Greek word for temple here is *naos* — the inner sanctuary, the holy of holies, not just the outer courts. Paul is not saying your body is a building in which something spiritual happens. He is saying the physical structure of your body is the specific location where the Spirit of the living God resides.

That changes everything about how you are obligated to treat it. You are not the owner. You were bought — the language of liberation from slavery, where the price was paid by someone else. The body belongs to the King. The obligation of stewardship follows from the fact of ownership.

This is not health-gospel prosperity theology. It is stewardship theology, which is a completely different category. The health gospel says: take care of your body and God will reward you with health and long life. Stewardship theology says: your body belongs to the King, and you are responsible for how you manage what has been entrusted to you. The motivation is not personal benefit. It is faithfulness to the owner.

## The Discipline of the Body as Kingdom Stewardship

Paul makes the point with additional specificity in 1 Corinthians 9:

"No, I strike a blow to my body and make it my slave so that after I have preached to others, I myself will not be disqualified for the prize." (1 Corinthians 9:27, BSB)

The Greek verb translated "strike a blow" is *hypopiazo* — literally, to strike under the eye, to give a black eye to. Paul is not being delicate. He is describing the deliberate, forceful discipline of the body as a necessary part of his Kingdom mission. Not because the body is evil, but because the body's default inclinations — toward ease, comfort, immediate gratification — work against Kingdom purposes if left ungoverned.

Every serious man who has trained athletically understands this language. The body's natural tendency is not toward maximum capacity. It is toward equilibrium at minimum effort. Training is the deliberate imposition of productive difficulty on that tendency in order to develop capacity the body would not develop on its own. Paul is applying that same framework to the spiritual life. The undisciplined body produces an undisciplined man. An undisciplined man produces undisciplined Kingdom citizenship.

This is not asceticism — the belief that the body is evil and must be punished. Paul is not punishing his body. He is governing it. The distinction matters. You do not govern your body because it is bad. You govern it because it is yours to steward.

## The Dominion Mandate and Physical Stewardship

The Kingdom framework for physical stewardship begins before 1 Corinthians, before the New Testament, at the very origin of human purpose:

"God blessed them and said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply; fill the earth and subdue it. Rule over the fish of the sea and the birds of the air and over every living creature that moves on the ground.'" (Genesis 1:28, BSB)

The dominion mandate — the original commission to rule and steward God's creation — begins with your own body before it extends to anything else. A man who cannot govern himself cannot govern a domain. A man who has no discipline over his physical habits — sleep, nutrition, training, rest — has conceded the first territory. Everything the dominion mandate calls him to steward externally begins with the internal domain of self-governance.

This is not a performance standard. You are not disqualified from Kingdom citizenship by being out of shape. But the honest application of Kingdom mindset to the physical domain produces a man who takes his body seriously as the first and most immediate domain of stewardship he has been given.

## What a Kingdom Approach to the Body Looks Like

This is where the rubber meets the road, and where this site's voice matters. A Kingdom approach to physical stewardship is not the same as the fitness-culture obsession with aesthetics, or the health-gospel obsession with longevity as evidence of God's favor. It is stewardship — managing the King's property well.

**Training.** A Kingdom man trains because his body is a tool of Kingdom purpose, and a well-maintained tool serves better than a neglected one. This does not mean elite athlete standards for every man. It means asking: am I maintaining adequate physical capacity for the purposes the King has for me? Strength, endurance, mobility — the basic physical capacities of a man who is expected to work, serve, lead, and remain effective into later decades.

As a lifelong athlete, I can tell you that physical training has always been a spiritual discipline for me as much as a physical one. The man who trains seriously is practicing, in a physical domain, the same disciplines Kingdom mindset requires in the spiritual domain: showing up when you do not feel like it, pushing through discomfort with purpose, maintaining the long-term view when the short-term is unpleasant. These are not separate skills. They are the same discipline in different domains.

**Nutrition.** The temple metaphor applies here with specificity. What you consistently put in your body is a stewardship decision. Not a spiritual performance metric — a management decision about the King's property. The question is not what diet system produces the best aesthetics. It is: am I managing this body well enough for the purposes it needs to serve?

**Rest.** This is the domain most disciplined men fail in. The same drive that produces good training habits, good work habits, and good Kingdom habits also produces the temptation to maximize output by minimizing rest. But rest is not a compromise with laziness — it is a design specification. "He gives rest to His beloved" (Psalm 127:2, BSB). The man who refuses adequate sleep is not more Kingdom-oriented for his sacrifice. He is managing the King's property badly.

**Avoiding what degrades the temple.** This is straightforward. Substance abuse, sexual immorality, and lifestyle patterns that systematically damage the body are addressed specifically in the temple passage (1 Corinthians 6). The context of Paul's temple statement is a section on sexual immorality and the body — he is making the argument that what you do with your body matters because the body belongs to God. Every behavior that degrades the body is, in Kingdom terms, damaging something that does not belong to you.

## The Integration

Kingdom mindset is not only interior. It is not only about what you believe about your identity and how you understand the King's reign. It is the integration of all domains of your life under Kingdom governance — including the domain you inhabit most immediately and constantly: your physical body.

The man with a genuinely Kingdom-oriented mindset about his body is not obsessed with it, is not performing for an audience, and is not chasing the self-improvement metric of a better physique. He is a steward. He manages what he has been given — with discipline, with wisdom, with the long view — on behalf of an owner who will evaluate the stewardship.

That framing changes how training feels, how nutrition decisions are made, and how rest is understood. Less performance. More faithfulness. Less about what you can achieve. More about what you have been entrusted with.

That is Kingdom physical stewardship. It is different from every other framework for thinking about the body. And it is the one that actually holds together across a lifetime.
