"Enter by the narrow gate. For the gate is wide and the way is easy that leads to destruction, and those who enter by it are many. For the gate is narrow and the way is hard that leads to life, and those who find it are few." — Matthew 7:13–14

These two verses appear near the end of the Sermon on the Mount, after the Beatitudes, after the intensification of the law, after the Lord's Prayer, after the teaching on anxiety and provision. They function as a summary warning — and they are among the most uncomfortable words Jesus ever spoke.

He is not saying that salvation is difficult to attain. He is saying that the way that leads to life is the way that few people take, and that the way most people take leads somewhere else entirely. Read straight, without softening, this has implications that most preaching never seriously addresses.

## Two Gates, Two Roads, Two Destinations

The metaphor is precise. There are not many roads and many destinations. There are two roads and two destinations. The wide gate leads to a broad road that leads to destruction. The narrow gate leads to a hard way that leads to life.

"Easy" in verse 13 translates the Greek *eurykhōros* — spacious, roomy, with plenty of room to maneuver. The broad road accommodates a wide range of choices and lifestyles. You can bring your assumptions, your habits, your preferences. There's space for all of it.

The narrow gate, by contrast, requires leaving things behind. Gates that are narrow don't let you bring everything through. You cannot enter carrying what won't fit. This is not incidental to Jesus's point — it's the point.

And the paths diverge at the gate. The choice of which gate to enter determines the road you travel, and the road determines the destination.

## "Those Who Find It Are Few"

The word "find" in verse 14 is significant. You don't stumble onto the narrow path. You find it — which implies searching, intention, effort. The broad road is the default. You end up on it without deciding to be there. The narrow path requires being sought.

"Those who find it are few" is not a triumphalist statement about who's in and who's out. It's a sober assessment of probability. Most people, left to the natural pull of ease and accommodation, will not take the harder way. Jesus is not saying this to make his followers feel superior. He's saying it as a warning — including to the people who are already following him.

The Sermon on the Mount is addressed to disciples (5:1), not to the unbelieving masses. The warning about the broad road is directed at people who already think they are on the right path. It's a calibration device: don't assume that because you have made some religious choices, you are on the narrow way. Test it.

## The False Prophets Connection

The verses immediately following — Matthew 7:15–20 — introduce false prophets "who come to you in sheep's clothing but inwardly are ravenous wolves." The connection to the narrow path teaching is not incidental.

False prophets are, in this context, people who make the broad road look like the narrow one. They dress the wide gate to look like the narrow gate. They teach a version of the faith that is accommodating, that doesn't require leaving things behind, that offers life without the hardness of the way. And people follow them, because the broad road's primary appeal is that it's easy.

Jesus's test for false prophets is fruit (7:16–20). Not doctrine, not eloquence, not the size of the following. Fruit. The life the teaching produces. A tree is known by what it produces, and a teacher's truth is ultimately known by what the teaching does to people over time.

This is a more demanding standard than most religious institutions apply. Doctrinal correctness is assessable in the short term. Fruit takes years to evaluate.

## "Not Everyone Who Says to Me, 'Lord, Lord'"

Matthew 7:21–23 is the most sobering passage in the Sermon on the Mount, and it belongs with the narrow path teaching. "Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the Kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven."

Jesus describes people who will claim, at the final judgment, to have prophesied in his name, cast out demons, and performed "many mighty works." He responds: "I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness."

These are not people who rejected Jesus. They actively used his name. They did religious activities under his banner. And Jesus says he never knew them.

The criterion is not religious activity. It's not even impressive religious activity. It's knowing Jesus — and being known by him — in a way that produces genuine alignment with the Father's will. The narrow path is the path of that relationship. The broad road can accommodate religious performance indefinitely while bypassing the relationship entirely.

## What "Hard" Means

The narrow way is described as "hard" (*thlibō* in some manuscripts — pressed, constricted, difficult). This is not suffering for its own sake. Jesus is not teaching that difficulty is inherently virtuous. The hardness of the narrow way comes from specific sources.

It requires leaving things behind at the gate. Assumptions about self-sufficiency, about deserving, about what you're owed. Habits that are incompatible with Kingdom values. Relationships that pull toward the broad road.

It runs against the current. The broad road is easy in part because it follows the direction most people are going. The narrow way involves consistent friction with the surrounding culture — including, often, the surrounding religious culture.

It demands sustained attention. You don't stay on the narrow path by accident. "Those who find it" — the present tense of finding — suggests ongoing seeking, not a one-time discovery.

## The Honest Confrontation

This teaching from Jesus does not permit easy comfort. It cannot be read as "everyone who sincerely tries will be fine." It cannot be flattened into "just believe and you're on the narrow path." It places a question in front of every reader: which road are you actually on?

Not which road do you believe yourself to be on. Not which road does your religious community affirm you're on. Which road does the evidence of your life — your choices, your priorities, your fruit — suggest you're actually traveling?

The narrow gate is open. The path is findable. Few find it, but "few" is not "none."
