Most Christians treat Genesis 1:26-28 as the opening of a creation story. It is that. But it is also something else: a commissioning document. The first job description ever issued. And almost nobody reads it that way.

If you want to understand Kingdom mindset, you have to start before Mark 1:15. You have to start in Genesis 1, where humanity is given a specific function with specific verbs — and where the entire Bible's account of authority, identity, and purpose is set in motion.

## What the Text Actually Says

> "Then God said, 'Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth.' So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them. And God blessed them. And God said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over every living thing that moves on the earth.'" — Genesis 1:26-28

Two Hebrew verbs are doing the heavy lifting: *radah* (have dominion) and *kabash* (subdue). These are not soft words.

*Radah* means to rule, to have authority over. It is the same verb used later for kings ruling nations. When God uses it of humanity, he is describing a vice-regency — humans ruling on his behalf over the territory he has placed them in.

*Kabash* is stronger still. It means to bring into submission, to tread down, to take raw potential and force it into productive order. It is what a farmer does to soil. What a craftsman does to wood. What a leader does to chaos.

This is not gentle. It is not passive. It is active, intentional, ordered shaping of the created world.

## Image-Bearing as Function, Not Just Essence

The phrase "image of God" — *tselem Elohim* — has been theologized into abstraction. Most people assume it means humans have a soul, or rationality, or moral conscience. Those things may be true. But that is not what the phrase meant in its ancient Near Eastern context.

In the ancient world, a king who conquered new territory would erect an *image* of himself in that territory. The image represented his rule. It was the visible reminder that this land belonged to him and was governed by his authority — even when he himself was not physically present.

When God creates humanity in his image and places them on the earth, he is using that exact vocabulary. Humanity is the visible representation of the invisible King. The earth belongs to God. Humans are placed on it as his image — the proof and instrument of his rule.

This is not flattering self-talk. It is a job assignment.

## Vice-Regents, Not Owners

A vice-regent rules on behalf of a sovereign. He does not own the territory. He represents the one who does. His authority is real, but it is derivative. He can be removed. He can be held accountable. And his decisions are supposed to reflect the will of the one who appointed him — not his own preferences.

This is the structure of human authority in Genesis 1. Humanity is given real, substantial dominion over the earth. But the dominion is not autonomous. It is exercised under God, on behalf of God, for purposes God has defined.

Most modern people, including most Christians, have absorbed one of two distorted views of this. Either humans are essentially insignificant accidents who should defer to nature, or humans are autonomous masters who can do whatever they want with the planet. The biblical view is neither. Humanity is significant — staggeringly so, given the dignity of the assignment — but accountable.

This is why a [Kingdom mindset](/kingdom-mindset/2026/05/12/what-is-kingdom-mindset/) is foundational. The Kingdom is the domain where God's authority is operative. The dominion mandate is the original instance of that authority being delegated. When Jesus announces the Kingdom is at hand, he is announcing the restoration of what was forfeited in Genesis 3.

## What Was Lost in the Fall

The fall did not strip humanity of the image of God. Genesis 9:6 explicitly affirms the image persists after the fall. But the exercise of dominion was corrupted. The vice-regency was broken — not formally revoked, but functionally compromised.

Ground that was supposed to yield to cultivation now resists. Relationships that were supposed to be partnership now contain power struggle. The natural creation, which was the territory of human stewardship, became a site of futility (Romans 8:20).

The dominion mandate did not disappear. It became something humanity could no longer carry out cleanly. The image-bearer was still on the throne, but the throne had cracked.

## Christ as the True Image

This is why the New Testament keeps calling Jesus "the image of the invisible God" (Colossians 1:15) and "the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature" (Hebrews 1:3). He is what Adam was supposed to be. He is the true vice-regent — the human who actually rules on the Father's behalf with perfect alignment.

Hebrews 2:5-9 makes the connection explicit. The author quotes Psalm 8 — which itself quotes Genesis 1 — about humanity being crowned with glory and honor and given dominion. He then says, "At present, we do not yet see everything in subjection to him. But we see him who for a little while was made lower than the angels, namely Jesus, crowned with glory and honor."

Christ is the restoration of the dominion mandate. And those who are in Christ are restored to the original assignment, with the original Spirit, under the original King.

## Implications for Kingdom Living

This is not historical curiosity. The dominion mandate frames the entire question of why human work and creativity matter, why your domain of responsibility is sacred, and why "spiritual" life cannot be separated from physical stewardship.

You are not on this planet as a tourist waiting for evacuation to heaven. You are on this planet as a vice-regent in training, learning to exercise delegated authority well in preparation for greater authority later. Jesus's own teaching on faithfulness — "you have been faithful over a little, I will set you over much" (Matthew 25:21) — assumes exactly this structure.

The work you have been given to do — the people you have been given to lead, the territory you have been given to steward — is not incidental to your spiritual life. It is your spiritual life. The dominion mandate is the original frame, and the [renewing of the mind](/kingdom-mindset/2026/05/12/renewing-your-mind-romans-12/) is the process by which you actually start carrying it out again.

Start with the assignment. Then live like it is real.
