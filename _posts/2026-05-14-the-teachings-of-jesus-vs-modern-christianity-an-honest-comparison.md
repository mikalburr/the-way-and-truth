Let's be honest about the gap.

Modern Western Christianity — particularly the evangelical and charismatic streams that dominate American religious life — has a set of core concerns that are not hard to identify. Get people into heaven. Help them behave better. Keep them attending and giving. Build the church. Manage moral failure with grace. Repeat.

Jesus had a different set of core concerns. He preached the Kingdom of God as a present reality, demanded radical love of enemies, called for generosity that exceeded what was comfortable, insisted on inner transformation as the precondition of authentic action, and repeatedly warned that religious performance was the most dangerous counterfeit to genuine Kingdom citizenship.

The gap between these two sets of concerns is not a minor theological nuance. It is a fundamental divergence in what the project is about.

## What Modern Christianity Often Centers On

### Getting to Heaven

The dominant frame in most evangelical preaching is eschatological rescue: Jesus died so you can go to heaven when you die. This is presented as the core of the gospel. Salvation is primarily about destination — the afterlife rather than the present.

This is not false. The New Testament does speak of eternal life, resurrection, and a future with God. But it is radically incomplete as a summary of what Jesus taught.

Jesus spoke of eternal life (*zoe aionios*) most often in the present tense. John 5:24: *"Very truly I tell you, whoever hears my word and believes him who sent me has eternal life and will not be judged but has crossed over from death to life."* Present tense: *has* eternal life. *Has* crossed over.

John 17:3 defines it: *"Now this is eternal life: that they know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom you have sent."*

Eternal life is a quality of relationship with God that begins now. It is not primarily a post-mortem destination. Reducing it to that shrinks the gospel to a rescue package and misses the Kingdom invitation entirely.

### Moral Behavior

Modern Christianity often functions as a morality improvement project. Don't drink too much. Don't commit adultery. Be honest. Give to charity. Attend church. Vote the right way. The goal is a moral life that God approves and that distinguishes the believer from the secular culture.

Jesus was not uninterested in behavior. But his diagnosis of the problem ran much deeper than behavior modification can reach. In Matthew 5:21-22, he escalated the command against murder: *"You have heard that it was said to the people long ago, 'You shall not murder, and anyone who murders will be subject to judgment.' But I tell you that anyone who is angry with a brother or sister will be subject to judgment."*

The problem is not the behavior (murder). The problem is the interior condition (anger, contempt) from which the behavior flows. Jesus went on to do the same with adultery (the lust of the heart), oath-taking (the lack of integrity in the heart), and retaliation (the justice-seeking instinct of the heart).

His target is the operating system, not the output. Moral Christianity that addresses outputs without the operating system is exactly the kind of religion Jesus repeatedly confronted in the Pharisees — *"You are like whitewashed tombs, which look beautiful on the outside but on the inside are full of the bones of the dead and everything unclean"* (Matthew 23:27).

### Church Attendance as Discipleship

The modern church has largely conflated discipleship with church attendance. Come to services, join a small group, volunteer, give — this is discipleship. The more active you are in church programs, the more you are assumed to be growing as a follower of Jesus.

Jesus never said this. His model of discipleship was direct personal engagement: *"Come, follow me"* (Mark 1:17). He then led his disciples through a three-year intensive — teaching, correcting, sending them out, debriefing when they returned, modeling prayer, confronting their failures in real time.

The result was not people who attended his gatherings. It was people who had been so thoroughly reshaped by proximity to him that they turned the Roman world upside down (Acts 17:6).

Church attendance is not discipleship. It is one possible context within which discipleship might occur. The difference matters enormously.

## What Jesus Centered On

### The Present Kingdom

Mark 1:14-15 is the summary statement of Jesus's ministry: *"The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news."*

The Greek *eggiken* — translated "has come near" — carries the force of something that has arrived, not something that is approaching. The Kingdom is not a future state to be entered after death. It is a present reality to be entered through *metanoia* (complete reorientation) and *pistis* (trust and allegiance).

Everything Jesus taught was Kingdom teaching. The Beatitudes describe Kingdom citizens. The parables describe Kingdom dynamics. The healings are demonstrations of Kingdom power breaking into human reality. The resurrection is the vindication of the King and the guarantor that the Kingdom is ultimate reality, not the kingdoms of this world.

### Love of Enemies

Matthew 5:44 is the sharpest edge of Jesus's teaching: *"But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you."*

No ancient ethical system taught this. The Greek Stoics taught indifference to enemies. The Hebrew Bible commanded justice. The Greco-Roman honor system demanded retaliation to preserve dignity. Jesus commanded love — not sentiment, but active goodwill and intercession.

Modern Christianity often softens this into "don't hate people" or "pray for them to change." Jesus's command is more demanding: love them as they are, pray for them specifically, and do this so that you *"may be children of your Father in heaven"* (Matthew 5:45). Kingdom citizens behave like the King, who makes his sun rise on the evil and the good.

### Radical Generosity

Luke 12:33: *"Sell your possessions and give to the poor. Provide purses for yourselves that will not wear out, a treasure in heaven that will never fail."*

This is not a counsel for monks and missionaries. Jesus said it to his disciples. The context is the parable of the rich fool (Luke 12:16-21) — a man who accumulated for himself and was not rich toward God.

Jesus's economics are Kingdom economics: the resources of the Kingdom are infinite, therefore hoarding is irrational; generosity is not sacrifice, it is investment in the only economy that cannot collapse.

Modern Christianity is broadly comfortable with wealth accumulation, charitable giving that is proportional and convenient, and a theology of blessing that reassures the affluent that their prosperity is God's approval. Jesus's actual teaching on wealth is one of the most consistently uncomfortable bodies of teaching in the Gospels — and one of the most consistently softened.

### Inner Transformation

Matthew 5:8: *"Blessed are the pure in heart, for they will see God."*

The Greek word translated "pure" is *katharos* — clean, unmixed, without contamination. Not pure behavior. Pure heart. The inner source from which behavior flows.

Jesus's entire project is the transformation of the heart — the operating system of human action. Matthew 6:22-23: *"The eye is the lamp of the body. If your eyes are healthy, your whole body will be full of light. But if your eyes are unhealthy, your whole body will be full of darkness."*

The "eye" here is the intention, the orientation, the inner posture. Kingdom transformation is not behavioral modification; it is the renovation of the heart's fundamental orientation from self-centered autonomy to Kingdom-centered allegiance.

## The Most Uncomfortable Passage

Matthew 7:21-23 sits at the end of the Sermon on the Mount like a warning siren:

*"Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but only the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven. Many will say to me on that day, 'Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name and in your name drive out demons and in your name perform many miracles?' Then I will tell them plainly, 'I never knew you. Away from me, you evildoers!'"*

These are people who are religiously active, charismatically gifted, and calling Jesus "Lord." They are not atheists. They are not people who rejected Christianity. They are people who mistook religious activity for Kingdom citizenship.

Jesus says: *I never knew you.* The word is *ginosko* — intimate, relational knowledge. Not acquaintance. Not affiliation. Knowing.

This passage should arrest every serious reader. It is not a warning to sinners outside the church. It is a warning to the religiously active inside it. The gap between religious activity and genuine Kingdom citizenship is real, and Jesus is explicit that many people will not discover it until it is too late.

## The Honest Conclusion

The gap between the teachings of Jesus and the practices of modern Christianity is not a gap of malice. It is a gap of drift — two thousand years of cultural accommodation, theological systematization, institutional self-interest, and the natural tendency of institutions to prioritize their own survival.

But acknowledging the drift is not anti-Christian. It is the most Christian thing a believer can do — to go back to the source, to let Jesus speak for himself, and to let his words interrogate everything that has been layered over them.

The question is not whether you attend church or identify as Christian. The question Jesus asks is whether you know him and he knows you — and whether what you call discipleship actually looks like what he described.

That is a harder question, and it is the right one.
