The parable of the talents is usually flattened into a self-help slogan: "use your gifts." That misses the actual structure. Jesus is teaching a Kingdom-economic principle about entrusted resources, risk, and the fear-vs-faith distinction. The third servant is not condemned for losing the money. He is condemned for refusing to risk it.

This is one of two parables Jesus tells in Matthew 25 about his return and the accounting that follows. The other is the wise and foolish virgins (25:1-13). Both turn on the question of what was done in the interval. Both end with a separation.

## The Setup (vv. 14-15)

"For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away."

A *talanton* was originally a unit of weight (around 75 pounds), used for measuring precious metals. By the first century, a talent referred to a sum of money — roughly 6,000 denarii, or twenty years of a day-laborer's wages. A single talent was a massive amount. Five talents was a fortune.

The master distributes "according to his ability" (*kata tēn idian dynamin*). The amount given is calibrated to capacity. This matters: the master is not being arbitrary. He knows his servants. The five-talent servant gets five because he can handle five, not because he is favored.

Then the master leaves. The servants are not given instructions about what to do with the money. The expectation is implied by the gift itself.

## The First Two Servants (vv. 16-17)

"He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more."

The Greek *ērgasato en autois* — "worked with them" or "traded with them" — implies active investment. They did not preserve the talents. They put them in motion. And they doubled them.

The text gives no detail about *how* they doubled the talents. Trade, lending, business ventures — the parable does not specify. What it specifies is that they took risk. Doubling money in the first century, as in any century, required risk of loss. They accepted that risk.

The "went at once" (*eutheōs*) for the five-talent servant is also notable. He did not deliberate for years. He moved.

## The Third Servant (v. 18)

"But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his master's money."

The verb *ōryxen* — dug — describes the standard first-century practice for hiding valuables in a politically unstable environment. Burial was the safe option. The servant did not lose the money. He did not embezzle it. He preserved it.

Notice the structure: the third servant gives the talent the most exhaustive protection. Burial in the earth. No risk of loss to robbery, no risk of loss to bad investment, no risk of loss at all. By the standard of pure preservation, this servant did the best job.

By the master's standard, he did the worst.

## The Reckoning (vv. 19-23)

"Now after a long time the master of those servants came and settled accounts with them."

The Greek *meta polyn chronon* — "after a long time" — is the same theme that runs through Matthew 24-25. The master's return is delayed. The interval matters.

The first two servants give the same report and receive the same commendation:

"Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master." (25:21, 23)

Three things to notice:

1. **The commendation is identical.** The five-talent servant and the two-talent servant receive the same words. The proportion of what was produced (doubled) was identical; the absolute amounts differed. The reward is keyed to faithfulness, not to scale.

2. **"Faithful over a little."** Even five talents — millions of dollars — is called "a little" by the master. The Kingdom scale dwarfs the entrusted scale. What is given now is small compared to what is being prepared.

3. **"Enter into the joy of your master."** The reward is not just additional responsibility. It is fellowship with the master himself. The relational dimension is the apex.

## The Third Servant's Defense (vv. 24-25)

"Master, I knew you to be a hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you have what is yours."

The servant's theology of the master is the problem. He calls the master *sklēros* — hard, harsh — and characterizes him as exploitative. Out of fear, he buried the talent.

This is the diagnostic moment. The third servant's failure is upstream of the buried talent. The failure is the picture of the master that motivated the burial. He believed the master was unjust and would punish failure. So he protected himself by ensuring there was no movement to evaluate.

The principle is sharper than usually preached: how you see the master determines what you do with what he gives you. A servant who believes the master is generous and trusting takes risk. A servant who believes the master is harsh buries the gift.

## The Master's Response (vv. 26-28)

"You wicked and slothful servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I should have received what was my own with interest."

The master accepts the servant's framing argumentatively without conceding it. *Even if* the master were as the servant describes, the burial response would still be inadequate. At minimum, the servant could have placed the money with bankers and earned interest.

The master is exposing the laziness behind the fear-language. *Sklēros* was not actually the issue. *Oknēros* — sluggish, lazy — is the issue. Fear was the cover story for refusal to act.

The talent is taken from him and given to the ten-talent servant.

## "To Everyone Who Has, More Will Be Given" (v. 29)

"For to everyone who has will more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who has not, even what he has will be taken away."

This sounds harsh, but the principle is consistent across Jesus's teaching (Matthew 13:12, Mark 4:25, Luke 8:18, Luke 19:26). The principle: faithful use of what is entrusted leads to more entrusted; refusal to use what is entrusted leads to losing even that.

It is not about meritocracy. It is about capacity. A person who refuses to develop the capacity they have cannot be given more without further atrophy.

## The Outer Darkness (v. 30)

"And cast the worthless servant into the outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth."

This is one of the three uses of *to skotos to exōteron* — outer darkness — in Matthew (see [the post on what Jesus said about hell](/true-teachings/2027/01/03/what-jesus-said-about-hell/)). The fate is exclusion from the master's joy — the inversion of the commendation given to the faithful servants.

The third servant ends up where he is because of what he refused to do with what he was given.

## What the Parable Teaches

1. **The master gives generously and calibrates to capacity.** Nobody gets nothing. Even the one-talent servant has a fortune to deploy.
2. **The expectation is deployment, not preservation.** Burial of resources for safety is not faithfulness. It is fear masquerading as caution.
3. **Faithfulness is measured by proportion, not by scale.** The two-talent servant and the five-talent servant receive identical praise.
4. **Theology of the master determines stewardship.** Seeing God as harsh produces paralysis; seeing him as generous produces risk-taking deployment.
5. **The accounting is real and it comes.** "After a long time" — but it comes.

The parable connects directly to Jesus's wider teaching on money (see [what Jesus said about money](/true-teachings/2026/10/11/what-jesus-said-about-money/)) and to the cost of discipleship (see [take up your cross](/true-teachings/2026/12/06/take-up-your-cross-meaning/)). The Kingdom is not entered by burying what was given for the sake of preservation. It is entered by deployment — putting what has been entrusted to work, even at risk of loss.

The buried life is the lost life.
